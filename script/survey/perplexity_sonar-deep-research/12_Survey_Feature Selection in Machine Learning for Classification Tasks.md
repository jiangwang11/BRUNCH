# Feature Selection in Machine Learning for Classification Tasks: A Comprehensive Review of Advances from 2022-2025

This comprehensive review synthesizes recent advances in feature selection methods for classification tasks spanning 2022 through 2025, examining three major methodological paradigms: filter-based approaches that rank features independently of classifiers, wrapper and embedded methods that integrate feature selection with model training, and emerging deep learning-based techniques that leverage neural network architectures for feature discovery. The analysis encompasses traditional statistical methods, novel neural network-based approaches including concrete autoencoders and attention mechanisms, and information-theoretic frameworks grounded in mutual information and Shapley values. Key findings indicate a convergence toward hybrid methodologies combining multiple selection criteria, enhanced stability metrics addressing reproducibility concerns, and increased attention to fairness and causal considerations in high-dimensional classification settings. Recent benchmarking studies reveal that no single method dominates across diverse problem scenarios, with performance highly dependent on data characteristics, dimensionality, and downstream model architecture. This review identifies critical challenges including computational complexity in extreme high-dimensional settings, feature interaction detection, and the tension between statistical efficiency and computational cost, while highlighting promising research directions such as quantum-enhanced feature selection, fairness-aware selection frameworks, and integration with large language models for feature engineering.

## Introduction

### Motivation and Significance of Feature Selection

Feature selection remains among the most critical preprocessing steps in machine learning pipelines for classification tasks, despite decades of research development[1]. The fundamental challenge arises from the curse of dimensionality, where high-dimensional feature spaces simultaneously increase computational burden, model complexity, and risk of overfitting while reducing the statistical power needed for reliable pattern discovery[1]. Beyond these technical considerations, feature selection serves critical interpretability and explainability objectives, enabling practitioners and domain experts to understand which variables drive model predictions—a requirement increasingly important in regulated domains such as healthcare, finance, and criminal justice[36][43].

In recent years, the explosion of high-dimensional biological data, particularly genomic and expression datasets with tens of thousands of features but limited samples, has intensified the practical urgency of effective feature selection methods[1][40]. Simultaneously, the rise of deep learning has created new opportunities and challenges for feature selection, as neural networks can learn complex nonlinear feature interactions that traditional statistical methods fail to capture[6][7][8]. The intersection of these trends has catalyzed a wave of innovation combining classical statistical principles with modern machine learning architectures, yielding hybrid methods that capitalize on strengths of multiple paradigms.

### Scope and Organization

This review systematically examines feature selection literature from 2022 through 2025, focusing exclusively on methods applied to supervised classification tasks. The analysis organizes the field into six major categories reflecting both methodological lineage and architectural choices: traditional filter methods emphasizing computational efficiency, wrapper and embedded approaches integrating selection with learning, deep learning-based selection through neural network architectures, information-theoretic frameworks, causal and fairness-aware selection methods, and emerging techniques including quantum approaches. For each category, we synthesize representative works from top-tier venues including NeurIPS, ICLR, Nature, JMLR, and leading arXiv contributions, critically evaluating methodological contributions against standardized evaluation criteria including selection accuracy, stability, computational efficiency, and generalization performance on downstream tasks.

## Major Feature Selection Methodologies and Representative Works

### Filter Methods and Classical Statistical Approaches

Filter methods represent the classical foundation of feature selection, operating independently of downstream classifiers by ranking features according to their statistical relationship with target variables[1]. These methods prioritize computational efficiency, making them essential for initial exploratory analysis in high-dimensional settings. A foundational 2022 Frontiers review systematically categorizes filter approaches into univariate methods (variance filtering, correlation analysis, mutual information-based ranking) and multivariate techniques addressing feature redundancy[1]. This work demonstrates that simple variance-based filters often achieve competitive performance against more sophisticated methods while maintaining superior scalability to extremely high-dimensional genomic datasets. The review particularly emphasizes advanced multivariate filters including minimal-redundancy-maximal-relevance (mRMR) and fast correlation-based filter (FCBF), which detect and eliminate redundant features while preserving information content[1].

A 2023 benchmark study by Hofner and colleagues comparing fourteen filter methods across eleven high-dimensional gene expression survival datasets identified striking empirical patterns contradicting conventional wisdom[40]. Surprisingly, the simple variance filter outperformed substantially more complex methods across multiple performance metrics including predictive accuracy, feature selection stability, and computational efficiency. The authors developed three similarity clusters among filter methods based on their ranking behavior, discovering that correlation-adjusted regression score (CARSS) filters offered a more sophisticated alternative achieving comparable predictive performance while incorporating outcome information overlooked by purely statistical filters[40]. These findings underscore a critical principle: computational and methodological sophistication does not guarantee practical superiority, and careful empirical validation remains essential before adopting complex methods.

Filter methods face inherent limitations rooted in their classifier-independence. Since they cannot adapt to specific downstream algorithms' requirements, selected feature sets often exhibit suboptimal performance for particular classifiers while potentially including features irrelevant to any specific model. Additionally, filter methods traditionally struggle with detecting feature interactions and nonlinear dependencies, particularly in datasets where predictive power emerges from complex combinations of individual features[1][40].

### Wrapper Methods and Embedded Feature Selection in Neural Networks

Wrapper methods address filter limitations by evaluating feature subsets according to downstream classifier performance, enabling task-specific optimization at the cost of substantially increased computational complexity[1]. Recursive feature elimination (RFE) exemplifies this approach, iteratively training classifiers on progressively smaller feature subsets, identifying which features contribute least to predictive performance[1]. Contemporary implementations leverage efficient approximations including SHAP-based elimination to reduce computational burden while maintaining selection quality.

Embedded approaches achieve computational efficiency by integrating feature selection directly into model training, with regularization-based methods like Lasso and elastic net providing differentiable proxies for feature counting[1][11]. A 2021 JMLR paper by Lemhadri and colleagues introduced LassoNet, a neural network framework employing skip connections and feature-level gates to achieve global feature selection while training deep networks end-to-end[27]. The skip connection architecture enables individual features to bypass hidden layers if deemed uninformative by the learning algorithm, yielding an entire regularization path of solutions with varying sparsity levels[27]. Remarkably, LassoNet produces feature rankings directly applicable across different downstream tasks without requiring separate wrapper evaluations, substantially reducing computational costs compared to traditional wrapper methods while maintaining competitive or superior accuracy.

A fundamental challenge in applying Lasso-based methods to neural networks emerged from categorical variable encoding—a 2023 study demonstrated that unlike linear regression, both Lasso and group Lasso exhibit inconsistent feature selection depending on encoding strategy (one-hot versus other approaches) for categorical predictors[11]. This work revealed that group Lasso, while addressing some Lasso limitations, tends toward including excessive features when few are actually required, suggesting that embedded methods require careful consideration of data preprocessing and encoding choices. These findings highlight a critical gap between classical statistical properties and neural network behavior that practitioners frequently overlook.

### Deep Learning-Based Feature Selection Architectures

The explosion of deep learning methodologies has enabled novel feature selection approaches leveraging neural network expressiveness to discover complex feature patterns. A 2022 NeurIPS paper by Gretton and colleagues proposed CompFS, a deep ensemble architecture discovering composite features—groups of features exhibiting predictive interactions—rather than treating individual features as atomic units[8]. The method employs an ensemble of group selection models and aggregate predictors, learning to identify which feature combinations jointly contribute to predictions[8]. Experiments on synthetic datasets with known ground truth revealed that CompFS discovers correct feature groupings with zero false discovery rate while achieving higher similarity metrics to true group structures compared to standard feature selection baselines, demonstrating unique advantages for understanding feature interactions.

Concrete autoencoders (CAEs) represent another influential deep learning approach, employing Gumbel-Softmax layers for differentiable feature selection during reconstruction or prediction tasks[26]. A 2024 paper identified recurring instability issues in vanilla CAE implementations, showing that training often requires excessive time and produces suboptimal performance, particularly when selecting redundant features[26]. The proposed IP-CAE (Indirectly Parameterized CAE) modification addresses these instabilities through an indirect parametrization strategy, enabling stable end-to-end training with non-linear decoders while maintaining superior feature selection accuracy[26]. Comparative experiments demonstrate IP-CAE outperforms LassoNet and Stochastic Tree-based Gate (STG) methods across both reconstruction and classification benchmarks.

A 2023 NeurIPS benchmark study by Cherepanova and colleagues systematically evaluated feature selection for tabular deep learning, constructing challenging benchmarks using real datasets with corrupted and second-order features[15]. The authors proposed Deep Lasso, an input-gradient-based analogue of Lasso for neural networks, showing substantially superior performance compared to classical methods like RFE and random forests when selecting from corrupted or nonlinearly dependent features[15]. This work revealed that classical methods struggle with feature interactions and corrupted signals, while gradient-based neural approaches capture these complex dependencies more effectively.

### Information-Theoretic and Ranking-Based Approaches

Information-theoretic frameworks ground feature selection in mutual information and related information-theoretic quantities, providing principled selection criteria with theoretical justification. A 2024 ICLR paper by Gadgil and colleagues developed a method for dynamic feature selection—sequentially querying features during prediction—by directly predicting conditional mutual information with response variables rather than estimating full distributions[10]. This discriminative approach proves substantially more efficient than generative alternatives, enabling feature selection policies adaptive to varying feature budgets and non-uniform feature acquisition costs[10]. Experiments across diverse datasets demonstrate consistent performance gains over recent state-of-the-art methods, with practical applicability to scenarios where feature acquisition involves real computational or financial costs.

An important methodological advance emerged from recognizing that feature selection and feature importance ranking represent distinct but related problems. A 2024 arXiv paper introduced RAMPART (Ranked Attributions with MiniPatches and Recursive Trimming), a framework explicitly optimizing for top-k ranking accuracy rather than converting importance scores to ranks as post-processing[3]. The method combines adaptive sequential halving with efficient ensembling, progressively focusing computational resources on promising features while eliminating suboptimal candidates[3]. Theoretical guarantees establish that RAMPART achieves correct top-k ranking with high probability under mild consistency conditions. Empirical validation across simulation studies and genomics applications demonstrates consistent superiority over existing importance methods, with particularly strong performance on high-dimensional problems where distinguishing similarly-ranked features proves crucial.

Shapley value-based approaches offer game-theoretic foundations for feature attribution, with SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) becoming industry-standard for local and global explanations[32][28]. A 2024 paper revealed that SHAP explanations prove surprisingly sensitive to upstream data engineering choices including feature encoding and representation strategies[12]. Remarkably, manipulating feature representations while maintaining semantic equivalence can substantially alter SHAP rankings, suggesting that post-hoc explanation methods do not provide model-independent feature importance but rather depend critically on data pipeline design. A 2024 paper proposed ShapG, a sampling-based Shapley value approach for feature importance that operates on undirected graphs of feature relationships, achieving more accurate explanations while substantially reducing computational complexity compared to KernelSHAP and Sampling SHAP methods[28].

### Causal and Fairness-Aware Feature Selection

Recent work has begun addressing critical limitations in standard feature selection by incorporating causal inference principles and fairness considerations. A 2025 arXiv paper proposed an enhanced three-stage framework for feature selection in causal inference, recognizing that proper covariate selection requires identifying confounders while eliminating spurious variables and pure outcome predictors[44]. Standard statistical methods prove inadequate because including only treatment-associated variables introduces bias while balancing on spurious variables increases variance in causal quantity estimation. The proposed framework integrates SVM estimators for exposure models with penalty smoothing functions, demonstrating superior performance in identifying confounders and achieving lower bias and variance in average treatment effect estimation compared to existing methods[44]. Applications to observational healthcare data studying opioid use disorder and suicidal behavior demonstrate practical utility in causal inference contexts.

Recognizing that algorithmic fairness and feature selection remain deeply intertwined, a 2024 paper evaluated fair feature selection methods that consider equal importance across demographic groups rather than maximizing global accuracy alone[43]. The authors propose balancing fairness and accuracy metrics within the feature selection process itself, rather than treating them as competing objectives in post-hoc bias mitigation. Testing on three publicly available healthcare datasets revealed that fairness-aware feature selection achieves simultaneously improved fairness metrics and minimal accuracy degradation, contradicting common assumptions of fundamental performance-fairness trade-offs. This work demonstrates that thoughtful feature selection can address fairness concerns at the model design stage.

### Specialized Application Domains and Emerging Techniques

Feature selection in specialized domains requires domain-specific modifications to accommodate unique data characteristics. A 2024 Nature paper developed VAE-PRS, combining variational autoencoders with polygenic risk score construction for genotypic data, leveraging VAE's ability to model latent genotype distributions while simultaneously predicting phenotypic outcomes[18]. By reconstructing genotype matrices during training, the model captures interaction effects in high-dimensional genetic data while remaining interpretable through SHAP analysis of individual marker contributions. Experiments across blood cell traits demonstrate that VAE-PRS maintains computational efficiency advantages over elastic net while accessing 100k variants compared to elastic net's handling of tens of thousands, suggesting that generative modeling approaches may prove particularly valuable when interaction effects are prevalent.

A 2024 NIH paper proposed FAIM (Fairness-Aware Interpretable Modeling), addressing feature selection in healthcare contexts where eliminating sensitive variables entirely versus intelligently adjusting for them represents a critical choice[47]. Rather than forcing binary inclusion-exclusion decisions, FAIM explores diversity among near-optimal models to achieve contextualized fairness reflecting clinical expertise and stakeholder input. Applications to hospital admission prediction demonstrate that eliminating race and sex variables while maintaining high discriminatory performance proves feasible, contradicting assumptions that protected characteristics must enter models.

Quantum machine learning approaches recently emerged as a frontier for feature selection optimization. A 2024 SPIE paper investigated quantum annealing for feature selection in Internet of Things network intrusion detection, leveraging quantum mechanics principles to solve combinatorial optimization problems inherent in selecting from thousands of network traffic features[67]. The hybrid quantum computing service provides "one-shot" solutions without iterative classical model training, potentially offering computational advantages for extreme feature selection problems where classical evolutionary or heuristic approaches prove infeasible.

## Comprehensive Experimental Analysis and Cross-Cutting Findings

### Comparative Performance Across Methods and Datasets

Synthesizing experimental findings across the reviewed literature reveals several robust cross-cutting patterns despite substantial methodological diversity. First, no single method dominates consistently across different problem contexts; rather, performance depends critically on data characteristics including dimensionality, sample size, feature interaction complexity, and downstream model architecture[1][15][40]. In low-dimensional settings with clear feature-target relationships, simple variance-based filters often match or exceed performance of sophisticated methods while maintaining computational advantages[40]. Conversely, in high-dimensional biological datasets with complex interactions and redundancy patterns, sophisticated multivariate methods and neural approaches achieve meaningful improvements.

Second, feature selection stability—the reproducibility of selected features across random dataset subsamples—represents an independent and crucial evaluation dimension distinct from accuracy[36][40]. A comprehensive study of feature selection stability across four classification methods on high-dimensional genetic data revealed that even when models achieve similar accuracy, their feature selection stability varies dramatically[36]. Feature selection stability impacts generalization to independent datasets and reproducibility of scientific findings, yet remains underreported in many contemporary studies. The authors demonstrated that combining stability metrics with accuracy provides more comprehensive model evaluation, discovering that regularization parameter values substantially influence stability in ways not reflected by accuracy metrics alone.

Third, wrapper and embedded methods generally achieve higher downstream predictive accuracy than filter methods when computational budgets permit their application, since they directly optimize for task-specific performance[1][27]. However, this accuracy advantage comes at substantially increased computational cost, often requiring hundreds or thousands of model training cycles. A performance-driven benchmark study found that Deep Lasso and other gradient-based neural approaches provide efficient compromises, achieving wrapper-quality accuracy with embedded method computational efficiency[15].

### Feature Interaction Detection and Nonlinear Dependencies

A critical limitation of classical univariate filter methods involves their inability to detect feature interactions and nonlinear dependencies, where combinations of features prove predictive despite individual features exhibiting weak target correlations[1][8]. Deep learning approaches demonstrate particular advantage in discovering such interactions. CompFS explicitly targets composite feature discovery, while RAMPART's recursive trimming strategy proves especially valuable for distinguishing similarly-ranked features whose importance emerges only from their combinations[3][8]. Concrete autoencoders achieve comparable interaction detection through learned nonlinear transformations, though at increased computational cost[26].

The benchmark studies consistently reveal that classical methods including random forests and standard Lasso variants struggle dramatically with second-order and higher-order feature interactions[15][60]. This limitation becomes critical in biological domains where gene-gene interactions (epistasis) or environmental-genetic interactions prove essential for understanding disease mechanisms.

### Computational Complexity and Scalability

Filter methods maintain computational advantages in extreme high-dimensional settings, scaling to datasets with tens of thousands or millions of features where wrapper methods prove infeasible[1][40]. The computational complexity of wrapper methods grows roughly quadratically or worse with feature count, rendering them impractical for genomic or imaging datasets. LassoNet and Deep Lasso provide intermediate scalability, handling thousands to tens of thousands of features where pure wrapper approaches fail[15][27].

Interestingly, quantum annealing approaches propose orthogonal solutions to scalability challenges through quantum speedups for combinatorial optimization, though current implementations require access to specialized hardware and demonstrate limited practical deployment[67].

## Emerging Trends and Future Research Directions

### Trend One: Integration with Explainability and Causal Frameworks

The boundary between feature selection, feature importance, and explainability continues blurring as the field recognizes their fundamental connections. Rather than treating feature selection as purely predictive optimization, recent work increasingly integrates causal inference principles, recognizing that different selection objectives (causal effects vs. prediction) require distinct methodologies[44][48]. The shift toward fairness-aware and interpretability-focused feature selection reflects growing recognition that model transparency and alignment with domain expertise represent increasingly critical objectives alongside predictive accuracy[43][47]. Future work will likely develop integrated frameworks where feature selection, causality, fairness, and explainability represent jointly optimized objectives rather than separately addressed concerns.

### Trend Two: Dynamic and Adaptive Feature Selection

Traditional batch feature selection identifies static feature subsets before model training, yet many practical applications benefit from adaptive selection as new data arrives or different prediction tasks emerge. The 2024 ICLR paper on dynamic feature selection via conditional mutual information prediction exemplifies this trend, enabling sequential feature querying with adaptive budgets[10]. Future research will likely develop increasingly sophisticated approaches to task-specific and adaptive feature selection, particularly relevant for transfer learning scenarios where different downstream tasks require different feature subsets despite operating on identical raw feature spaces.

### Trend Three: Hybrid Methods Combining Multiple Selection Criteria

Rather than advocating for single methodologies, recent benchmarking work and sophisticated implementations increasingly employ ensemble approaches combining multiple selection criteria. CompFS ensembles multiple feature selection models to discover composite features[8], while optimized implementations like IP-CAE and Deep Lasso integrate classical regularization principles with neural network flexibility[26][27]. This trend reflects growing recognition that feature selection problems exhibit sufficient complexity that no single criterion captures all relevant aspects—stability, accuracy, interpretability, and efficiency simultaneously require balanced optimization. Future research will develop increasingly sophisticated ensemble strategies that theoretically characterize when and why combining multiple criteria improves overall performance.

## Synthesis and Future Challenges

### Critical Challenges Limiting Current Approaches

Despite substantial methodological advances, feature selection in classification tasks remains challenged by several fundamental limitations. First, the high-dimensional setting where features vastly outnumber samples—common in biological and medical applications—creates fundamental statistical identifiability problems where many distinct feature subsets achieve equivalent predictive performance, rendering feature selection under-determined without additional constraints or domain knowledge[1][40][43]. Second, determining optimal numbers of features remains theoretically and practically challenging; most methods require hyperparameter tuning or external validation to set selection thresholds, creating dependencies on validation data availability. Third, computational complexity continues limiting practical application of sophisticated wrapper and causal methods to extreme high-dimensional problems, despite advances like quantum approaches offering potential future solutions.

### Methodological Gaps and Underexplored Directions

Several promising research directions remain substantially underdeveloped despite their practical importance. First, integration of domain knowledge and expert elicitation into automated feature selection frameworks remains nascent; most methods treat domain knowledge as post-hoc validation rather than integrated prior information. Second, feature selection for structured data (graphs, hierarchies, sequences) has received less attention than tabular data, yet poses unique challenges where feature relationships encode critical information. Third, robustness of feature selection to distribution shift and domain adaptation represents an increasingly important but understudied problem, particularly relevant for deploying models across institutions or time periods where data distributions evolve.

### Recommendations for Practitioners

Based on the comprehensive analysis above, practitioners should consider the following decision framework: (1) For initial exploratory analysis and extreme high-dimensional settings, employ variance filtering or mRMR methods as computationally efficient baselines, recognizing their often-competitive performance; (2) When computational budgets permit, employ embedded methods like LassoNet or gradient-based approaches like Deep Lasso, particularly when downstream models include neural networks; (3) Treat feature selection stability as independent evaluation criterion distinct from accuracy, employing stability metrics alongside standard performance metrics; (4) In high-interaction or nonlinear domains, strongly consider deep learning approaches including concrete autoencoders or ensemble methods, despite their increased complexity; (5) For mission-critical applications including healthcare and criminal justice, explicitly incorporate fairness and causal considerations rather than treating these as post-hoc concerns.

## Conclusion

Feature selection in machine learning classification tasks has evolved from a mature optimization problem toward a sophisticated, multi-objective challenge integrating predictive accuracy, computational efficiency, statistical stability, interpretability, fairness, and causal validity. Contemporary research demonstrates that no universal solution exists; rather, optimal approaches depend critically on data characteristics, downstream model architecture, and application requirements. The convergence toward hybrid methods combining multiple selection criteria, information-theoretic frameworks, deep learning architectures, and causal principles reflects growing recognition of feature selection's fundamental connections to explainability and fairness.

The 2022-2025 period witnessed particular progress in three areas: (1) comprehensive benchmarking studies establishing empirical foundations for method comparison across realistic datasets; (2) integration of feature selection with deep learning through novel architectures like concrete autoencoders and gradient-based approaches; (3) extension of feature selection beyond pure predictive optimization toward incorporating causal and fairness considerations. These advances position the field to address increasingly complex real-world applications requiring simultaneous optimization of multiple competing objectives.

Looking forward, the field will likely see continued emphasis on adaptive and dynamic feature selection, increasingly sophisticated ensemble approaches, deeper integration with causal inference and fairness frameworks, and continued exploration of emerging computational paradigms including quantum approaches. The fundamental tension between statistical rigor (favoring simpler, interpretable methods) and empirical performance (favoring complex, flexible approaches) will remain, requiring practitioners to carefully balance multiple objectives according to domain-specific requirements. Future research should emphasize comprehensive benchmarking on diverse realistic datasets, theoretical characterization of when and why different methods succeed or fail, and development of principled frameworks for combining multiple selection criteria rather than treating them as competing objectives.
