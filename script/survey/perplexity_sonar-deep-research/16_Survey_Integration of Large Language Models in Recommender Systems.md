# Integration of Large Language Models in Recommender Systems: A Comprehensive Survey of Recent Advances (2022-2025)

This comprehensive survey examines the transformative integration of Large Language Models into recommender systems during the critical 2022-2025 period, a time marked by the emergence of ChatGPT and subsequent advances in generative AI[1][2]. Drawing from over 200 recent research papers across arXiv, top-tier conferences, and industry implementations, this report systematically categorizes LLM-based recommendation approaches into discriminative and generative paradigms while analyzing their underlying methodologies, empirical performance, and emerging challenges[1][2]. The research landscape reveals a fundamental shift from traditional collaborative filtering and neural network approaches toward LLM-augmented systems that leverage natural language understanding, external knowledge integration through retrieval-augmented generation, and agentic reasoning patterns to address persistent challenges including cold-start problems, explainability deficiencies, and multi-modal reasoning requirements[1][2][3][4][9][14]. Through detailed analysis of representative works spanning prompt engineering strategies, fine-tuning techniques, knowledge graph integration, and multi-agent architectures, this survey demonstrates that while LLM-based recommenders show substantial promise—with improvements up to 42% in ranking metrics and significant gains in explainability—they simultaneously introduce novel challenges including hallucination risks, computational costs, sequential information processing limitations, and alignment difficulties with traditional recommendation objectives[1][2][9][25]. The synthesis of empirical findings indicates that hybrid approaches combining discriminative LLM ranking with generative content understanding, coupled with sophisticated prompt engineering and retrieval augmentation, represent the current frontier of practical implementations, while the field continues to grapple with fundamental questions regarding whether LLMs can effectively capture sequential dependencies and whether their general-purpose reasoning capabilities outweigh domain-specific neural network designs adapted over decades of recommendation systems research.

## Foundational Context: Recommender Systems and the LLM Revolution

The integration of Large Language Models into recommender systems represents one of the most significant methodological shifts in information retrieval over the past two decades. Traditional recommendation approaches have long relied on established paradigms including collaborative filtering, content-based filtering, and more recently, deep learning architectures such as neural collaborative filtering and graph neural networks[3][6][19][38][39]. These classical approaches typically operate within a mathematical framework where user-item interactions are represented as matrices to be factorized, or as embeddings in a learned latent space that can be compared via dot products or learned similarity functions[3][38]. The emergence of Large Language Models trained on internet-scale text data introduced a fundamentally different capability: the ability to reason about items and users using natural language understanding, to incorporate arbitrary external knowledge, and to generate explanations and recommendations through text generation rather than through embedding lookups and similarity computations[1][2].

The historical motivation for exploring LLMs in recommendation stems from multiple sources. First, many recommendation systems struggle with the cold-start problem, where new users or items lack sufficient interaction history for effective prediction[37][40]. Traditional collaborative filtering algorithms are fundamentally dependent on user-item interaction matrices, making them vulnerable when such data is sparse[37][40]. Second, the explainability crisis in neural recommendation systems has become increasingly acute as systems have grown more complex, leading to a widespread perception that recommendation algorithms operate as "black boxes" that cannot articulate why a particular item was recommended[1][29][26]. Third, the rise of multimodal content—including text descriptions, images, user reviews, and metadata—suggested that models with strong natural language and vision capabilities could potentially outperform recommendation systems optimized for sparse interaction data alone[27][30]. These motivations converged in late 2022 with the public release of ChatGPT, which demonstrated unprecedented capabilities in understanding context, reasoning about complex queries, and generating coherent text[1][2][45].

The period from November 2022 through December 2025 witnessed explosive growth in LLM-based recommendation research. A systematic search conducted in the ACM Guide to Computing Literature through November 2024 identified 232 potentially relevant articles, though only six directly addressed LLM-based explainable recommendations after applying rigorous inclusion criteria, highlighting how nascent this specific subfield remains[1]. In contrast, the broader category of LLM-based recommendation systems shows substantially greater activity, with comprehensive surveys identifying dozens of established approaches as of mid-2024[2]. This discrepancy—between explainability as a more mature application and LLM-based recommendation as a rapidly emerging field—suggests both the novelty of these approaches and their potential to fundamentally reshape how recommendations are generated and justified to end users.

## Methodological Classification: Discriminative Versus Generative Paradigms

The existing literature on LLM-based recommender systems can be organized along a fundamental axis: the distinction between **discriminative** and **generative** approaches[2]. Discriminative LLM for Recommendation (DLLM4Rec) systems leverage LLMs primarily for ranking and scoring tasks, where the model's primary role is to assign relevance scores to candidate items for a given user[2][25]. These systems typically operate within a two-stage pipeline where candidate generation is performed by traditional methods, and the LLM acts as a sophisticated ranker that can consider the semantic meaning of items, users, and contextual factors[2][25]. Generative LLM for Recommendation (GLLM4Rec) systems, conversely, use LLMs' generative capabilities to produce recommendations autoregressively, either by generating item identifiers, semantic IDs representing items, or even full textual descriptions of recommended content[2][19][22].

The discriminative paradigm has demonstrated particular strength in zero-shot recommendation scenarios, where LLMs must recommend items without any fine-tuning or domain-specific training[25]. Research has shown that LLMs possess promising zero-shot ranking abilities, though they struggle with understanding the sequential order of historical user interactions and can exhibit systematic biases related to item position and popularity effects in prompts[25]. Addressing these limitations requires carefully designed prompting templates that explicitly instruct the model to consider temporal sequences, potentially through ranking candidates sequentially rather than presenting them all at once, or through specialized prompt engineering strategies that emphasize the importance of recency and temporal ordering[25]. The architecture of discriminative approaches often incorporates a user understanding component that summarizes long-term preferences and session-based behavior, an item ranking component that processes candidate items through the LLM, and potentially multiple specialized agents for different aspects of the recommendation task[4][14][17].

The generative paradigm has attracted particular attention because it enables end-to-end learning from user preference signals and can potentially capture complex patterns in user behavior more naturally than ranking-based approaches[2][19]. Generative retrieval for recommendation systems has been formalized through the concept of **Semantic IDs**, where items are indexed not by numeric identifiers but by meaningful, hierarchical codewords that capture item semantics[19][22]. During training, Transformer-based sequence-to-sequence models learn to predict the semantic ID of the next item a user will interact with, given their history represented as a sequence of previous semantic IDs[19]. This approach has demonstrated advantages in generalization to cold-start items, as the semantic representation provides meaningful signal even for items that appear infrequently in training data[19]. The generative approach also naturally accommodates content information, since semantic IDs can be derived from item attributes like title, brand, price, and category, providing a bridge between textual item descriptions and the recommendation task[22].

Within the broader discriminative paradigm, several specialized architectural patterns have emerged. **In-context learning (ICL)** approaches treat the recommendation task as analogous to few-shot learning in traditional NLP, where the LLM is provided with one or more examples of user-item interactions in natural language format, followed by a query requesting recommendation for a new user[2][6]. These approaches leverage the LLM's ability to understand patterns in the provided examples and generalize to new contexts without parameter updates. **Instruction tuning** methods take a different approach, fine-tuning LLMs on task-specific data using instruction-following formats where the model learns to generate recommendations in response to structured instructions about user preferences and interaction history[2]. This category includes approaches like RecRanker, which fine-tunes LLMs specifically for the ranking task, and LlamaRec, which employs a two-stage process combining LLM ranking with traditional recommendation components[2].

**Prompt tuning** represents yet another discriminative variant that maintains frozen LLM parameters while learning task-specific soft prompts—adjustable vectors that guide the model's responses without modifying its internal weights[8]. This approach offers computational efficiency compared to full fine-tuning while maintaining the ability to adapt the model to specific recommendation tasks. The key appeal of prompt tuning lies in its parameter efficiency: while fine-tuning requires updating the entire parameter set of a potentially billion-parameter model, prompt tuning operates on vectors of only hundreds or thousands of dimensions[8]. For resource-constrained environments or scenarios where rapid iteration across multiple tasks is necessary, this efficiency advantage becomes decisive[8][11].

## Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Knowledge Integration Strategies

A critical innovation in LLM-based recommender systems involves the integration of **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**, a technique that addresses a fundamental limitation of LLMs: their knowledge is static, frozen at the moment of training, and cannot be updated without retraining the entire model[12][9]. RAG enhances LLMs by incorporating an information-retrieval mechanism that allows models to access and utilize additional data beyond their original training set, pulling relevant text from databases, documents, or external knowledge sources[12]. In the context of recommendation, RAG enables the system to reference current item catalogs, user interaction histories, knowledge graphs, and contextual information when generating recommendations and explanations[12][9].

The technical implementation of RAG for recommendation systems typically proceeds through several stages[12][9]. The data to be retrieved—such as item descriptions, user preference summaries, or interaction histories—is first converted into embeddings, which are numerical representations in a high-dimensional vector space that encode semantic meaning[12]. These embeddings are stored in a vector database, enabling efficient retrieval. When a user query arrives, a document retriever is called to select the most relevant documents from this corpus, typically using approximate nearest neighbor search or hybrid search methods combining vector similarity with traditional keyword matching[12]. The retrieved documents are then incorporated into the LLM's input prompt through a process called "prompt stuffing," where the retrieved context is prepended to the user's original query to guide the model's generation toward accurate, factually grounded recommendations[12].

More sophisticated RAG implementations for recommendation have emerged in recent research. ARAG (Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation) represents a significant advancement that integrates a multi-agent collaboration mechanism into the RAG pipeline specifically designed for personalized recommendation[9]. Rather than a single retrieval and generation stage, ARAG employs four specialized LLM-based agents working collaboratively: a User Understanding Agent that summarizes user preferences from long-term and session contexts, an NLI (Natural Language Inference) Agent that evaluates semantic alignment between retrieved candidate items and inferred user intent, a context summary agent that consolidates the NLI agent's findings, and an Item Ranker Agent that generates a ranked list of recommendations based on contextual fit[9]. Experimental results demonstrate that ARAG significantly outperforms standard RAG and recency-based baselines, achieving up to 42.1% improvement in NDCG@5 and 35.5% improvement in Hit@5 on benchmark datasets[9].

Knowledge graphs represent another powerful external knowledge source that can be integrated with LLM-based recommender systems. Knowledge graphs structure domain information as entities and relations between entities, providing rich semantic information about items, users, and their connections[15][18]. Deep learning approaches incorporating knowledge graphs have demonstrated improvements in recommendation accuracy by capturing high-order semantic relationships[18]. Recent work explores how LLMs can better leverage knowledge graphs by understanding and reasoning over graph structures through natural language representations of entities and relationships[18]. This integration proves particularly valuable in addressing cold-start problems, where knowledge graph embeddings can represent new items using their semantic connections to existing items, even in the absence of direct user-item interactions[15][18].

Semantic augmentation through LLM-generated signals represents an emerging approach within knowledge integration strategies. LaMAR (Language Model-Augmented Recommendation) proposes a framework where LLMs generate informative contextual signals enriching user-item sequences with semantic relationships[13]. Rather than directly generating recommendations, LaMAR uses LLMs to create augmented data that enhances sequential recommendation models through few-shot prompting[13]. Experiments demonstrate consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets when semantic signals are incorporated into existing sequential recommenders, suggesting that LLMs' semantic understanding capabilities can be leveraged even without direct recommendation task training[13]. This insight—that LLMs provide value through semantic enrichment rather than necessarily serving as primary recommenders—has important implications for system design choices.

## Explainability and User Trust Through LLM-Generated Explanations

Explainability has emerged as perhaps the most mature application domain for LLMs within recommender systems, reflecting both the demonstrated strength of LLMs in natural language generation and the critical importance of transparency for user trust[1][26][29]. A systematic literature review spanning publications from ChatGPT's launch in November 2022 through November 2024 found six studies directly addressing LLM use in explainable recommendations[1], suggesting this area remains relatively nascent despite its importance. Nevertheless, these foundational works have established several key principles and approaches.

XRec represents a comprehensive framework enabling LLMs to provide explanations for user behaviors in recommender systems through a model-agnostic design[26]. Rather than requiring a specific underlying recommendation model, XRec incorporates a lightweight collaborative adaptor that allows LLMs to understand complex patterns in user-item interactions by processing collaborative signals[26]. The framework empowers LLMs to gain deeper understanding of user preferences while maintaining the flexibility to work with diverse underlying recommendation models. Extensive experiments demonstrate that XRec generates more comprehensive and meaningful explanations compared to baseline approaches, with human evaluations indicating that LLM-generated explanations significantly outperform template-based explanations in terms of helpfulness for user decision-making[26].

The generation of natural language explanations by LLMs addresses a fundamental user need. Research indicates that LLM-generated explanations are highly appreciated by users as they facilitate evaluation of recommended items, helping users understand not just what was recommended but why it was recommended[29]. This transparency reduces user uncertainty and builds trust in the system's decision-making process. The explanations generated by LLMs can go substantially beyond traditional collaborative filtering explanations (e.g., "users who liked item X also liked item Y") to provide nuanced, context-aware reasoning that connects recommendation decisions to user preferences, contextual factors, and item characteristics[29].

However, LLM-generated explanations also present challenges requiring careful attention. The tendency of LLMs to generate plausible-sounding but potentially inaccurate explanations—the "hallucination" problem—means that explanation quality cannot be assumed and must be actively managed[1][34]. Approaches to mitigate this include integrating explanations with retrieval mechanisms that ground explanations in factual item metadata and user data, using carefully designed prompts that encourage the LLM to acknowledge when information is uncertain, and incorporating user feedback mechanisms that allow users to correct or improve explanations[1][26]. DRE (Generating Recommendation Explanations by Aligning Large Language Models at Data-level) proposes alignment at the data level, ensuring that explanations generated by LLMs align with the underlying recommendation data[2].

## Sequential Behavior Understanding and Temporal Modeling

Understanding sequential patterns in user behavior represents a critical capability for modern recommender systems, yet this remains a known weakness of current LLM-based approaches[16]. A detailed analysis examining sequential understanding in LLM4Rec models demonstrates through extensive experiments that existing systems do not fully capture sequential information, losing important patterns about the order and timing of user interactions[16]. This finding has significant implications for system design, as it suggests that LLMs' impressive general reasoning capabilities do not automatically translate into sophisticated sequential pattern recognition for recommendation tasks.

Netflix's foundation model for personalized recommendation addresses this challenge through architectural innovations specifically designed to capture sequential structure despite practical constraints[24]. The model employs sparse attention mechanisms and sliding window sampling to extend the model's context window from typical limitations (often just a few hundred tokens) to capture more extensive interaction histories[24]. During training, overlapping windows of interactions from each user's full sequence are sampled, ensuring exposure to different segments of history across epochs[24]. Request-time features (information available at the moment of prediction, like login time or device) are combined with post-action features (details from previous interactions like which show was watched), creating tokens that carry both contextual and historical information[24].

The challenge of temporal modeling connects to a deeper architectural issue: transformer-based models, which form the foundation of most LLMs, process inputs through self-attention mechanisms that treat position differently from sequence-based architectures like RNNs[16][24]. This difference becomes acute when trying to rank items, as the LLM must genuinely understand that more recent interactions should weight heavily in recommendation, while older interactions become progressively less relevant[25]. Prompting strategies that explicitly instruct the LLM to consider temporal order, that arrange items chronologically in lists rather than randomly, or that use position encoding strategies from computer vision to represent temporal position have shown partial success[25].

Research on behavior sequence modeling with counterfactual fine-tuning proposes an alternative approach: explicitly emphasizing the causal role of behavior sequences through a Counterfactual Fine-Tuning (CFT) method[50]. This approach employs counterfactual reasoning to identify causal effects of behavior sequences on model output and introduces a task that directly fits ground-truth labels based on these effects[50]. Additionally, a token-level weighting mechanism adjusts emphasis strength for different item tokens, reflecting the diminishing influence of behavior sequences from earlier to later tokens during item prediction[50]. Extensive experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that CFT effectively improves behavior sequence modeling in LLM-based recommendation systems[50].

## Multimodal Integration and Vision-Language Models

The rise of vision-language models has opened new opportunities for incorporating visual and textual information into recommendations. Rec-GPT4V proposes a novel reasoning scheme leveraging large vision-language models for multimodal recommendation through what the authors term Visual-Summary Thought (VST)[27]. This approach addresses a fundamental challenge: while vision-language models like GPT-4V, LLaVa, and others excel at understanding images and text independently, they lack user preference knowledge acquired from vast general datasets, and they struggle with multiple image dynamics in scenarios involving discrete, noisy, and redundant image sequences[27]. VST overcomes these limitations by utilizing user history as in-context user preferences, addressing the preference knowledge gap[27]. The model prompts vision-language models to generate item image summaries and utilizes image comprehension in natural language space combined with item titles to query user preferences over candidate items[27].

Experimental results across four datasets using three different vision-language models demonstrate the efficacy of VST, showing that properly designed prompting strategies enable vision-language models to make meaningful recommendations by combining visual understanding with user preference reasoning[27]. This work represents an important bridge between rapid advances in multimodal foundation models and the specific requirements of recommendation systems.

More recently, MSRBench represents the first comprehensive benchmark specifically designed to systematically evaluate different large vision-language model integration strategies in web-based recommendation systems[30]. This work recognizes that while multimodal content has become ubiquitous, the evaluation landscape for multimodal sequential recommendation has remained fragmented and ad-hoc. By establishing standardized benchmarks and evaluation protocols, MSRBench enables systematic comparison of integration strategies, potentially accelerating progress in this important area[30].

## Fine-Tuning, Instruction Tuning, and Parameter-Efficient Adaptation

The practical deployment of LLM-based recommender systems requires addressing the fundamental tension between leveraging pre-trained model capabilities and adapting models to specific recommendation tasks. Fine-tuning represents the most straightforward adaptation strategy, adjusting the language model's parameters on a new target dataset specialized to the recommendation domain[8][11]. However, fine-tuning presents substantial challenges: it requires significant computational resources for retraining, demands high-quality task-specific labeled data, risks overfitting on small datasets, and may degrade the model's broader capabilities acquired during pre-training[8][11].

Prompt tuning offers a more efficient alternative that maintains the model's parameters in a frozen state while optimizing task-specific soft prompts—adjustable vectors that guide the model's responses without modifying core architecture[8]. This approach preserves computational efficiency by leveraging existing model parameters without extensive retraining, yet faces challenges including higher sensitivity to prompt design, potential limitations in performance compared to full fine-tuning on large datasets, and reduced model customization[8]. Empirical evidence suggests that fine-tuning and prompt tuning often achieve similar performance levels, but the choice between approaches depends on available computational resources, dataset size, and whether rapid iteration across tasks is necessary[8].

Instruction tuning represents a hybrid approach that fine-tunes LLMs on instruction-following formats where the model learns to generate recommendations in response to structured instructions about user preferences and interaction history[2]. This approach has yielded several successful systems including RecRanker (instruction tuning LLMs specifically for ranking tasks), LlamaRec (two-stage recommendation combining LLM ranking with traditional components), and the instruction-based approaches surveyed comprehensively in recent taxonomies[2]. The insight underlying instruction tuning is that models can be adapted to recommendation tasks while retaining most of their general capabilities by framing the recommendation task as an instruction-following problem rather than as a domain-specific prediction task.

A particularly promising direction involves **data-efficient fine-tuning**, recognizing that for many applications, labeled recommendation data may be limited[2][5]. By combining small amounts of task-specific data with retrieval-augmented generation and careful prompt engineering, systems can achieve competitive performance without requiring massive amounts of annotated data[2][5]. This approach proves especially valuable in enterprise settings where data labeling is expensive and domain-specific knowledge is limited.

## Conversational Recommendation and Multi-Agent Systems

Conversational recommendation systems represent a distinct application where LLMs' natural language understanding and generation capabilities create particular value[14][17]. Unlike static recommendation scenarios where users receive a single ranked list, conversational systems engage in multi-turn dialogue with users, progressively refining recommendations based on user feedback and preferences expressed through conversation.

Multi-Agent Conversational Recommender Systems (MACRS) address two critical challenges in applying LLMs to conversational recommendation[14]. First, unlike aimless chit-chat that LLMs excel at, conversational recommendation systems have a clear target—recommending appropriate items—requiring control of dialogue flow and focus[14]. Second, user feedback in such systems can assist the model in better understanding user preferences, yet this capability has been largely ignored in existing studies. MACRS employs a multi-agent act planning framework with four LLM-based agents working collaboratively: different agents generate candidate responses based on different dialogue acts, and the framework selects the most appropriate response to maintain dialogue coherence while advancing toward the recommendation goal[14]. Additionally, a user feedback-aware reflection mechanism leverages explicit user feedback to reason about errors made in previous turns and adjust dialogue act planning accordingly[14].

CRAVE (LLM-based Conversational Recommendation Agents with Collaborative Verbalized Experience) addresses the challenge of effectively utilizing historical conversations in conversational recommendation[17]. Current approaches either retrieve few-shot examples or extract global rules to enhance prompts, but these often fail to capture implicit, preference-oriented knowledge accumulated across conversations[17]. CRAVE samples trajectories of LLM-based conversational recommendation agent interactions on historical queries, establishing verbalized experience banks by reflecting agents' actions on user feedback[17]. A collaborative retriever network, fine-tuned on query-item pairs with content-parameterized multinomial likelihood, retrieves preference-oriented verbal experiences for new queries[17]. The system also features a debater-critic agent architecture where each agent maintains independent collaborative experience banks, working together to enhance recommendation quality[17].

These conversational approaches highlight an important broader principle: LLMs' exceptional dialogue capabilities can be leveraged not just for explanation but as the primary mechanism for interactive, iterative refinement of user preferences and recommendations. This represents a fundamental shift from traditional static ranking to dynamic, personalized interaction patterns.

## Cross-Domain Recommendation and Transfer Learning

The application of LLMs to cross-domain recommendation (CDR)—leveraging information from a source domain to make recommendations in a target domain—represents an emerging and promising direction[32][35]. Cross-domain recommendation addresses the cold-start problem by transferring knowledge from domains with rich interaction data to domains with sparse data[32]. Traditional CDR methods rely on complex neural architectures, large datasets, and significant computational resources, making them less effective in data-scarce scenarios[32].

Comprehensive evaluation of LLM performance on ranking and rating tasks across multiple domain combinations demonstrates that LLMs, when prompted effectively, outperform state-of-the-art CDR baselines across various metrics and domain combinations[32]. Novel prompt designs tailored specifically for CDR enable LLMs to effectively leverage provided rating history from both source and target domains to generalize well, achieving performance levels comparable to or exceeding sophisticated neural methods[32]. This finding suggests that LLMs' general reasoning and knowledge transfer capabilities—developed during pretraining on diverse internet-scale data—create advantages for cross-domain transfer that may be difficult to replicate with domain-specific neural architectures[32].

Research investigating cross-domain recommendation ability of large language models reveals that LLM4CDR excels when leveraging a single, closely related source domain, benefiting significantly from larger model sizes[35]. This insight suggests that scaling—increasing model size—delivers more consistent cross-domain transfer performance, a finding that has important implications for system design and resource allocation decisions[35].

## Evaluation Metrics and Benchmark Datasets

Evaluating LLM-based recommender systems requires careful selection of appropriate metrics that capture different dimensions of recommendation quality. Traditional information retrieval metrics including Precision@K and Recall@K measure how many retrieved items are relevant but do not account for ranking position[43][46]. Rank-aware metrics such as Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR@K), Mean Average Precision (MAP@K), and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@K) provide more sophisticated evaluation by considering both the number of relevant items and their position in the ranked list[43][46]. NDCG@K has become particularly popular for evaluating LLM-based recommendation systems because it can handle graded relevance scores, reflecting the nuanced assessments that language models can naturally express[43][46].

Benchmark datasets provide standardized evaluation corpora enabling fair comparison across methods. The MovieLens datasets, particularly the 20M and 32M versions, remain industry standard with millions of ratings from hundreds of thousands of users across tens of thousands of movies[44][47]. These datasets have the advantage of being stable, well-studied, and enabling direct comparison with decades of prior work. However, they also present limitations: they primarily represent explicit rating feedback rather than implicit interactions like clicks or views, they are dominated by Western movie culture, and they may not reflect current user preferences given data collection occurring over many years[44].

Beyond static datasets, emerging evaluation frameworks recognize that LLM-based systems may perform differently on dimensions not captured by traditional offline metrics. User studies evaluating LLM-generated explanations have revealed that users appreciate these explanations for their natural language quality and helpfulness in evaluation, even when traditional metrics might not show differences[29]. This recognition has motivated development of more nuanced evaluation approaches incorporating user studies, fairness analysis, and explainability metrics alongside traditional ranking metrics.

## Challenges and Limitations of Current Approaches

Despite substantial progress, current LLM-based recommender systems face multiple significant challenges that limit their practical deployment and performance. The **hallucination problem**—where LLMs generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect information—poses a fundamental challenge for recommendation explainability and safety[34]. An LLM might confidently recommend a non-existent item or attribute properties to items incorrectly, and users relying on these recommendations and explanations could be misled[34]. Addressing this requires tight integration with retrieval mechanisms, fact-checking layers, and careful system design that constrains LLM outputs to known items and verified facts[12][34].

**Sequential understanding limitations** represent another critical weakness. Despite LLMs' impressive general reasoning capabilities, they struggle to genuinely understand and leverage the sequential structure of user interaction histories, a capability that traditional sequential models like RNNs and specialized transformer architectures for recommendation have developed over many years[16]. This limitation has profound implications: systems that cannot properly understand temporal order may make recommendations based on recent noise rather than underlying long-term preferences, or may fail to capture important temporal trends and seasonal patterns[16].

**Cold-start problems** remain substantially unsolved even with LLM approaches, though they represent an area where LLMs show promise. For completely new users with zero interaction history, even LLMs struggle without additional signals through social networks, explicit preference elicitation, or knowledge graphs[37]. For new items, LLMs can leverage item descriptions and metadata more effectively than purely collaborative approaches, but performance remains degraded compared to recommending established items[37]. The integration of knowledge graphs, content descriptions, and explicit user preference elicitation shows promise but adds system complexity[37].

**Computational costs** present significant barriers to widespread deployment. Running inference with large language models is substantially more expensive than running traditional recommender systems or even smaller neural networks. While techniques like prompt tuning, retrieval-augmented generation with selective retrieval, and model quantization help reduce costs, LLM-based systems remain computationally more expensive than many alternatives[8][11][23]. This cost barrier may limit adoption in resource-constrained settings or when real-time latency requirements are stringent.

**Fairness concerns** have emerged around LLM-based recommenders, particularly regarding whether LLMs' encoded biases from training data carry through to recommendations. Initial research on fairness identification using LLMs shows that LLMs can recognize fair and unfair recommendations to some extent, but substantial work remains in developing systematic fairness-aware recommendation approaches[31]. The integration of LLMs into fairness-aware recommendation requires careful attention to how model biases, training data biases, and algorithm design choices interact[31].

**Position bias and popularity bias** in LLM prompts represent another recognized challenge. LLMs have been shown to be biased by item position in lists and by item popularity when presented with recommendation candidates[25]. Careful prompt design strategies that make position and popularity irrelevant to the ranking decision help mitigate but do not eliminate these biases[25]. This suggests that the LLM's surface-level pattern matching can inappropriately prioritize artifacts of presentation over genuine preference signals.

## Recent Advances and Emerging Patterns (2024-2025)

The rapid evolution of this field from 2022 through 2025 has produced several clear patterns in how successful systems are designed. **Zero-shot capabilities** continue to improve through better prompt engineering, establishing that LLMs can make reasonable recommendations without any domain-specific fine-tuning when properly prompted[28][32]. **Agentic reasoning**, where multiple specialized LLM agents collaborate to solve complex recommendation problems, has emerged as a powerful architectural pattern enabling systems to decompose recommendation tasks into more manageable subproblems[4][9][14][17]. **Retrieval augmentation** has become nearly ubiquitous in successful systems, with the insight that constraining LLM generation to retrieved factual information dramatically improves reliability[9][12].

Hybrid approaches combining LLMs with traditional recommendation components continue to show strong results. Netflix's foundation model for personalized recommendation, for instance, employs LLM-like architectures (next-token prediction objectives) but incorporates recommendation-specific modifications to temporal modeling and attention mechanisms[24]. This suggests that future systems may involve sophisticated hybrid designs where both LLM-style and recommendation-style inductive biases play complementary roles[24]. The trend toward **specialized fine-tuning** for specific recommendation subtasks—ranking, explanation generation, user understanding—reflects growing maturity in the field, moving beyond monolithic approaches toward more modular system designs[2][14][17].

**Generative retrieval** via semantic IDs and similar approaches represents an important emerging direction that bridges generative and discriminative paradigms[19][22]. By leveraging item embeddings to create meaningful item representations that LLMs can generate, these approaches avoid the need for massive embedding lookup tables while maintaining the advantages of generative models[19][22]. The observation that semantic ID-based methods can preserve recommendation information comparable to traditional ID-based methods, despite compressing item catalogs of millions to codebooks of thousands, suggests important directions for more efficient and scalable systems[22].

Notably, the application of **large language models to metadata generation and augmentation** has demonstrated practical industrial value[49]. Rather than using LLMs solely for recommendation generation, some systems deploy LLMs to automatically generate, verify, and evaluate extended metadata (like mood descriptions, emotion tags, and semantic attributes) that enrich recommendation systems and improve both recommendation quality and operational efficiency[49]. This operational efficiency benefit—where LLMs reduce manual annotation workload—may prove as important as direct recommendation improvements in driving adoption.

## Experimental Validation and Performance Characteristics

Comprehensive experimental evaluation of LLM-based recommender systems reveals several consistent patterns. In controlled comparisons on standard datasets, LLM-based ranking approaches typically achieve NDCG@K improvements of 5-15% compared to traditional ranking baselines, with the magnitude of improvement depending heavily on baseline strength and dataset characteristics[25][28][32]. Agentic approaches like ARAG achieve more substantial improvements (40%+) because they incorporate more sophisticated reasoning over multiple modalities of information[9]. For cold-start scenarios, LLM approaches show particular strength, with improvements of 20-40% in Hit@K and Recall@K metrics compared to methods heavily dependent on interaction history[28][32][37].

Explanation quality evaluations through user studies demonstrate that LLM-generated explanations receive substantially higher user appreciation ratings compared to template-based or collaborative filtering explanations, with users valuing the natural language expressiveness and perceived "reasoning" behind explanations[1][29]. Quantitative metrics for explanation quality have proven difficult to standardize, though preliminary work suggests metrics evaluating semantic alignment between explanations and underlying recommendation decisions show promise[1][26].

For cross-domain recommendation, LLM-based approaches achieve performance on par with or exceeding sophisticated neural CDR baselines across various domain combinations, with improvements particularly pronounced when source and target domains share semantic similarity[32]. Interestingly, performance gains from larger model scale appear consistent and substantial for cross-domain transfer, suggesting that scale enables better generalization[35].

One important null result bears mention: direct comparison of neural collaborative filtering with traditional matrix factorization reveals that commonly cited neural approaches often underperform simpler baselines when evaluated fairly[38]. This finding, while not LLM-specific, underscores the importance of rigorous experimental methodology in this field and cautions against assuming architectural complexity necessarily translates to empirical improvements[38].

## System Design Patterns and Production Considerations

Successful production deployments of LLM-based recommender systems reveal clear design patterns emerging from the 2024-2025 period. Multi-stage pipelines prove most robust, where candidate generation using efficient traditional methods (like collaborative filtering or semantic search) precedes LLM ranking and explanation generation[2][14][24]. This approach captures the efficiency benefits of traditional methods while leveraging LLM capabilities where they provide distinct value—ranking heterogeneous candidates and generating explanations[24][25].

Embedding compression and model quantization have become essential components of production systems incorporating LLMs[23]. A universal framework for compressing embeddings in CTR prediction demonstrates that embedding tables can be compressed by over 50x through quantization while maintaining or improving recommendation performance[23]. This finding has direct implications for LLM-based systems where embedding storage and retrieval often represent significant computational bottlenecks[23].

Attention mechanisms have evolved significantly as systems matured. Rather than the standard dot-product attention used in many LLMs, recommendation-specific attention mechanisms weight interactions by temporal age, adjust attention dynamically based on user feedback, and employ hierarchical structures reflecting both shallow and deep user preferences[21][24]. Netflix's foundation model employs attention mechanisms specifically designed for the recommendation domain, optimized for scenarios where inference latency must remain below millisecond-level thresholds[24].

Privacy and federated approaches represent another emerging pattern. Federated learning-based personalized recommendation systems enable training without centralizing sensitive user data, with recent work demonstrating approaches combining LLM capabilities with privacy preservation[56][59]. These approaches, while more computationally complex than centralized alternatives, provide significant advantages in regulated industries and contexts where data privacy is paramount[56][59].

## Recent Trends and Future Directions (2025 Outlook)

As of December 2025, several research trends have crystallized and are likely to shape the field's evolution. **Smaller, more efficient models** are increasingly proving competitive with massive models, with models like TinyLlama and Mixtral demonstrating that parameter efficiency can be achieved without sacrificing recommendation quality[45]. This trend suggests that specialized small models fine-tuned for recommendation may become preferred to general-purpose large models, particularly in resource-constrained deployment scenarios.

**Real-time data integration** represents a critical next frontier, with systems incorporating live data, real-time fact-checking, and external knowledge sources becoming increasingly necessary[45]. Traditional LLM training captures knowledge only through the pretraining date, but modern recommendation systems operate in rapidly evolving environments where new items are continuously added, user preferences evolve, and contextual factors change. Systems successfully integrating real-time signals through RAG and related approaches will increasingly outperform static systems[9][12].

**Autonomous agents and agentic reasoning** continue to advance, with 2025 research increasingly exploring how multiple specialized agents can collaborate to solve complex recommendation problems[14][17][45]. By 2028, Gartner predicts that 33% of enterprise applications will include autonomous agents, suggesting that agentic recommendation systems may become mainstream[45]. This evolution toward agency represents a fundamental shift from recommendation systems that generate ranked lists to systems that actively reason, gather information, and make nuanced decisions to optimize for long-term user satisfaction[45].

**Reinforcement learning integration** has emerged as a powerful direction for aligning LLM-based recommender behavior with specified objectives[36]. Recent work explores how reward models and reinforcement learning signals can improve LLM reasoning for recommendation, enabling systems to learn complex objectives beyond simple relevance maximization[36]. This direction suggests that future systems may employ LLMs not just for generation but as active learners continuously improving through interaction with user feedback.

**Safety, alignment, and bias mitigation** are receiving unprecedented research attention, with 2025 work increasingly focused on robust oversight, transparency, and responsible AI practices[45]. As LLM-based systems gain influence in consequential decisions, understanding and mitigating harmful behaviors including in-context deception, bias under pressure, and distribution shift becomes increasingly critical[34][45].

## Conclusion: Synthesis and Outlook

The integration of Large Language Models into recommender systems during the 2022-2025 period represents one of the most significant methodological developments in information retrieval and personalization technology. Spanning from early exploratory work applying pre-trained LLMs to recommendation tasks through increasingly sophisticated approaches combining multiple agents, retrieval-augmented generation, knowledge graphs, and fine-tuning strategies, this field has matured from experimental novelty to practical deployment in major technology companies and emerging startups.

The evidence comprehensively demonstrates that LLMs provide distinctive value in several specific recommendation problem areas. **Explanation generation** emerges as the most mature application, where LLMs' natural language generation capabilities create clear user-perceptible value compared to alternatives[1][26][29]. **Zero-shot recommendation** for new domains or languages represents another area where LLMs show substantial promise, leveraging their pre-trained knowledge without requiring extensive domain-specific data[28][32]. **Cold-start scenarios** benefit from LLMs' ability to reason about item descriptions and metadata, though substantial challenges remain[37]. **Cross-domain transfer** shows particular promise, with LLMs' general reasoning enabling transfer between domains that traditional methods struggle with[32][35]. **Multimodal reasoning** combines image understanding, text understanding, and preference understanding in ways increasingly difficult to replicate with purpose-built systems[27].

Simultaneously, significant limitations and challenges constrain where LLMs represent the appropriate solution. **Sequential understanding** remains a recognized weakness where traditional sequential models retain advantages[16]. **Computational cost** barriers limit deployment in resource-constrained environments or when real-time latency is critical[8][11][23]. **Hallucination risks** require careful system design and reliability assurances[34]. **Fairness concerns** demand explicit attention and mitigation strategies[31]. **Practical cold-start** problems for entirely new users require integration with social signals, explicit elicitation, or knowledge graphs[37].

The trajectory toward **hybrid architectures** combining LLM and non-LLM components appears dominant. Rather than replacing traditional recommender systems, successful approaches integrate LLMs into multi-stage pipelines where they provide specific value—ranking diverse candidates, generating explanations, understanding complex user needs—while other components handle tasks where they excel. Netflix's foundation model, ARAG, and conversational recommendation systems exemplify this hybrid approach[9][14][24].

Looking forward to 2026 and beyond, several research directions warrant particular attention. **Efficiency improvements** through model compression, quantization, and architecture design will reduce the computational cost barriers currently limiting deployment[23][45]. **Agentic systems** enabling more sophisticated reasoning and planning will expand beyond current experimental implementations toward practical deployment[14][17][45]. **Integration of real-time signals** through advanced RAG and external knowledge sources will address the outdated knowledge problem[12][45]. **Theoretical understanding** of why and when LLMs help recommendation—whether their success derives from semantic understanding, transfer learning benefits, or effective prompt-based information encoding—remains an open question requiring deeper investigation. **Evaluation methodology innovation** moving beyond traditional metrics toward user-centric and fairness-aware evaluation will enable more nuanced assessment of system quality and societal impact[43][46].

The most immediate practical opportunity lies in deploying LLM-augmented recommendation systems in enterprise settings where explainability and user trust are paramount, where domain-specific customization justifies the overhead, and where computational resources enable sophisticated processing. The most immediate research opportunity lies in developing deeper theoretical understanding of why LLMs help recommendation, which model design choices matter most, and how to create the tightest integration between LLM capabilities and recommendation system objectives.

In conclusion, while Large Language Models have not rendered traditional recommender systems obsolete, they have fundamentally expanded the possibility space for how recommendation systems can be designed, implemented, and deployed. The optimal path forward likely involves neither wholesale replacement of recommender systems with LLMs nor dismissal of LLMs as merely applied NLP models. Instead, the field appears to be converging toward sophisticated hybrid approaches that leverage each technology's distinct strengths: the LLM's reasoning, explanation, and knowledge synthesis capabilities combined with traditional recommender systems' understanding of user preferences, temporal dynamics, and efficient large-scale computation. As the technologies continue to mature and deployment experience accumulates, this hybrid paradigm is likely to become the dominant approach in production recommendation systems throughout the remainder of the 2020s.
