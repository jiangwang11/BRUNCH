# Multi-Label Learning Systems and Extensions: A Comprehensive Survey of Recent Advances (2022-2025)

Multi-label learning represents a fundamental paradigm shift in machine learning classification tasks, where individual instances can be assigned multiple labels simultaneously rather than confined to a single class label, reflecting the complexity inherent in real-world applications from image annotation to biomedical text classification. This comprehensive survey examines the state-of-the-art advances in multi-label learning systems from 2022 to 2025, synthesizing recent methodological innovations that address critical challenges including label correlations, computational efficiency, partial annotations, and extreme label spaces. Recent developments demonstrate significant progress in leveraging deep learning architectures, contrastive learning frameworks, large language models, and graph-based approaches to capture complex label dependencies while maintaining computational tractability. Through systematic analysis of over fifty peer-reviewed publications from premier venues including NeurIPS, IJCAI, ACM conferences, and arXiv preprints, this review identifies key research directions centered on handling incomplete data scenarios, addressing long-tail distributions, and developing efficient algorithms for extreme multi-label classification tasks. The synthesis reveals that contemporary approaches increasingly prioritize the integration of semantic information through contrastive learning mechanisms, the exploitation of label-label correlations through graph neural networks, and the adaptation of foundation models to multi-label settings, establishing a foundation for understanding future research trajectories in this rapidly evolving field.

## Evolution of Multi-Label Learning Paradigms: Historical Context and Current Landscape

Multi-label learning has evolved substantially over the past decade, transitioning from relatively simple binary relevance approaches and power-set methods to sophisticated deep learning systems that explicitly model label dependencies and handle extreme label spaces. Early foundational work established that naive approaches treating multi-label problems as independent binary classification tasks consistently underperform compared to methods that capture inter-label relationships. The historical challenge of exponential output spaces, where the number of possible label combinations grows exponentially with the number of labels, has motivated increasingly sophisticated solutions. Contemporary methods recognize that real-world multi-label datasets exhibit pronounced long-tail distributions where a few dominant labels appear frequently while numerous rare labels appear in only a handful of examples, creating significant performance disparities between head and tail classes. The field has also recognized the practical importance of partial label scenarios where complete label annotations are either prohibitively expensive or fundamentally unattainable, motivating weakly-supervised and semi-supervised approaches. Current research increasingly emphasizes scalability to extreme label spaces containing hundreds of thousands of labels, as encountered in recommendation systems, product classification, and knowledge graph completion tasks. The integration of pre-trained language models and vision models into multi-label frameworks represents a significant methodological shift, leveraging abundant unlabeled data through transfer learning mechanisms while reducing dependency on large amounts of labeled examples.

## Label Correlation Exploitation Through Graph and Network-Based Methods

### Capturing Label Dependencies via Graph Representations

Recent advances demonstrate that explicitly modeling label correlations substantially improves multi-label classification performance across diverse domains. Researchers have increasingly adopted graph-based representations to encode label relationships, recognizing that labels exhibit complex dependencies that vary across different feature spaces and instance subgroups. The use of graph convolutional networks combined with hierarchical structures has proven particularly effective for capturing both global label relationships and fine-grained hierarchical dependencies within label taxonomies. A hierarchical multi-label classification model enhanced with heterogeneous graph semantics achieved precision of 84.21%, recall of 88.89%, and F1-score of 86.49% on science and technology news classification, significantly outperforming baseline approaches that treat labels independently[2]. This model integrates graph convolutional networks to extract node information and hierarchical relationships from heterogeneous graphs while incorporating prior knowledge from domain knowledge graphs to address data scarcity challenges. The superiority of this approach over hierarchical attention networks (HAN) and other graph-based methods underscores the effectiveness of heterogeneous graph construction mechanisms that integrate prior knowledge to enhance understanding of hierarchical labels[2].

Label distribution learning represents an alternative but complementary perspective on leveraging label correlations, wherein each instance receives a label distribution rather than discrete binary or multi-label assignments. Recent research has revealed that label distribution matrices typically maintain full rank rather than exhibiting the low-rank structure traditionally assumed in prior work, challenging foundational assumptions in this domain[7]. Nevertheless, incorporating auxiliary multi-label learning tasks can effectively capture low-rank label correlations, with methods exploiting this auxiliary structure demonstrating superior performance across extensive experiments[7]. The insight that low-rank label correlation finds widespread adoption in multi-label learning literature due to the often low-rank nature of label co-occurrence patterns suggests that label distribution learning can benefit from explicitly incorporating multi-label classification as an auxiliary task[7]. These findings establish that label-label correlations remain fundamentally important for addressing the exponential output space challenge, though the precise mechanisms for capturing these correlations require careful consideration of dataset characteristics and task structure.

### Extreme Multi-Label Classification with Label Features

Extreme multi-label classification (XMC) addresses scenarios where label spaces contain millions of possible labels, presenting computational and memory challenges that render conventional dense approaches infeasible. A novel approach termed Gandalf leverages label co-occurrence graphs to treat label features as additional training data points, fundamentally changing how label information is exploited[10]. By constructing valid training instances from label features and generating soft-label targets using label co-occurrence patterns, Gandalf achieves training sets less than half the original size while surpassing the performance of models trained on complete original datasets, particularly on tail labels[10]. This approach demonstrates that creating synthetic training instances from label features leads to smoother training signals due to soft labels, addressing the high variance inherent in long-tail distributions[10]. The integration of label features as additional supervised training points, rather than pursuing contrastive learning approaches, proves remarkably effective, yielding average improvements of 5% across six state-of-the-art algorithms and four benchmark datasets[10]. Recent developments in extreme multi-label classification increasingly recognize label features as valuable auxiliary information, a perspective shift from treating labels primarily as prediction targets rather than exploitable data sources.

More recent work on prototypical extreme multi-label classification introduces dynamic margin loss mechanisms to address the high granularity and ambiguity inherent in extreme label spaces[31]. The proposed method, PRIME, develops label prototypes that aggregate information from multiple queries while incorporating a novel adaptive triplet loss formulation accounting for semantic relationships among labels[31]. This approach enables positive and negative labels to be projected closer in embedding space when semantically related, while also accommodating uncertain cases with ambiguous samples or missing labels—a well-known limitation in extreme label spaces[31]. PRIME achieves state-of-the-art results while maintaining computational efficiency comparable to or better than competing approaches, demonstrating that prototype-based metric learning provides an effective alternative to data-to-data comparisons at extreme scale[31]. The synergy between prototype-based representation learning and dynamic margin adjustments suggests that future extreme multi-label systems will increasingly incorporate semantic awareness into metric learning frameworks.

## Contrastive Learning Approaches for Multi-Label Classification

### Supervised Contrastive Learning for Multi-Label Settings

Contrastive learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm for representation learning in multi-label settings, though adapting contrastive objectives to multi-label scenarios introduces non-trivial challenges absent in single-label classification. Traditional supervised contrastive learning, originally developed for single-label scenarios, requires modification to accommodate the reality that multiple instances may share multiple labels with varying degrees of overlap. A novel contrastive learning approach for multi-label classification introduces a modified loss function enabling accurate modeling of label overlap between sample pairs[12]. This MultiSupCon framework achieves top-1 mean average precision of 73.9% on the CelebA dataset using TResNet-M architecture, representing a 2.8% improvement over previous contrastive learning methods evaluated on the same architecture[12]. The innovation lies in leveraging information about label overlap between samples while maintaining the benefits of contrastive learning's supervised signal, demonstrating that with appropriate loss function design, deep networks can learn patterns containing information about correlations between classes[12]. Despite its effectiveness, supervised contrastive learning faces inherent limitations when applied to datasets with small numbers of labels and ranking-based evaluation metrics, though it demonstrates excellent performance on datasets with large numbers of labels in terms of Macro-F1 scores[9].

A comprehensive empirical study of contrastive learning loss for multi-label classification across diverse settings including datasets with small and large numbers of labels, varying amounts of training data, and applications spanning computer vision and natural language processing, reveals that promising outcomes of contrastive learning stem not solely from consideration of label interactions but equally from robust optimization schemes inherent to contrastive loss[9]. The research demonstrates that while supervised contrastive loss faces challenges with datasets containing few labels, performance divergences become substantial when evaluating on datasets containing large label sets[9]. These findings suggest that label count and dataset size fundamentally influence the effectiveness of contrastive approaches, requiring careful consideration when selecting loss functions for specific multi-label application domains.

### Multi-Agent Collaboration Frameworks

Recent developments in text classification introduce multi-agent collaboration frameworks that integrate BERT with specialized agents for enhanced classification accuracy and robustness[25]. A novel framework employing Lexical, Contextual, Logic, Consensus, and Explainability agents through dynamic threshold-based escalation mechanisms achieves a 5.5% increase in accuracy compared to standard BERT-based classifiers across diverse text classification tasks[25]. While not exclusively designed for multi-label classification, this multi-agent approach demonstrates the potential for collaborative decision-making to improve classification robustness, particularly for handling low-confidence predictions that might benefit from specialized analysis[25]. The generalizability of this approach to multi-label scenarios suggests that future frameworks might employ specialized agents for analyzing label correlations and handling ambiguous cases, though current implementations focus primarily on single-label settings.

## Handling Incomplete Data, Partial Labels, and Weak Supervision

### Partial-Label Learning and Label Disambiguation

Partial-label learning represents a critical challenge in practical multi-label settings where annotations are incomplete, with each instance receiving a candidate label set containing the true labels mixed with false positives. A cross-selection strategy enabling two deep models to select true labels from partially labeled data introduces a novel pseudo-label refinement mechanism[11]. The CroSel method leverages historical predictions for label identification, introducing consistency regularization through co-mix augmentation to avoid sample waste and minimize noise from false label selection[11]. Extensive experiments demonstrate that CroSel consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets while achieving over 90% accuracy and quantity for true label selection on CIFAR-type datasets across various partial-label settings[11]. The consistency regularization mechanism proves particularly effective for maintaining training stability while identifying true labels with high precision[11].

Complementary research on partial-label learning provides fundamental empirical analysis addressing what makes partial-label learning algorithms effective, identifying common behaviors among successful methods characterized by progressive transition from uniform to one-hot pseudo-labels[8]. This analysis reveals that high accuracy on benchmark-simulated datasets can misleadingly amplify perceived effectiveness of some general techniques that improve representation learning but provide limited impact on addressing inherent challenges of partial-label learning[8]. The identification of mini-batch partial-label purification as critical for achieving top performance, combined with a proposed minimal working algorithm that proves surprisingly simple yet effective, provides valuable insights for algorithm design[8].

### Data Scarcity and Few-Shot Multi-Label Scenarios

Few-shot learning scenarios, where classifiers must learn from extremely limited labeled examples, present acute challenges for multi-label classification particularly when label spaces contain many classes. FusionSent, an efficient prompt-free approach for few-shot classification of scientific documents with many classes, leverages contrastive fine-tuning of multiple sentence embedding models whose parameters are subsequently fused[21]. The approach uses available training examples and their label texts to contrastively fine-tune two different sentence embedding models before combining their complementary knowledge into a single model, achieving significant improvements of 6.0 F1 points on average across multiple scientific document classification datasets[21]. Beyond standard benchmarks, this work introduces a new dataset containing 203,961 scientific articles with 130 classes from arXiv category taxonomy, providing valuable resources for few-shot multi-label classification research[21]. The fusion-based approach suggests that leveraging complementary representations from differently initialized models can substantially improve few-shot performance in multi-label settings.

## Large Language Models and Foundation Model Adaptation

### LLM-Based Multi-Label Classification

Large language models have introduced new paradigms for multi-label classification by leveraging their inherent understanding of semantic relationships and complex textual patterns. A large language model-based framework for sequential sentence classification addresses fundamental limitations in model size, sequence length handling, and support for multi-label settings, proposing LLM-SSC that generates classification labels through designed prompts incorporating demonstrations and query descriptions[39]. The framework introduces a multi-label contrastive learning loss with auto-weighting scheme enabling effective multi-label classification while supporting both in-context learning and task-specific tuning paradigms[39]. A newly released biomedical dataset (BIORC800) containing unstructured abstracts with manual annotations provides crucial resources for evaluating LLM-based approaches to multi-label sentence classification[39]. Experimental demonstrations confirm that LLM-SSC achieves strong performance without requiring extensive model scale increases, suggesting that prompt engineering and contrastive loss design provide viable alternatives to model scale expansion.

Open-world multi-label text classification under extremely weak supervision represents an even more challenging scenario where users provide only brief objective descriptions without label inventories or ground-truth examples. The X-MLClass framework iteratively discovers label spaces and builds multi-label classifiers by leveraging large language models to generate keyphrases, clustering them to form initial label spaces, and subsequently identifying long-tail labels through analyzing documents with low classifier confidence[19][22]. Comparative evaluation against traditional label generation methods demonstrates significantly higher coverage scores for ground-truth label spaces, achieving 40% and 30% improvements on AAPD and RCV1-V2 datasets respectively[19]. The framework's ability to discover label spaces with minimal human annotation effort, requiring only brief classification objective descriptions, addresses acute practical challenges in real-world multi-label scenarios where complete label hierarchies remain unknown a priori[22].

### Vision-Language Models for Multi-Label Visual Recognition

Integration of vision-language models into multi-label visual recognition tasks leverages pre-trained semantic understanding to address challenges inherent to long-tailed distributions and partial annotations. The CAPNET framework explicitly models label correlations from CLIP's textual encoder, incorporating graph convolutional networks for label-aware propagation and learnable soft prompts for refined embeddings[41]. The framework addresses a critical limitation of CLIP's original design, which optimizes for single-label image-text matching and proves suboptimal for multi-label tasks, through correlation adaptation mechanisms that derive reliable semantic inter-class relationships from well-sampled classes rather than directly from imbalanced datasets[41]. Distribution-balanced Focal loss with class-aware re-weighting enables optimized training under imbalance, while test-time ensembling and parameter-efficient fine-tuning improve generalization[41]. Extensive experiments on VOC-LT, COCO-LT, and NUS-WIDE benchmarks demonstrate substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods, validating the effectiveness of explicit label correlation modeling for long-tailed multi-label visual recognition[41].

Vision-language pseudo-labeling introduces an alternative perspective addressing multi-label image classification in single-positive label settings where only one positive class annotation exists despite multiple objects being present[38]. The VLPL framework uses vision-language models to suggest strong positive and negative pseudo-labels, substantially improving performance beyond previous approaches that relied solely on loss function innovations[38]. Evaluations across Pascal VOC, MS-COCO, NUS-WIDE, and CUB-Birds datasets demonstrate that VLPL outperforms several strong baselines with particularly substantial improvements on MS-COCO (15.6%), NUS-WIDE (15.2%), and CUB-Birds (11.3%), underscoring the effectiveness of vision-language guidance for pseudo-label generation[38]. This approach suggests that leveraging rich semantic representations from foundation models provides more reliable signal than purely data-driven pseudo-label generation mechanisms.

## Hierarchical and Fine-Grained Multi-Label Classification

### Hierarchical Structures and Label Hierarchies

Hierarchical multi-label classification extends standard multi-label settings by incorporating structured relationships among labels, enabling models to enforce consistency constraints reflecting domain-specific taxonomies. Recent work on hierarchical multi-label classification with missing information for benthic habitat imagery applies state-of-the-art self-supervised learning techniques on large seafloor image datasets while studying performance on complex hierarchical multi-label downstream tasks[27]. The approach demonstrates capacity to conduct hierarchical multi-label training under multiple levels of missing annotation information, addressing practical scenarios where heterogeneous real-world data collected by multiple research groups employ differing data collection protocols[27]. Crucially, when using smaller one-hot image label datasets typical of local or regional scale projects, models pre-trained with self-supervision on larger in-domain benthic data substantially outperform ImageNet pre-trained models, establishing the importance of domain-specific pre-training for hierarchical tasks[27]. These findings validate that self-supervised pre-training provides effective alternatives to standard ImageNet initialization for specialized domains with hierarchical label structures.

### Label-Specific and Disentangled Representations

A novel approach to multi-label classification through disentangled label feature learning introduces the One-specific-Feature-for-One-Label (OFOL) mechanism, contrasting with traditional single-label inspired approaches that learn shared features for multiple labels[43]. The proposed Disentangled Label Feature Learning (DLFL) framework proves mathematically that shared-feature mechanisms fail to maintain high similarities with multiple classifiers simultaneously when minimizing cross-entropy loss, establishing theoretical motivation for label-specific features[43]. Through learnable semantic queries and Semantic Spatial Cross-Attention modules, the framework localizes label-related spatial regions and aggregates their features into label-specific representations[43]. Evaluations across eight datasets spanning multi-label classification, pedestrian attribute recognition, and continual multi-label learning demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, validating that explicit label disentanglement substantially improves robustness and discriminative power[43]. This conceptual shift away from shared representations represents an important perspective change in multi-label architecture design, suggesting that future approaches should prioritize label-specific feature paths.

Label-specific metric learning provides another avenue for improving similarity measurement in multi-label scenarios by learning different metrics for different label subspaces. A label-specific multi-metric learning framework (LSMM) recognizes that consistent metric learning approaches neglect nonlinear distribution characteristics by learning label-specific local metrics for different instances alongside a global metric capturing high-order label correlations[56]. Partition strategies including semantic-based and cluster-based approaches enable learning of local metrics in disjoint partitions while global metrics implicitly exploit high-order relationships across all labels[56]. The combined framework utilizing global and label-specific local metrics for semantic similarity measurement demonstrates superiority across benchmark multi-label datasets, providing evidence that nonlinear distribution characteristics warrant label-specific treatment[56].

## Addressing Long-Tail Distributions and Class Imbalance

### Multi-Focal Modifier Loss and Head-Tail Balancing

Long-tail distributions, wherein a few dominant head classes contain numerous examples while many tail classes contain only a handful of examples, present fundamental challenges to multi-label classification performance. A comprehensive framework addressing both partial labeling and long-tailed multi-label classification introduces Multi-Focal Modifier (MFM) Loss addressing two imbalance types: intra-instance positive-negative imbalance and inter-instance head-tail imbalance[32]. The framework's progressive learning paradigm proceeds through Correction, Modification, and Balance stages, with label correction mechanisms gradually addressing missing labels according to predicted confidence and dynamically adjusting classification loss under real-time estimated class distributions[32]. The Modification module employs dual focal factors handling hard negatives/positives and rare categories independently, while Head-Tail Balance (HTB) modules measure optimization direction and develop balanced learning schemes using head and tail model distillation[32]. Extensive experimental evaluations demonstrate improvements across prevalent long-tail and multi-label model families on benchmark datasets, validating the effectiveness of comprehensive approaches addressing both imbalance sources.

Game-theoretic approaches to long-tail multi-label learning recognize that head label dominance prevents gradient signal from reaching tail classes, fundamentally affecting optimization dynamics. Recent research formalizes long-tail imbalance in multi-label learning through game-theoretic frameworks where head labels dominate gradient signals while rare labels struggle to achieve adequate gradient flow[35]. This perspective motivates novel training strategies that explicitly balance gradient contributions across head and tail classes, though detailed algorithmic innovations remain to be fully characterized in available publications. The game-theoretic viewpoint provides valuable conceptual insights into why standard approaches fail on long-tail distributions and motivates principled solutions beyond ad-hoc loss reweighting.

### Curriculum Learning and Progressive Training

Curriculum learning strategies that progressively introduce more complex examples can effectively address class imbalance by ensuring models develop representations capturing both head and tail class characteristics. A novel curriculum learning framework for multi-label emotion classification (CLF-MEC) employs category-level assessment of sample difficulty rather than instance-level difficulty, enabling dynamic difficulty adjustment as the model improves[45]. The framework implements "learning" and "tackling" training modes trained alternately to mimic human learning processes, directing additional attention to hard-to-learn categories through contrastive learning for enhanced semantic representation[45]. Experimental validation demonstrates significant improvements in multi-label emotion classification, suggesting that category-aware curriculum strategies provide effective mechanisms for balanced training across classes. The integration of contrastive learning within curriculum frameworks establishes synergies between two complementary training paradigms, motivating investigation of their combined benefits for long-tail distributions.

Curriculum learning based masked autoencoders (CurriMAE) apply curriculum principles to self-supervised pre-training for multi-label medical imaging, progressively increasing masking ratios during pretraining to balance task complexity and computational efficiency[48]. The approach avoids exhaustive masking ratio tuning through progressive masking schedules, demonstrating superior performance compared to ResNet, ViT-S, and standard masked autoencoders while reducing computational costs[48]. Evaluations on multi-labeled pediatric thoracic disease classification establish CurriMAE as an effective and scalable framework, suggesting that curriculum-based pre-training strategies merit wider application to multi-label settings requiring efficient self-supervised learning.

## Robustness, Noise Handling, and Adversarial Considerations

### Learning with Noisy Multi-Label Annotations

Real-world multi-label datasets frequently contain noisy or incorrect annotations, yet robust approaches to handling such noise remain limited in the multi-label literature. A method for robust multi-label learning with noisy annotations proposes BalanceMix, a unified data augmentation approach addressing both label imbalance and annotation noise through samplers generating minority-augmented instances with high diversity[50]. The framework refines multi-labels at label-wise granularity, categorizing noisy labels as clean, re-labeled, or ambiguous for robust optimization[50]. Extensive experiments on three benchmark datasets demonstrate that BalanceMix outperforms state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for multi-label settings[50]. This approach establishes that simultaneous consideration of both imbalance and noise provides more effective solutions than addressing either challenge independently.

Deep neural network methods for learning from noisy multi-label food recognition employ Attentive Feature Mapping with adaptive MixUp, considering the degree of ingredient overlap when generating soft labels[53]. The method's MixUp adaptation reduces memorization of noisy multi-labels through degree-of-overlap consideration, while probabilistic variants provide robustness analysis against data perturbations[53]. Evaluations on food datasets demonstrate notable performance improvements in Jaccard index and F1 scores, validating that ingredient-aware soft label generation reduces harmful memorization effects[53]. These specialized approaches suggest that domain-specific considerations regarding label relationships can enhance robustness mechanisms.

### Adversarial Robustness in Multi-Label Settings

Multi-objective approaches to adversarial robustness establish connections to multi-label classification by promoting consistent feature representations across natural and adversarial examples. MOREL, a multi-objective representation learning framework, encourages models to produce consistent features for inputs within the same class despite adversarial perturbations through multi-positive contrastive losses[55]. By aligning natural and adversarial features in shared embedding spaces, MOREL achieves substantial improvements in accuracy-robustness trade-offs without requiring architectural changes at test time[55]. While not exclusively designed for multi-label settings, the insights regarding robust representation learning through contrastive mechanisms extend naturally to multi-label scenarios where maintaining consistent representations across multiple class dimensions becomes increasingly critical.

## Semi-Supervised and Weakly-Supervised Multi-Label Approaches

### Semi-Supervised Learning with Pseudo-Labels

Semi-supervised multi-label learning leverages both labeled samples with multiple labels and unlabeled samples to improve classification performance. Semi-Supervised Multi-Label Learning with Balanced Binary Angular Margin loss (S²ML²-BBAM) addresses variance bias problems arising from learning with labeled and pseudo-labeled samples simultaneously[33]. The approach extends binary angular margin loss with feature angle distribution transformations under Gaussian assumptions, iteratively updating distributions during training[33]. Efficient prototype-based negative sampling methods maintain high-quality negative samples for each label, while theoretical extensions address feature angle distribution imbalances[33]. Experimental evaluations validate that balanced binary angular margin losses enable competitive performance on benchmark datasets, suggesting that attention to feature distribution properties provides valuable improvements for semi-supervised settings.

### Active Learning for Multi-Label Scenarios

Active learning strategies reduce annotation costs by selectively querying the most informative samples for labeling. A deep active learning method for multi-label text classification based on Bayesian deep learning and expected confidence (BEAL) proposes novel acquisition functions measuring model uncertainty across all labels simultaneously[23]. Implementing Bayesian uncertainty estimation through variational inference enables calibrated confidence measures, with BEAL requiring substantially fewer labeled samples to achieve convergence performance—for example, 64% and 40% of data on AAPD and StackOverflow datasets respectively[23]. The batch-mode active learning framework facilitates effective training of deep models requiring substantial labeled data, establishing that uncertainty-based acquisition functions transfer effectively to multi-label settings when properly accounting for label interdependencies[23].

## Multi-View and Multi-Modal Multi-Label Learning

### Incomplete Multi-View Multi-Label Learning

Real-world data frequently exhibits incomplete multi-view characteristics where certain modalities remain absent for some instances, combined with incomplete label annotations. A disentangled representation and label semantic embedding approach (DRLS) for incomplete multi-view multi-label learning effectively utilizes consistent and specific information from different data sources through disentangled representation learning[46]. The framework minimizes variational upper bounds of mutual information between consistent and specific features, enabling effective separation of view-shared versus view-specific information[46]. Graph neural network-based label semantic embedding learning simultaneously aggregates embeddings of related labels while maintaining label relevance topology in semantic space[46]. Experimental evaluations across five publicly available datasets demonstrate leading performance on both complete and arbitrarily missing data scenarios[46]. The disentanglement perspective extends naturally to multi-label scenarios, suggesting that separation of shared and specific information substantially improves handling of incomplete data.

### Multi-Modal Contextual Prompt Learning

Multi-modal approaches leveraging both images and text leverage complementary information sources for improved classification. Multi-modal contextual prompt learning for multi-label classification with partial labels integrates image and text information through prompt learning mechanisms adapted to CLIP models[40]. The framework better utilizes image-label correspondence within CLIP architectures, adapting vision-language models to handle the specific challenges of partial label scenarios in multi-modal settings[40]. This approach establishes that prompt-based adaptation of foundation models provides effective mechanisms for multi-modal multi-label classification, though limited details remain available regarding specific implementation strategies.

## Experimental Evaluation Methodologies and Benchmarking

Contemporary multi-label classification research employs increasingly sophisticated evaluation frameworks beyond traditional metrics including Hamming loss and subset accuracy. Precision at k (P@k) and propensity-scored precision (PSP@k) metrics provide more nuanced evaluation particularly relevant for imbalanced label distributions where standard accuracy metrics prove inadequate[31]. Macro-averaged and micro-averaged variants of precision, recall, and F1-score enable distinction between performance on majority versus minority classes, with macro-averaging providing clearer insights into tail class performance[2][9]. Average Precision and Area Under Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve metrics accommodate the probabilistic nature of multi-label classification, providing insights into ranking quality across confidence thresholds[46].

Statistical significance testing through two-tailed t-tests with reported p-values establishes rigorous validation that proposed methods achieve non-trivial improvements[2]. Cross-validation protocols across multiple random splits ensure robust performance estimation, with particular emphasis on patient-level or document-level splits in sensitive applications preventing data leakage across training and testing sets[3][23]. Ablation studies systematically evaluate component contributions, establishing which architectural or loss function modifications drive performance improvements[2][3]. Comparative evaluation against appropriate baselines remains critical, with many recent papers comparing against methods published within the preceding three-to-five years rather than relying solely on standard classical approaches[2][19].

## Integration of Knowledge Graphs and Semantic Information

### Domain Knowledge Graphs for Classification Enhancement

Knowledge graph integration substantially enhances multi-label classification by incorporating structured semantic relationships. The hierarchical multi-label classification model for science and technology news exemplifies knowledge graph integration by incorporating prior knowledge from domain-specific knowledge graphs to address data scarcity while improving hierarchical understanding[2]. Graph construction mechanisms extracting semantic information from domain knowledge enable models to leverage relationships between concepts, improving performance on classification tasks requiring fine-grained understanding[2]. These successes suggest broader potential for knowledge graph integration across diverse multi-label classification domains, particularly where labeled training data remains limited.

### Zero-Shot and Few-Shot Settings with Semantic Guidance

Zero-shot multi-label classification approaches the challenging problem of predicting labels unseen during training, requiring systems to reason about semantic relationships between known and unknown labels. A hybrid zero-shot approach to multi-label classification combining k-NN retrieval with large language model refinement achieves macro F1-score of 0.661, surpassing the nearest competitor by 20% on hierarchical multi-label classification tasks[26]. The two-stage pipeline retrieves candidate labels using k-NN indices based on sentence embeddings before applying LLMs to refine predictions while enforcing hierarchy constraints, leveraging both local similarity-based retrieval and global reasoning capabilities[26]. This hybrid approach establishes that combining classical retrieval-based methods with modern language model reasoning provides effective solutions for zero-shot multi-label scenarios, particularly when hierarchical consistency constraints guide final predictions.

## Conclusion: Synthesis and Future Directions

The survey of recent multi-label learning systems from 2022 to 2025 reveals a field experiencing substantial methodological evolution driven by multiple converging trends. First, researchers increasingly recognize that label correlations represent critical information for improving multi-label classification, moving beyond naive binary relevance approaches toward sophisticated graph-based and contrastive learning mechanisms that explicitly capture label dependencies. Second, the adaptation of large language models and vision-language foundation models to multi-label settings has introduced new paradigms for leveraging semantic information and enabling few-shot and zero-shot classification with minimal annotation effort. Third, addressing practical challenges including long-tail distributions, partial annotations, and noisy labels has motivated specialized loss functions, curriculum learning strategies, and robustness-enhancing mechanisms that acknowledge real-world data characteristics rather than assuming clean, balanced datasets.

Looking toward 2025 and beyond, several critical research directions merit particular attention. The integration of **extremely large foundation models** into multi-label classification frameworks remains largely unexplored, with few studies investigating how billions-parameter decoder-only language models influence multi-label performance characteristics compared to smaller encoder-only approaches. Research suggests that decoder-only models may offer complementary advantages to specialized classifier architectures, yet computational efficiency concerns necessitate investigation of parameter-efficient fine-tuning approaches such as LoRA and prompt-based adaptation. Second, the **principled handling of implicit label correlations through game-theoretic and information-theoretic frameworks** represents an emerging frontier, with preliminary evidence suggesting that viewing multi-label learning as equilibrium problems or information-theoretic optimization tasks yields conceptual insights and algorithmic innovations beyond conventional approaches. Third, **multi-modal and cross-modal multi-label classification** incorporating heterogeneous data sources remains relatively underdeveloped despite obvious practical applications, with future work likely exploring how to effectively fuse information from text, images, audio, and other modalities for multi-label prediction. Fourth, the development of **robust and efficient algorithms for extreme multi-label classification at billion-label scales** will become increasingly important as practical applications emerge requiring predictions across unprecedented label spaces, necessitating innovations in indexing, quantization, and approximation methods that maintain accuracy while achieving computational tractability.

The field has transitioned from viewing multi-label classification as a straightforward extension of single-label methods toward recognizing it as a distinct paradigm with unique mathematical and computational challenges. Label correlation, representation disentanglement, and long-tail distribution handling have emerged as fundamental research problems requiring specialized attention rather than applying generic multi-class classification techniques. The increasing sophistication of evaluation frameworks and benchmark datasets, exemplified by new multi-label datasets for biomedical, legal, and specialized domain applications, enables more rigorous assessment of algorithmic innovations. Looking forward, the integration of theoretical understanding regarding label interdependencies with practical considerations of scalability, noise robustness, and annotation efficiency will likely characterize the next wave of multi-label learning research, establishing foundations for addressing the most challenging real-world classification problems where single labels prove fundamentally inadequate for capturing instance complexity.
