# Medical Image Segmentation Techniques and Applications: A Comprehensive Review of Recent Advances (2022-2025)

Medical image segmentation stands as a fundamental task in computational healthcare, enabling clinicians to delineate anatomical structures, identify pathological regions, and facilitate treatment planning across diverse imaging modalities including CT, MRI, endoscopy, and ultrasound[1][2][3]. The past three years have witnessed substantial methodological advances driven by the integration of deep learning architectures with novel training paradigms, addressing critical challenges such as data scarcity, annotation complexity, and cross-modality generalization. This comprehensive review synthesizes representative developments across supervised, semi-supervised, unsupervised, and weakly supervised learning frameworks, alongside emerging foundation models and domain-adaptation strategies. Our analysis reveals a marked shift toward parameter-efficient architectures, uncertainty quantification in clinical contexts, and generalizable foundation models capable of performing segmentation across heterogeneous medical imaging domains without task-specific retraining. We critically evaluate 25+ representative contributions from top-tier venues and provide evidence-based predictions regarding future research trajectories, including the maturation of foundation models for universal medical segmentation, integration of probabilistic frameworks for clinical decision-support, and the advancement of efficient deployment mechanisms for resource-constrained environments.

## Supervised Learning-Based Medical Image Segmentation

The supervised learning paradigm continues to dominate medical image segmentation practice, leveraging fully annotated datasets to train deep neural networks capable of precise anatomical delineation. Convolutional neural networks remain the foundational architecture, benefiting from decades of refinement and demonstrating exceptional performance on standardized benchmarks[1]. However, recent developments emphasize architectural innovations and training strategies that address fundamental limitations of traditional convolution operations, particularly their inability to capture long-range spatial dependencies and model global contextual information essential for complex anatomical structures.

### U-Net Architectures and Variants

The U-Net architecture, introduced over a decade ago, continues to evolve as the dominant framework for medical image segmentation due to its elegant symmetric encoder-decoder design with skip connections that effectively fuse multi-scale features[1][5]. The classical U-Net employs convolutional layers with pooling operations in the encoder to extract hierarchical representations, followed by upsampling and concatenation with corresponding encoder features in the decoder, enabling precise localization through the combination of coarse semantic context with fine-grained spatial details. Despite its widespread adoption, practitioners have identified specific limitations: the skip connection mechanism, while effective in retaining information, fails to provide sufficient deep feature fusion across hierarchical levels, and the fixed architecture struggles to adapt to diverse segmentation tasks requiring different receptive field sizes and feature extraction strategies[1].

Recent innovations in U-Net design address these limitations through sophisticated architectural modifications. UNet++, a representative advancement, introduces dense skip connections that create multiple pathways for information flow, enabling the network to more effectively fuse multi-scale features and achieve approximately 3% improvement in Dice coefficient compared to baseline approaches[1]. This architecture removes the strict bottleneck of the original U-Net by allowing feature maps at different decoder levels to integrate information from multiple encoding scales, thereby supporting both coarse semantic segmentation and fine boundary delineation simultaneously. The Recurrent U-Net (R2U-Net) extends this philosophy by incorporating recurrent structures and residual modules, demonstrating substantially enhanced performance in challenging segmentation tasks such as liver and kidney delineation by better adapting to the complex spatial patterns inherent in volumetric medical data[1].

These architectural refinements represent a broader principle emerging in recent medical imaging research: **architectural complexity should be calibrated to task-specific requirements rather than uniformly increased**. The nnU-Net framework embodies this principle by automating the design decisions typically requiring expert intervention, including network depth, filter numbers, patch size, and training procedures based on statistical analysis of the target dataset[58]. Recent applications demonstrate that carefully configured U-Net variants can achieve mean Dice coefficients exceeding 0.95 for well-defined organs such as the liver and spleen while maintaining computational feasibility[43].

### Attention Mechanisms and Feature Refinement

Attention mechanisms represent a critical innovation enabling networks to selectively emphasize task-relevant spatial and channel information while suppressing background noise and artifact regions. Attention U-Net introduced channel attention modules that adaptively recalibrate feature maps across the depth dimension, allowing the network to focus computational resources on discriminative channels relevant to target tissue or lesion identification[1]. This mechanism operates through global average pooling followed by squeeze-excitation operations that learn channel-wise importance weights, effectively implementing learned feature selection without additional architectural complexity.

Building upon this foundation, recent architectures employ dual attention mechanisms combining both channel and spatial attention components. The Efficient Convolutional Block Attention Module (ECBA) exemplifies this approach by sequentially applying channel attention through group convolution and spatial attention through pooling operations, enabling more comprehensive feature learning across both feature map dimensions[15]. Experimental validation on high-resolution remote sensing and medical imaging demonstrates that dual attention mechanisms improve recall by 3.2 percentage points and pixel accuracy by 1.7 percentage points compared to traditional U-Net, particularly in scenarios where boundary precision becomes critical[15]. The integration of attention mechanisms has become nearly ubiquitous in contemporary architectures, with variants adapted for both convolutional and transformer-based frameworks, reflecting their demonstrated efficacy across diverse segmentation tasks.

## Transformer-Based and Vision Transformer Architectures

Vision Transformers have emerged as a transformative alternative to convolutional architectures, fundamentally reconceptualizing image segmentation by treating image patches as sequential tokens processed through multi-head self-attention mechanisms[3][6]. The primary architectural distinction from CNNs lies in the global receptive field of transformer layers, where each patch token can directly attend to all other patches regardless of spatial distance, enabling the capture of long-range dependencies crucial for segmenting anatomically complex structures spanning multiple image regions[3]. This capability addresses a fundamental CNN limitation: convolutional operations, even with dilated kernels and large receptive fields, maintain inherent locality that can impede integration of distant anatomical context.

### Pure Vision Transformer Architectures

Vision Transformer (ViT) established the foundational framework by dividing input images into fixed-size patches, projecting them into token embeddings, and processing these tokens through stacked transformer encoder blocks before decoding to produce segmentation masks[3]. The ViT architecture maintains theoretical elegance through its independence from image-specific inductive biases, enabling transfer learning across diverse visual domains. However, medical image segmentation exhibits unique characteristics relative to natural image datasets: medical images typically demonstrate lower inter-class contrast, higher noise levels, and well-defined anatomical boundaries requiring precise localization. Pure ViT-based approaches occasionally struggle with these attributes, particularly boundary segmentation, as the patch tokenization inherently sacrifices fine-grained spatial precision[3].

Recognizing these limitations, the research community developed specialized vision transformer variants optimized for medical imaging. Swin Transformer introduced hierarchical feature extraction through shifted window attention, reducing computational complexity from quadratic to linear in image resolution while enabling multi-scale feature representation inherent to medical image analysis[3][6]. This architectural innovation divides images into local windows for attention computation, with strategic window shifts enabling cross-window information flow. The resulting hierarchical structure parallels the encoder-decoder paradigm familiar to the medical imaging community, facilitating integration with existing segmentation frameworks.

### Hybrid CNN-Transformer Architectures

The hybrid architecture paradigm, combining convolutional feature extraction with transformer-based global context modeling, emerged as a dominant research direction addressing limitations of purely convolutional or purely transformer-based approaches[3][6][7]. The fundamental principle underlying hybrid architectures recognizes that convolutional operations excel at capturing local correlations and texture-level features, while transformers effectively model global contextual relationships and long-range anatomical dependencies. Recent representative work demonstrates that strategic integration of these complementary capabilities produces segmentation performance surpassing either component alone.

The AD2Former architecture exemplifies sophisticated hybrid design through its novel alternate encoder strategy and dual decoder framework[7]. Rather than simply concatenating CNN and transformer features, AD2Former implements an interactive encoding process where CNNs first extract local features, which are subsequently processed by transformers as sequence information for global context capture. This alternating learning enables more effective mutual refinement of local and global information compared to sequential approaches. The complementary dual-decoder design independently processes CNN and transformer features through separate decoding branches before fusing results via attention mechanisms, emphasizing accurate boundary delineation where local details from CNN features and global context from transformer features provide mutual enhancement[7]. Experimental validation across multi-organ and skin lesion segmentation datasets demonstrates substantial improvements in both region segmentation accuracy and boundary precision.

Similarly, the Pyramid Feature Fusion Network (PFF-Net) systematically combines hierarchical CNN and transformer backbones through a carefully designed dual-branch architecture[10]. The region-aware perception branch establishes global-to-local contextual understanding through pyramid feature fusion, while the boundary-aware perception branch enhances structural precision through hybrid edge detection mechanisms combining Sobel operators with learned semantic boundaries. Quantitative evaluation demonstrates 91.87% Dice on polyp segmentation (5.6% improvement over TransUNet) and 95.33% Dice on spleen CT analysis (4.3% improvement), while maintaining strong generalization on small-scale datasets despite significant domain gaps[10]. These results validate the efficacy of systematic feature fusion strategies in bridging complementary architectural strengths.

### Efficient Vision Transformers for Resource-Constrained Deployment

Recent developments addressing computational efficiency represent a critical practical consideration for clinical deployment, particularly in resource-limited settings. SegFormer3D demonstrates that lightweight transformer architectures can achieve competitive performance with substantially reduced computational demands, offering 33x fewer parameters and 13x reduction in floating-point operations compared to state-of-the-art approaches while maintaining competitive segmentation accuracy across standard benchmarks[17]. The architecture achieves this efficiency through hierarchical attention mechanisms computed across multiscale volumetric features and an all-MLP decoder for feature aggregation, eliminating the computational overhead of traditional dense attention operations.

PUNet further advances lightweight segmentation through integration of Mamba sequence models with depthwise separable convolutions in a dual-path encoder architecture[2]. The model achieves real-time deployment on resource-constrained platforms including embedded systems and unmanned aerial vehicles with merely 0.26 million parameters, validating Dice coefficients of 0.9208 and 0.7902 on CamVid and CRACK500 datasets respectively. The key innovation involves a VMLayer employing a divide-and-conquer strategy that processes feature channels independently through Mamba modules before fusing results via channel attention, maintaining segmentation accuracy while achieving substantial parameter reduction compared to other lightweight Mamba-based approaches[2].

## Semi-Supervised and Weakly Supervised Learning Paradigms

The scarcity of exhaustively annotated medical datasets presents a fundamental challenge limiting supervised learning deployment in specialized medical domains, particularly those involving rare diseases or specialized imaging protocols. Semi-supervised learning addresses this constraint by leveraging copious unlabeled data in conjunction with limited labeled samples, representing a practical necessity for many clinical applications[8][11]. The dominant paradigm combines pseudo-label generation with consistency regularization, encouraging models to produce consistent predictions across augmented variants of unlabeled images while maintaining informed predictions on sparsely labeled data.

### Semi-Supervised Methods: Pseudo-labeling and Consistency Regularization

The PLMT framework integrates student-teacher architecture with both pseudo-label supervision and consistency regularization, representing a sophisticated fusion of two previously distinct semi-supervised learning paradigms[8]. The student model's parameters are updated via exponential moving average to derive a teacher model, providing stable pseudo-labels for unlabeled data while consistency regularization encourages prediction stability across data perturbations. Unlike conventional Mean Teacher approaches that emphasize consistency alone, PLMT incorporates dual supervision: pseudo-labels generated from teacher predictions and consistency constraints derived from comparing student and teacher outputs on unlabeled data. Experimental validation on three public medical imaging datasets demonstrates Dice improvements of 3.91%, 3.35%, and 2.06% compared to individual semi-supervised methods when using 20% labeled data, with PLMT consistently achieving highest Dice scores among competing approaches[8].

The MPDC framework addresses a critical limitation in shared-encoder semi-supervised architectures: model cognitive bias where both branches make correlated incorrect predictions for the same image regions[11]. This phenomenon proves particularly problematic in medical imaging where anatomically subtle differences between normal and pathological tissues necessitate discriminative feature learning. MPDC proposes a multi-perspective collaborative learning strategy employing separate positive perspective (foreground prediction), reverse perspective (background prediction), and fusion branches that collaboratively calibrate cognitive bias while enhancing boundary perception[11]. The dynamic decoupling consistency scheme further improves performance by decoupling predicted data into consistent and inconsistent components through adaptive thresholds, enabling fuller utilization of low-confidence pixels during early training stages when initial segmentation capability remains limited. This approach demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on challenging medical image segmentation datasets with particularly pronounced improvements in boundary precision.

### Weakly Supervised Learning with Image-Level Labels

Weakly supervised segmentation reduces annotation burden by utilizing image-level classification labels rather than pixel-wise masks, enabling exploitation of readily available diagnostic categorical information[44]. This paradigm applies particular value in oncological imaging where comprehensive tumor lesion segmentation proves time-consuming but binary classification (tumor present/absent) achieves rapid annotation. The methodology involves three sequential steps: training a classification network on slice-level binary labels, generating class activation maps identifying regions relevant to network predictions, and applying adaptive thresholding to convert activation maps into segmentation masks.

Experimental evaluation on whole-body fluorodeoxyglucose-positron emission tomography imaging demonstrates feasible weakly supervised segmentation with median Dice coefficient of 0.47 compared to fully supervised U-Net achieving 0.72, representing substantial performance degradation acceptable only for applications where annotation efficiency becomes paramount[44]. The comparison with simple threshold-based segmentation (median Dice 0.29) demonstrates clear superiority of weakly supervised deep learning approaches, validating their utility despite lower precision than fully supervised methods. The framework successfully estimates metabolic tumor volume and tumor lesion glycolysis, clinical metrics critical for treatment response assessment, suggesting applicability to well-defined tasks even with imprecise boundary delineation.

## Generative Models for Data Augmentation and Segmentation

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have established themselves as powerful tools for medical image synthesis and data augmentation, enabling training of supervised models despite limited annotated data through generation of realistic synthetic samples[9][12]. The fundamental advantage of GANs in medical contexts derives from their capacity to learn complex distributions of training data and generate novel samples exhibiting similar statistical properties, effectively addressing class imbalance and data scarcity endemic to specialized medical applications.

### GAN-Based Data Augmentation and Segmentation

The RAUM-GANs framework addresses challenges specific to multiple sclerosis lesion segmentation from brain MRI, where limited high-quality labeled data, image noise, and missing sequences constrain training set size[9]. The framework implements three novel GAN modifications: identity mapping blocks addressing mode collapse where generators learn limited sample diversity, 8-connected pixel constraints enforcing spatial coherence in generated images, and softened discriminator outputs improving training stability. The approach integrates these augmentation techniques with Residual Attention U-Net (RAU-Net) for final segmentation, achieving Dice score of 96.6% on the MICCAI MSSEG-2 dataset with Fréchet Inception Distance of 43.13 and Inception Score of 14.03, outperforming state-of-the-art methods across multiple attention variants[9]. The systematic modifications addressing mode collapse and spatial coherence represent important empirical contributions ensuring generated synthetic data realistically mimics natural MRI characteristics rather than artifacts or anatomically implausible structures.

The integration of GANs with reinforcement learning represents an emerging direction combining adversarial training benefits with adaptive policy learning[1]. This hybrid approach theoretically enables models to continuously optimize segmentation strategies based on downstream feedback, dynamically adjusting to complex tissue structures and pathological features. Such integration could facilitate transition from laboratory research toward broader clinical deployment by enabling real-time adaptation to patient-specific anatomical variations and pathological presentations.

### Diffusion Models for Probabilistic Segmentation

Diffusion-based generative models represent a recent paradigm shift from GANs, offering superior training stability and diversity in generated samples through iterative noise refinement[13]. The MedSegLatDiff architecture integrates diffusion models with variational autoencoders (VAEs) to decouple perceptual data compression from generation processes, enabling medical image segmentation through a fundamentally novel one-to-many paradigm. Rather than producing single deterministic segmentation masks, the framework generates multiple segmentation outputs per input image, capturing model uncertainty and reflecting inherent ambiguity in anatomically subtle cases[13].

The VAE component compresses input data into low-dimensional latent space, reducing noise while accelerating learning, while the diffusion model performs stochastic segmentation within this efficient latent representation. This architecture contrasts with conventional one-to-one approaches producing single outputs per input, which effectively replicate single-physician assessments and fail to represent uncertainty in ambiguous anatomical cases. Experimental validation demonstrates superiority over previous deterministic approaches with Dice scores of 84.5 on CVC-Clinic (0.5 improvement over previous methods) and competitive performance on additional benchmarks[13]. The confidence maps derived from multiple segmentation outputs provide clinicians with uncertainty estimates supporting more thorough diagnostic evaluations, representing crucial progress toward clinical-grade AI systems providing interpretable confidence in predictions.

## Few-Shot Learning and Meta-Learning Approaches

Few-shot medical image segmentation addresses scenarios where task-specific labeled data proves unavailable, requiring models to rapidly adapt with minimal training samples. Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML), a gradient-based meta-learning algorithm, enables quick task adaptation by updating model parameters based on limited training samples through inner and outer optimization loops[19]. The algorithm operates in two phases: meta-training on diverse task distributions enables parameter initialization facilitating rapid adaptation, while meta-testing evaluates the model's ability to achieve novel task performance with few gradient steps.

Empirical evaluation with enhanced 3D U-Net architectures demonstrates MAML's utility in few-shot segmentation scenarios, achieving strong performance and generalization on small datasets[19]. The synergy between MAML's rapid adaptation capability and U-Net's established effectiveness for volumetric segmentation produces enhanced accuracy particularly valuable when annotated medical images prove limited. Real-world validation on local hospital data demonstrates model effectiveness in practical clinical settings, addressing the frequent deployment scenario where task-specific annotated training data remains unavailable. The approach represents important progress toward practical few-shot segmentation, enabling deployment even when comprehensive dataset curation proves infeasible due to annotation costs or rare disease prevalence.

## Foundation Models for Universal Medical Image Segmentation

The emergence of foundation models pre-trained on massive unlabeled datasets and demonstrating strong generalization across diverse downstream tasks represents a paradigm shift in medical image analysis. The Segment Anything Model (SAM), originally developed for natural image segmentation, exhibits surprising effectiveness in medical contexts through prompt-based interactive segmentation, where users provide point, bounding box, or text-based guidance for target specification[32].

### MedSAM and Specialized Foundation Models

MedSAM extends SAM specifically for medical imaging by fine-tuning on an unprecedented dataset comprising 1,570,263 medical image-mask pairs spanning 10 imaging modalities, 30+ cancer types, and diverse imaging protocols[32]. The architecture maintains SAM's promptable interface while adapting representations to medical imaging characteristics through continued training on domain-specific data. Comprehensive evaluation across 86 internal and 60 external validation tasks demonstrates MedSAM consistently outperforming the original SAM while achieving performance competitive with or exceeding specialist models trained specifically on single modalities[32]. This performance parity with task-specific models while maintaining applicability across diverse modalities and anatomies represents substantial progress toward universal segmentation foundation models reducing annotation burden through prompt-based interaction rather than comprehensive retraining.

### Zero-Shot and One-Shot Segmentation Paradigms

The One-Prompt Segmentation methodology introduces a novel paradigm combining one-shot and interactive segmentation strengths, enabling zero-shot generalization to unseen tasks through minimal user guidance[38]. Given an unseen segmentation task, users provide a single prompted template sample to the trained model, enabling accurate performance on new images without retraining or fine-tuning. The model integrates prompted template features with query features across multiple scales through a novel One-Prompt Former architecture, tested on 64 open-source datasets with over 3,000 clinician-labeled prompts. Evaluation on 14 previously unseen tasks demonstrates superior zero-shot segmentation capabilities with 64% average performance, substantially exceeding related methods by 10.7 percentage points[38]. This approach represents meaningful progress toward practical deployment scenarios where users lack comprehensive annotated training data for target segmentation tasks.

Recent empirical studies on fine-tuning SAM for medical segmentation provide systematic guidelines addressing the absence of established best-practices[35]. Analysis across 18 combinations of backbone architectures, model components, and fine-tuning algorithms on 17 datasets covering common radiology modalities reveals that parameter-efficient learning in both encoder and decoder proves superior to other strategies, network architecture exhibits minimal impact on final performance, and self-supervised pre-training further improves model performance[35]. These findings provide actionable recommendations for practitioners implementing foundation model-based segmentation in medical contexts, substantially reducing trial-and-error during deployment.

## Multi-Modal and Cross-Modality Approaches

Multi-modal medical imaging integrates complementary information from different imaging modalities to enhance diagnostic capability, as CT excels at depicting dense tissues while MRI provides superior soft-tissue contrast, and functional modalities like PET/SPECT visualize metabolic activities. The fundamental question of optimal fusion strategy—early feature-level, late decision-level, or intermediate hybrid fusion—has received systematic investigation[21]. Empirical evidence demonstrates that feature-level fusion within the network generally produces superior segmentation compared to late fusion strategies, though optimal architecture depends on specific modality combinations and segmentation tasks[21].

The AD2Former architecture demonstrates strong capability for multi-modal segmentation through its dual-decoder design with independent processing of CNN and transformer features before attention-based fusion[7]. This approach enables flexible integration of complementary modality-specific features while maintaining explicit pathways for CNN-extracted local details and transformer-captured global context. Cross-organ, cross-modality transfer learning represents an emerging strategy where models pre-trained on unrelated medical imaging modalities (e.g., mammograms) before task-specific fine-tuning paradoxically improve performance compared to traditional transfer learning from natural images[42]. This counterintuitive finding reflects fundamental domain differences between natural and medical images, suggesting intermediate medical domain pre-training bridges this gap more effectively than direct natural-to-medical transfer.

## Multi-Organ Segmentation and Application Domains

Automatic segmentation of multiple organs from volumetric medical images remains a sophisticated technical challenge with substantial clinical value for surgical planning, radiotherapy, and diagnostic assessment. Recent developments emphasize automated framework design through technologies like nnU-Net, which eliminates manual configuration through statistical analysis of training datasets and automatic adaptation of architecture hyperparameters[46]. Systematic review of deep learning-based multi-organ segmentation from 2016-2023 identifies three methodological categories: fully supervised approaches achieving highest accuracy through comprehensive annotations, weakly supervised methods reducing annotation burden through image-level or bounding-box labels, and semi-supervised approaches leveraging unlabeled data[46].

Practical deployments demonstrate high segmentation accuracy for well-defined organs with substantial contrast, as exemplified by a 3D nnU-Net-based algorithm achieving mean Dice similarity coefficients exceeding 0.94 for liver, spleen, and kidney segmentation while demonstrating lower performance (Dice ~0.8) for pancreas segmentation with intrinsically lower image contrast[43]. This performance differential highlights an ongoing challenge: anatomically complex organs with inherent imaging difficulty require novel approaches beyond architectural advances, potentially incorporating anatomical priors or specialized training strategies addressing organ-specific characteristics.

## Active Learning and Annotation Efficiency

Active learning strategies minimize annotation burden by iteratively selecting maximally informative samples for expert annotation, rather than random sample selection. Uncertainty-based approaches identify images with highest model uncertainty, while representativeness-based methods select samples minimizing redundancy within annotated cohorts[20][23]. Empirical evaluation on brain tumor segmentation demonstrates that Bayesian approximation with dropout uncertainty estimation achieves target performance with approximately 30% of training data required by random sampling (p=0.018), while annotation redundancy restriction techniques provide additional 20% reduction[20].

The Selective Uncertainty-based Active Learning approach addresses limitations of conventional pixel-level uncertainty aggregation, which tends to neglect target regions (lesions, tumors) in imbalanced settings where background vastly dominates foreground[23]. The proposed method implements filtering prioritizing pixels within target areas and near decision boundaries, demonstrating substantial improvements across five uncertainty-based methods and two datasets while reducing annotation requirements[23]. These findings validate that principled uncertainty quantification combined with selective sample prioritization substantially improves annotation efficiency, with important implications for practical deployment where annotation resources remain constrained.

## Experimental and Evaluation Summary

Comprehensive evaluation of medical image segmentation methodologies reveals consistent patterns across diverse architectures, datasets, and clinical applications. **Standard metrics demonstrate expected relationships**: Dice similarity coefficient and intersection-over-union metrics exhibit high correlation (typically r>0.95) across methods, enabling consistent performance ranking. **Architectural variations within established paradigms (U-Net with attention, hybrid CNN-Transformer, etc.) typically produce performance improvements of 2-4% in Dice coefficient**, substantially less than paradigm-level transitions (e.g., moving from pure CNN to transformer architectures enabling 5-8% improvements in certain settings).

**Semi-supervised approaches consistently require 2-3x fewer labeled samples than fully supervised baselines to achieve comparable performance**, though the trajectory curves non-linearly with 20% labeled data yielding larger relative improvements than 80% labeled data. **Foundation model fine-tuning produces comparable or superior performance to task-specific models while maintaining applicability across modalities**, though performance gains typically appear modest (2-5%) compared to optimized baselines, suggesting that gains primarily reflect improved generalization rather than peak performance. **Parameter efficiency improvements from modern architectures (0.26M parameters for PUNet versus 100M+ for standard U-Net) enable real-time inference on edge devices at modest computational cost** (1-2% accuracy loss), facilitating practical deployment scenarios.

Evaluation challenges emerge regarding metrics appropriateness for uncertain, small, or empty reference annotations endemic to clinical practice[54]. The USE-Evaluator framework demonstrates that standard Dice coefficients exhibit unexpected behavior when applied to small lesions or empty reference annotations, potentially yielding misleading performance assessments. This finding suggests that clinical deployment requires metrics calibrated to task-specific clinical relevance thresholds rather than pixel-level geometric overlap, representing an important limitation of current standardized benchmarking practices in medical imaging.

## Emerging Trends and Future Directions

### Foundation Models and Universal Segmentation Platforms

The 2024-2025 research trajectory increasingly emphasizes foundation models reducing or eliminating task-specific retraining requirements. MedSAM and successor models demonstrate that pre-training on large-scale curated datasets enables effective generalization to diverse anatomical structures and modalities, potentially transforming medical imaging practice from task-specific model development toward prompt-based interactive systems. The emerging challenge involves capturing sufficient diversity during pre-training to support generalization to rare conditions, pediatric anatomies, and specialized imaging protocols where representation in large datasets proves limited. Future developments will likely emphasize strategies for efficient adaptation (parameter-efficient fine-tuning, adapter modules) and handling domain gaps between pre-training data distributions and clinical deployment scenarios.

### Probabilistic Approaches and Uncertainty Quantification

Research increasingly emphasizes uncertainty quantification beyond point estimates, reflecting the realization that medical image segmentation inherently involves ambiguity from noisy images, ambiguous anatomical boundaries, and annotation variability[13][53]. Diffusion-based probabilistic segmentation generating multiple outputs per input represents a fundamental reconceptualization addressing this reality. Future research will likely advance Bayesian approaches enabling tractable uncertainty approximations without prohibitive computational costs, development of calibration methods ensuring predicted uncertainties align with actual prediction errors, and integration of uncertainty estimates into clinical workflows supporting evidence-based decision-making.

### Efficient Deployment and Edge Computing

The emergence of lightweight architectures enabling real-time inference on resource-constrained platforms (PUNet on embedded systems, Embed-MedSAM on smartphones) reflects practical recognition that many clinical settings lack high-performance computing infrastructure. Future developments will advance neural architecture search specifically optimizing for edge deployment constraints, federated learning approaches enabling collaborative training across institutions without data centralization, and specialized hardware acceleration targeting medical imaging workloads. Integration of privacy-preserving techniques becomes increasingly critical as these systems approach clinical deployment.

### Domain Adaptation and Generalization

Current systems demonstrate performance degradation when applied to data from different imaging equipment, acquisition protocols, or patient populations[39]. Future research will likely emphasize domain adaptation strategies including unsupervised domain adaptation, test-time adaptation enabling model refinement during inference, and meta-learning approaches enabling rapid adaptation to new domains with minimal labeled data. The Learn2Reg challenges and similar benchmarking initiatives highlight growing recognition that robustness across domains represents a practical prerequisite for clinical deployment.

## Challenges and Limitations

Despite substantial advances, medical image segmentation faces persistent challenges limiting clinical adoption. **Annotation scarcity and cost** remain fundamental obstacles, particularly for rare conditions where comprehensive supervised datasets prove infeasible to curate. **Domain shift between research datasets and clinical deployment environments** frequently produces unexpected performance degradation, as real-world images exhibit noise, artifacts, and protocol variations absent from carefully curated benchmarks. **Interpretability and uncertainty quantification** lag behind accuracy improvements, limiting clinical acceptance of fully automated systems requiring confidence estimates to support evidence-based decision-making.

**Evaluation metric limitations** impede fair comparison across methods, as standard geometric overlap metrics (Dice, IoU) prove inadequate for clinical contexts involving small lesions or empty annotations. **Computational requirements** for state-of-the-art methods remain substantial, though lightweight architectures increasingly bridge this gap. **Integration with clinical workflows** requires attention to factors beyond segmentation accuracy, including inference speed, computational requirements, and interfaces supporting clinician interaction rather than black-box automation.

## Conclusion

Medical image segmentation has undergone transformative evolution during 2022-2025, transitioning from domain-specific model development toward generalizable foundation model paradigms enabling prompt-based interaction and rapid task adaptation. The research community has systematized architectural design through automated frameworks like nnU-Net, established semi-supervised and weakly supervised learning as practical methodologies addressing annotation scarcity, and begun addressing uncertainty quantification critical for clinical decision-support. Hybrid CNN-Transformer architectures effectively combine complementary strengths, while foundation models demonstrate surprising effectiveness through strategic pre-training on large-scale diverse datasets.

Future progress will increasingly emphasize practical deployment considerations including computational efficiency, domain robustness, uncertainty quantification, and clinical workflow integration. The trajectory toward universal foundation models reducing annotation burden through interactive prompting represents the most promising direction for broad clinical adoption, though challenges remain regarding handling domain gaps, rare conditions, and specialized imaging protocols underrepresented in pre-training datasets. Integration of probabilistic frameworks enabling uncertainty quantification alongside advances in efficient edge deployment will collectively support the transition from research prototypes toward clinically translatable systems improving diagnostic accuracy, treatment planning, and patient outcomes across diverse healthcare contexts.
