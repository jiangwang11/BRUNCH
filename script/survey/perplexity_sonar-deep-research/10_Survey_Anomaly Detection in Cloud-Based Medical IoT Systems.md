# Anomaly Detection in Cloud-Based Medical IoT Systems: A Comprehensive Review of Recent Advances and Applications

This comprehensive review synthesizes recent advances in anomaly detection techniques specifically designed for cloud-based medical Internet of Things (Medical IoT/IoMT) systems from 2022 through 2025. The proliferation of interconnected medical devices generating high-dimensional, real-time patient data has created unprecedented cybersecurity challenges, necessitating sophisticated detection mechanisms that balance detection accuracy with computational efficiency and privacy preservation. Through systematic analysis of deep learning architectures, hybrid ensemble methods, federated learning frameworks, and edge-based detection systems, this review identifies convergence toward physics-informed attention mechanisms, adaptive differential privacy integration, and hierarchical intrusion detection frameworks as defining characteristics of contemporary research. The study reveals that while individual deep learning models achieve extraordinary accuracy metrics (>99%), real-world deployment success increasingly depends on explainability, computational constraints at resource-limited medical devices, and robustness against evolving zero-day attack patterns. This review synthesizes experimental findings across benchmark datasets (CICIoMT2024, UNSW-NB15, NSL-KDD), identifies persistent methodological challenges in class imbalance and false positive management, and delineates emerging research trajectories toward privacy-preserving collaborative learning and real-time edge deployment.

## Introduction: Medical IoT as a Critical Infrastructure Challenge

The integration of Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) devices into cloud-based healthcare infrastructure has fundamentally transformed clinical operations by enabling real-time patient monitoring, remote diagnostics, and automated therapeutic interventions[1][2]. These systems encompass wearable sensors, implantable devices, diagnostic equipment, and therapeutic apparatus that continuously generate multimodal sensor streams encompassing vital signs, biomedical signals, and operational parameters. However, this technological advancement has introduced substantial cybersecurity vulnerabilities that directly impact patient safety and data confidentiality. Unlike traditional enterprise IT systems where security breaches primarily result in financial or reputational damage, compromised medical IoT devices can directly cause harm through modified therapeutic outputs, unauthorized data exfiltration, or system availability disruptions that interrupt critical patient care functions[2]. The distributed, heterogeneous nature of medical IoT environments, combined with resource constraints on edge devices and the high velocity of streamed data, renders traditional signature-based intrusion detection systems ineffective for detecting emerging threats and zero-day attacks.

The transition from on-premise healthcare systems to cloud-based infrastructures introduces additional complexity through multi-tenancy challenges, data residency concerns, and the requirement for continuous monitoring across geographically dispersed endpoints[3]. Cloud environments generate massive volumes of infrastructure logs, network traffic, and application telemetry data that contain behavioral patterns indicative of security incidents. Anomaly detection represents a paradigm shift from reactive, rule-based threat identification toward proactive, data-driven approaches that learn normal behavioral baselines and flag deviations requiring investigation. This shift proves particularly valuable in medical contexts where legitimate operational variations must be distinguished from malicious activities to minimize false positive rates that could overwhelm clinical staff with false alarms and undermine system trustworthiness.

The heterogeneity of medical IoT ecosystems poses fundamental technical challenges distinct from generic IoT security research. Medical devices operate under strict regulatory constraints (HIPAA, GDPR, FDA guidelines) that mandate audit trails, explainability, and documented risk management—requirements that deep "black-box" anomaly detection models struggle to satisfy[2]. Additionally, the high-stakes nature of healthcare applications demands extremely low false negative rates to avoid missing genuine security incidents, yet simultaneously requires manageable false positive rates to maintain operational efficiency. These competing constraints have driven research toward hybrid architectures combining multiple detection modalities, federated learning approaches that preserve data locality, and increasingly sophisticated explainable AI techniques that provide clinicians and security analysts with interpretable decision rationales.

## Methodological Approaches to Anomaly Detection in Cloud-Based Medical IoT

### Deep Learning-Based Approaches

Deep learning has emerged as the dominant paradigm for anomaly detection in medical IoT systems, with autoencoder-based and recurrent neural network architectures proving particularly effective for time-series anomaly identification. The foundational principle underlying these approaches recognizes that normal operational patterns in medical IoT systems exhibit regularities that can be learned through unsupervised training on unlabeled data, whereas anomalous behaviors deviate from these learned distributions in measurable ways.

**Autoencoder and LSTM Hybrid Architectures** represent one of the most extensively studied methodological categories. A recent study introduced a hybrid deep learning method integrating Autoencoders with three encoder-decoder layers for unsupervised feature learning combined with Long Short-Term Memory networks for temporal pattern analysis[1]. The methodology leverages autoencoders to capture reconstruction errors (deviations between input and reconstructed signals), which serve as indicators of anomalous network traffic or device behavior, while LSTM networks simultaneously model temporal dependencies in sequential network flow data. When evaluated on the CICIoMT2024 dataset containing traffic from forty medical IoT devices and eighteen distinct attack types, this hybrid approach achieved an Area Under the Curve (AUC) metric exceeding 99%, substantially outperforming either component architecture in isolation. The integration proves particularly valuable for medical IoT applications because LSTM's capacity to retain information over extended sequences addresses the temporal nature of sophisticated attacks that unfold gradually through multiple compromised devices or coordinated botnet activities rather than manifesting as instantaneous point anomalies.

An alternative architectural instantiation demonstrated the effectiveness of LSTM-based deep stacked sequence-to-sequence autoencoders for health status prediction in confined environments, wherein workers' vital signs collected through wearable sensors were monitored for anomalies indicative of occupational hazards[17]. This approach specifically addressed the challenge of limited abnormal training data through hybrid integration with one-class Support Vector Machines, enabling anomaly detection purely through normal pattern learning without requiring exposure to malicious examples during training. The framework achieved particular utility in detecting subtle physiological deviations that early-stage occupational exposures might trigger before overt adverse events manifest, demonstrating that temporal sequence models effectively capture the multi-step progression of both security breaches and health emergencies in constrained medical environments.

**Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-Based Detection Systems** have demonstrated exceptional performance for hierarchical feature extraction from network traffic and time-series medical data. A CNN-based approach evaluated on the CICIoMT2024 dataset achieved near-perfect accuracy of 99% in binary classification tasks (benign versus attack), with precision, recall, and F1-score metrics all exceeding 0.91[10]. CNNs excel at learning hierarchical representations of network traffic patterns through convolutional filters that progressively abstract low-level packet characteristics into high-level attack signatures. The key advantage over fully connected architectures lies in CNN's parameter efficiency—convolutional filters share weights across spatial positions, dramatically reducing trainable parameters while improving generalization to unseen traffic patterns. For medical IoT applications, CNNs prove particularly valuable because network traffic naturally exhibits local structure and translational invariance properties that convolutional operations are specifically designed to exploit. However, the research identified that while CNNs achieve superior single-model performance on standard benchmarks, they occasionally struggle with multi-class attack differentiation when attack types exhibit similar network traffic signatures, suggesting that ensemble approaches combining CNNs with complementary architectures often prove more robust.

**Graph Neural Networks for Traffic Pattern Analysis** represent an emerging direction recognizing that medical IoT network topology and inter-device communication patterns encode information distinct from individual packet-level features. A Graph Attention Network (GAT) based approach transformed network traffic data into graph structures where nodes represent devices and edges represent communication flows, enabling the model to learn both node-level features and the relational structure of device communications[21]. When evaluated on the NSL-KDD benchmark dataset, this approach achieved 98% accuracy for binary classification and 99.20% for multi-class attack categorization, with particular strength in identifying distributed attacks coordinated across multiple compromised devices. The graph-based formulation proves intuitive for medical IoT because healthcare facilities naturally organize devices into communication topologies reflecting clinical workflows, sensor networks in patient wards, and inter-system data exchanges. Graph representations explicitly capture these dependencies, enabling detection of distributed attacks that might appear benign when individual devices are analyzed in isolation but become anomalous when considering device communication patterns.

### Hybrid Ensemble and Evolutionary Optimization Approaches

Recognition that no single architecture uniformly dominates across all threat types and operational contexts has catalyzed development of sophisticated ensemble methodologies combining multiple detection algorithms with weighted voting or stacking-based aggregation.

**Ensemble Learning with Gradient Boosting** frameworks have demonstrated particular effectiveness by sequentially training base models where each successor corrects errors of predecessor models. A recent study proposed the EIoHTSCD-SEGO technique combining ensemble deep learning with Serial Exponential Golf Optimization for parameter tuning, specifically designed for Internet of Health Things security[31]. The methodology applied bidirectional LSTM networks alongside conventional ensemble classifiers including Random Forests and Gradient Boosting, achieving significant improvements in precision, recall, and F1-score metrics. The evolutionary optimization component proved particularly valuable for medical IoT applications because hyperparameter selection dramatically influences detection performance yet defies closed-form optimization solutions. By incorporating Serial Exponential Golf Optimization, the framework automatically explored hyperparameter configurations that balanced false positive and false negative rates according to clinical risk profiles rather than generic optimization objectives.

**Exhaustive Feature Selection with Least Squares Support Vector Machines** represents an alternative ensemble direction emphasizing transparent feature importance and computational efficiency. A comprehensive evaluation across three major intrusion detection benchmarks (NSL-KDD, CIC-IDS2017, UNSW-NB15) demonstrated that exhaustive feature selection combined with LS-SVM classifiers achieved 99.3%, 99.5%, and 93.3% accuracy respectively, with training times substantially shorter than competing approaches[33]. The exhaustive feature selection methodology systematically evaluates all possible feature subsets to identify optimal feature combinations, ensuring that every dimension contributing to detection performance can be explicitly documented and validated—a critical requirement for medical IoT systems operating under regulatory oversight. While computationally more intensive than greedy feature selection approaches, exhaustive methods provide certainty that no superior feature combinations were overlooked, particularly important in high-stakes medical environments.

### Federated Learning for Privacy-Preserving Collaborative Detection

The sensitive nature of medical data, combined with regulatory requirements for data locality and institutional autonomy, has motivated development of federated learning frameworks where model training occurs at distributed sites without centralizing patient data.

**Federated Learning with Differential Privacy** integrates mathematical privacy guarantees directly into the collaborative learning process by adding calibrated noise to model updates prior to transmission to central aggregators[8][41]. A novel framework combined federated learning with adaptive differential privacy, achieving 92.5% accuracy while reducing privacy loss by 85% relative to baseline methods, with membership inference attack success rates reduced to 13%[38]. The dual-layer privacy protection mechanism applied local differential privacy at individual healthcare institutions before central aggregation, dynamically adjusting privacy budgets based on data sensitivity and training progress. For medical IoT systems, federated learning proves invaluable because individual hospitals or healthcare networks can train collaborative models leveraging data from multiple institutions without exposing patient-level records to other parties, addressing both regulatory compliance and institutional data governance concerns while benefiting from the improved model generalization that larger, more diverse training datasets provide.

The federated learning framework specifically addresses challenges unique to healthcare data, including non-independent-and-identically-distributed (non-IID) data where different institutions serve patient populations with differing disease prevalences and demographic characteristics[8]. Traditional federated approaches often struggle with this distributional shift, leading to models that perform well on aggregated benchmarks but fail in specific institutional contexts. Adaptive differential privacy allocation allows institutions with greater privacy concerns or particularly sensitive data to contribute to model training while maintaining stricter privacy guarantees than those with less sensitive information, enabling fine-grained privacy-utility trade-offs.

## Evaluation Frameworks and Experimental Methodologies

### Benchmark Datasets and Standardized Evaluation Protocols

The establishment of standardized benchmark datasets has proven essential for comparing methodological approaches across disparate research groups and ensuring reproducibility. The **CICIoMT2024 dataset** represents the most recent IoMT-specific benchmark, comprising traffic from forty medical IoT devices across eighteen distinct attack categories including DDoS, reconnaissance, exploitation, and data exfiltration scenarios[1][10]. This dataset addresses a critical gap in prior benchmarks, which predominantly featured generic network traffic unrelated to medical device communication patterns. The CICIoMT2024 dataset captures healthcare-specific traffic characteristics including DICOM medical imaging protocols, HL7 health information exchange formats, and real-time vital sign transmissions, enabling evaluation of detection algorithms under conditions reflecting authentic clinical environments.

The **UNSW-NB15 dataset**, while not medical-specific, remains widely utilized for foundational algorithm evaluation[36]. This benchmark contains 2.54 million network traffic records across nine attack categories (Fuzzers, Analysis, Backdoors, DoS, Exploits, Generic, Reconnaissance, Shellcode, Worms) captured in a controlled cyber range environment using the IXIA PerfectStorm tool. The dataset's comprehensiveness and public availability have enabled meta-analyses comparing dozens of detection algorithms under controlled conditions, providing reliable estimates of relative algorithmic performance.

The **NSL-KDD and CIC-IDS2017 datasets** provide complementary evaluation perspectives, with NSL-KDD addressing limitations in the older KDD99 dataset through removal of redundant records and stratified train-test splits[33]. These datasets enable assessment of algorithm generalization across different threat landscapes and temporal distribution shifts.

### Performance Metrics and Class Imbalance Considerations

Standard evaluation metrics for anomaly detection include **Precision** (proportion of flagged instances that are genuine anomalies), **Recall** (proportion of actual anomalies successfully detected), **F1-score** (harmonic mean balancing precision and recall), and **Area Under Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC)** (measuring discrimination across classification thresholds). However, these metrics exhibit critical limitations in highly imbalanced settings where anomalies represent tiny fractions (<1%) of operational data, as occurs in most medical IoT deployments where normal operation vastly outnumbers security incidents[57]. In such scenarios, precision-recall curves (PR-AUC) provide more informative performance characterization than ROC curves because they focus specifically on the minority anomaly class.

Recent meta-analyses benchmarking thirty anomaly detection algorithms across diverse datasets revealed that most well-known methods achieve strong performance only when anomalies constitute <10% of data; performance degrades substantially with higher anomaly prevalence[57]. This finding carries direct implications for medical IoT deployment because simulated datasets intentionally over-represent attack traffic to ensure sufficient examples for algorithm training, whereas operational systems experience far lower true anomaly rates. Consequently, algorithms optimized on benchmark datasets may exhibit unexpectedly high false positive rates when deployed in production environments.

**Synthetic data augmentation through Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) and variants** addresses class imbalance by generating synthetic examples of the minority class through interpolation between existing instances[45][48]. Comprehensive evaluation across medical imaging anomaly detection tasks demonstrated that SMOTE integration with deep learning architectures substantially improved sensitivity (recall) and specificity while maintaining or improving accuracy[45]. In medical contexts, this translates to detecting more genuine anomalies while reducing false positives—precisely the performance profile required for clinical acceptability.

### Computational Efficiency and Edge Deployment Constraints

Practical deployment of anomaly detection systems in medical IoT environments confronts stringent computational constraints. **Lightweight signal processing combined with shallow machine learning models** enables real-time detection at edge devices with minimal latency, memory consumption, and energy requirements[39]. A framework integrating Fourier and Wavelet transforms for feature extraction with shallow neural networks, quantized deep learning models, or decision trees achieved F1-scores near 0.94 while reducing memory footprint by 75% and energy consumption by 60% relative to full-precision deep models. Decision Tree classifiers specifically maintained sub-millisecond latency with minimal memory usage, though at the cost of somewhat reduced recall. This performance-efficiency trade-off proves critical for medical IoT deployment because wearable sensors and implantable devices operate on battery power measured in months or years; algorithms requiring excessive computation drain batteries rapidly, making frequent device replacement impractical.

## Specialized Applications and Domain-Specific Challenges

### ECG Signal Anomaly Detection in Cloud-Based Monitoring

Electrocardiogram (ECG) signals represent prototypical medical data streams amenable to cloud-based anomaly detection. A comprehensive study implemented end-to-end AWS cloud infrastructure combining raw ECG data acquisition, preprocessing with Butterworth filters and wavelet transforms for noise reduction, and deep LSTM autoencoders for anomaly identification[3]. The architecture achieved 98% accuracy in distinguishing normal ECG patterns from pathological anomalies including arrhythmias, myocardial infarction indicators, and conduction abnormalities. The system's key innovation involved reconstructing input ECG signals and calculating reconstruction error thresholds to classify beats as normal or anomalous—when reconstruction error exceeds learned thresholds, the system flags the corresponding ECG segment as anomalous. The integration of wavelet time-scattering feature extraction reduced signal dimensionality while preserving diagnostically relevant information, enabling real-time processing on resource-constrained edge devices despite the streaming continuous nature of ECG data.

This application domain exemplifies broader principles applicable to other physiological signal streams (EEG, EMG, respiration, oxygen saturation) wherein anomalies manifest as deviations from learned normal signal characteristics rather than discrete categorical attacks. The success of LSTM autoencoders for this application reflects the algorithms' particular suitability for time-series data exhibiting complex, nonlinear temporal dependencies that traditional statistical methods (e.g., Z-score thresholding) fail to capture.

### Healthcare Provider Fraud Detection

Beyond device-level cybersecurity, medical IoT systems increasingly support fraud detection through anomalous healthcare provider billing patterns and clinical documentation practices. A generative adversarial network (GAN)-based framework trained exclusively on normal provider behavior identified anomalous billing patterns indicative of fraudulent claims or inappropriate resource utilization[28]. The discriminator network distinguished between real provider transaction patterns and generator-produced synthetic examples, with reconstruction error from the generator quantifying the degree of deviation from normal operational patterns. Logistic regression and XGBoost classifiers applied to GAN-derived anomaly scores achieved AUC metrics of 0.757 to 0.974 depending on dataset, with sensitivity rates exceeding 99% on targeted fraud detection tasks. This approach proves particularly valuable for medical IoT systems because fraudulent activities often involve sophisticated obfuscation techniques that evade signature-based detection, yet deviate measurably from aggregate statistical patterns of normal provider behavior.

### Real-Time Anomaly Detection in Smart Health Systems

Smart health environments within smart cities integrate 5G connectivity, distributed IoT devices, wearable sensors, and cloud-based data analytics to enable real-time clinical decision support and emergency response optimization. An **unsupervised anomaly detection approach for electronic health record (EHR) access patterns** using Isolation Forest and Local Outlier Factor algorithms identified insider threats wherein users accessed patient records inconsistent with their clinical role or specific patient care responsibilities[46][52]. The study evaluated Isolation Forest with three variant classifiers (SVM, Decision Tree, Random Forest) alongside Local Outlier Factor variants, revealing that Isolation Forest combined with SVM achieved 99.21% accuracy on real EHR data from a North England hospital. The methodology's particular value stemmed from identifying contextual anomalies undetected by baseline approaches—for example, a discharge date appearing anomalous when analyzed in combination with other patient attributes despite appearing individually unremarkable. Cross-correlation feature selection revealed that previous approaches had overlooked this diagnostic feature entirely, illustrating how domain-specific insight combined with systematic feature engineering can substantially improve anomaly detection even when algorithmic complexity remains modest.

## Contemporary Challenges and Emerging Directions

### Zero-Day Attack Detection and Concept Drift

One of the most persistent challenges in medical IoT security involves detecting zero-day attacks that exploit previously unknown vulnerabilities for which no training examples exist. A **hierarchical intrusion detection system leveraging meta-learning and one-class classification** addressed this challenge through unsupervised learning at Near Edge nodes (smartphones, PDAs) and Far Edge layers (edge computing infrastructure) using the usfAD algorithm for one-class classification[32]. The framework eliminated the requirement for extensive labeled abnormal examples by training models to recognize normal behavior patterns, flagging any deviation as potentially anomalous. Meta-learning enabled rapid adaptation to new device types and operational contexts through few-shot learning, achieving high detection accuracy despite minimal training data. Evaluation on the CICIoMT2024 dataset demonstrated that this hierarchical approach effectively identified both known attacks present in training data and zero-day attacks absent from training—the framework flagged unfamiliar attack patterns as suspicious rather than misclassifying them as normal, a critical distinction for security applications.

The concept drift challenge—wherein normal operational behavior evolves gradually over time as medical facilities upgrade equipment, modify clinical protocols, or adapt to seasonal variations—necessitates continuous model retraining and adaptive threshold adjustment[53]. Traditional static anomaly detection thresholds rapidly become obsolete as normal operations shift, leading to either increasing false positives (as thresholds remain fixed despite evolving baseline patterns) or increasing false negatives (as thresholds are manually adjusted conservatively). Emerging approaches employing **adaptive threshold mechanisms** and **online learning algorithms** that incrementally update models as new data arrives address this challenge more effectively than batch retraining on accumulated historical data.

### Explainability and Clinical Trust

The regulatory environment surrounding medical devices increasingly mandates transparency regarding automated decision-making through requirements for explainability and human oversight. A comprehensive framework for explainable anomaly detection in time-series data integrated extreme gradient boosting, decision trees, and LSTM autoencoders with multiple explainable AI (XAI) techniques including SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), LIME (Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations), and DiCE (Diverse Counterfactual Explanations)[34]. The methodology enabled security analysts and clinicians to understand which features contributed to specific anomaly classifications and how local perturbations to input data would alter model predictions. Quantitative evaluation using Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) ranked feature importance across different input instances, validating that explanations captured the most discriminative data dimensions. For medical IoT applications, explainability proves essential not merely for regulatory compliance but for clinical acceptance—security analysts will not trust anomaly detection systems flagging incidents without understanding the underlying evidential basis, and clinicians will not tolerate security responses interrupting patient care based on opaque algorithmic decisions.

The tension between model complexity and explainability manifests clearly in anomaly detection: more sophisticated architectures typically achieve higher detection accuracy but often lack transparent decision-making processes. Simple models like decision trees provide inherent interpretability but may sacrifice detection performance. Recent advances toward **hybrid architectures combining high-accuracy deep learning components with post-hoc explainability techniques** and **physics-informed models encoding domain knowledge directly into architecture design** suggest promising paths toward reconciling this fundamental trade-off.

### Handling Multivariate, High-Dimensional Data

Modern medical IoT systems generate multivariate time-series data encompassing dozens or hundreds of variables simultaneously—heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, respiratory rate, temperature, medication infusion rates, ventilator settings, and equipment operational parameters all streaming continuously. Traditional anomaly detection approaches designed for univariate signals struggle with high-dimensional data where subtle coordinated deviations across variables indicate anomalies while individual variables remain within normal ranges. An **inverted Transformer architecture with multivariate memory gates** specifically addressed this challenge by assigning dedicated memory modules to each variable, enabling the model to learn variable-specific normal operational patterns while simultaneously capturing cross-variable dependencies[37]. The framework achieved 96.3% accuracy on structural health monitoring applications using only 20% labeled data, demonstrating its utility for medical IoT scenarios where labeled anomalies are scarce. The key architectural innovation involved inverted token embedding, encoding time-step data for each variable independently before attention mechanisms unified information across variables—this design choice elegantly handles the observation that different physiological variables exhibit distinct normal ranges, variability patterns, and dependencies on temporal context.

## Synthesis of Experimental Findings and Comparative Performance

Across the diverse methodological approaches and application domains reviewed, several consistent empirical patterns emerge from experimental evaluations:

**Deep learning architectures substantially outperform classical machine learning methods on benchmark datasets**, with hybrid approaches combining autoencoders, LSTM networks, and attention mechanisms achieving 99%+ accuracy on standard intrusion detection benchmarks. However, this performance advantage often diminishes in production deployments where training data distributions diverge from operational reality due to dataset shift, concept drift, and the specific hardware constraints of medical IoT environments.

**Ensemble methods combining multiple architectures consistently achieve more robust performance across diverse threat types than individual models**, suggesting that no single architecture captures all discriminative patterns necessary for comprehensive threat detection. The complementary strengths of different architectures—CNNs excelling at spatial feature extraction, LSTMs capturing temporal patterns, autoencoders learning latent representations, attention mechanisms highlighting critical features—imply that ensemble approaches leverage these complementary capabilities effectively.

**Class imbalance remains a persistent practical challenge**, with algorithms achieving excellent recall (detecting 99%+ of genuine anomalies in benchmark scenarios) but unacceptably high false positive rates when deployed operationally. This suggests that benchmark dataset evaluations substantially underestimate false positive burdens in real deployments, where anomalies constitute <0.1% of data compared to 1-10% in benchmark datasets.

**Computational efficiency and edge deployment capabilities increasingly differentiate research approaches**, with production-viable systems requiring architectural innovations enabling real-time detection on resource-constrained devices without sacrificing detection accuracy. Lightweight signal processing combined with shallow neural networks, quantization techniques for model compression, and pruning strategies reducing parameter counts all prove essential for practical medical IoT deployment.

**Privacy-preserving techniques through federated learning and differential privacy introduce measurable but acceptable accuracy trade-offs**, typically reducing accuracy by 1-3% while providing robust privacy guarantees quantifiable through membership inference attack resilience and formal differential privacy bounds.

## Research Trends and Emerging Directions for 2025 and Beyond

### Physics-Informed Anomaly Detection

A significant emerging direction involves embedding domain knowledge from medical science and engineering principles directly into anomaly detection architectures rather than treating detection as a purely data-driven machine learning problem. The **Pi-Transformer framework** exemplifies this approach by combining data-driven series attention with physics-informed prior attention encoding expected temporal relationships and scale-related self-similarity patterns[40]. The framework achieved state-of-the-art performance on timing-sensitive anomalies while remaining interpretable—the dual-pathway attention mechanism provides a structured reference point that distinguishes genuine disruptions from harmless variability based on physical principles rather than empirical pattern matching alone. For medical IoT, this direction suggests embedding physiological constraints (e.g., plausible ranges for vital signs, realistic rate-of-change limitations, interdependencies among variables reflecting known medical physiology) directly into anomaly detection architectures, potentially substantially improving generalization to novel operational contexts and out-of-distribution scenarios.

### Adaptive Anomaly Detection with Continuous Learning

Static models trained on historical data rapidly become obsolete as medical facilities upgrade equipment, modify operational procedures, and encounter novel attack patterns. **Adaptive threshold mechanisms, online learning algorithms enabling continuous model updates as new data arrives, and meta-learning approaches supporting rapid adaptation to new contexts** represent the emerging research direction addressing this challenge. These techniques maintain detection performance despite concept drift, equipment changes, and evolving threat landscapes without requiring full retraining on accumulated historical data—a practical necessity for systems requiring high availability without extended maintenance windows.

### Hierarchical and Edge-Distributed Detection Architectures

Recognition that centralized cloud-based detection introduces latency, bandwidth overhead, and privacy concerns has motivated development of **hierarchical intrusion detection systems distributing detection across multiple infrastructure layers**. Near-edge detection at medical devices themselves catches compromised device behavior immediately without network latency; fog-layer edge computing provides aggregate pattern detection across device clusters within facilities; cloud infrastructure supports global threat intelligence and long-term pattern analysis. This architectural distribution reduces latency, decreases bandwidth consumption by filtering false positives locally before cloud transmission, improves privacy through local data processing, and degrades gracefully if cloud connectivity fails. The hierarchical approach proved particularly valuable for detecting zero-day attacks through one-class classification at edge nodes, enabling rapid detection without central servers.

### Integration with Supply Chain and Third-Party Risk Management

The 2024-2025 healthcare cybersecurity landscape reveals that over 80% of healthcare data breaches occurred at third-party vendors, software services, and business associates rather than at primary healthcare institutions themselves[51]. This finding suggests that anomaly detection research increasingly must address medical IoT systems embedded within complex supply chains involving equipment manufacturers, software vendors, cloud service providers, and healthcare networks. Emerging research directions involve **software bill of materials (SBOM) monitoring for medical device vulnerabilities, supply chain-aware threat modeling, and third-party risk management integration with anomaly detection systems**—ensuring that detection systems monitor not merely operational systems but also the external dependencies and supply chain partners enabling those operations.

## Challenges and Limitations in Current Approaches

Despite substantial methodological advances, significant obstacles impede practical medical IoT anomaly detection deployment:

**Regulatory and Compliance Complexity**: Medical IoT systems operate under FDA regulations, HIPAA privacy requirements, GDPR in European contexts, and institutional policies that constrain which algorithms, data processing techniques, and security mechanisms remain permissible. Many state-of-the-art algorithms involve deep neural networks lacking transparent decision-making processes, creating regulatory friction despite their superior detection performance. The regulatory environment increasingly mandates explainability and documented risk management incompatible with purely empirical "black-box" approaches.

**Data Heterogeneity and Standardization**: Medical IoT systems span devices from multiple manufacturers, communication protocols, and operational standards, with data formats varying substantially across institutions. This heterogeneity complicates development of generalizable anomaly detection approaches—algorithms trained on data from one facility's equipment configurations often perform substantially worse when deployed in different institutional contexts with different device vendors or operational procedures. Standardization efforts remain incomplete despite decades of interoperability initiatives.

**Adaptive Adversaries and Arms Races**: Cybercriminal organizations continuously adapt attack techniques in response to deployed defensive mechanisms. Anomaly detection algorithms trained on known attacks may fail against evolved variations thereof, creating an arms race dynamic wherein attackers modify tactics to evade detection while defensive researchers develop novel detection strategies. Zero-day attack detection remains intrinsically difficult because novel attack patterns lack training examples by definition.

**Operational Acceptance and Alert Fatigue**: Even technically excellent anomaly detection systems fail operationally if security analysts become overwhelmed by alert volumes. High false positive rates cause analysts to habituate to alerts and dismiss genuine warnings ("cry-wolf" phenomenon). Conversely, highly conservative thresholds ensuring low false positive rates necessarily miss some genuine anomalies. Achieving operationally acceptable balance remains challenging and context-dependent.

## Conclusion: Synthesis and Recommendations for Future Research

The medical IoT anomaly detection landscape from 2022-2025 reflects maturation of deep learning approaches toward practical deployment constraints, increasing recognition that no single algorithmic approach universally dominates across all scenarios, and growing integration of privacy-preserving techniques, explainability mechanisms, and hierarchical architectures reflecting operational complexity. Hybrid ensemble methods combining complementary detection modalities consistently outperform individual approaches, suggesting that future advances should emphasize sophisticated aggregation strategies rather than developing fundamentally new individual algorithms.

The convergence toward federated learning frameworks preserving data locality, physics-informed architectures embedding domain knowledge, and hierarchical detection systems distributing intelligence across infrastructure layers represents a significant paradigm shift from centralized cloud-centric approaches toward distributed, privacy-aware, explainable systems designed for operational complexity and regulatory constraints. These architectural directions prove more aligned with healthcare sector requirements than purely algorithmic optimization.

For medical IoT practitioners, evidence supports prioritizing: (1) ensemble approaches combining multiple detection modalities rather than relying on single state-of-the-art algorithms; (2) explicit attention to computational constraints and edge deployment capabilities rather than assuming unlimited cloud resources; (3) integration of explainability and audit trail capabilities from inception rather than as post-hoc additions; (4) continuous validation against production data distributions rather than assuming benchmark dataset performance generalizes; (5) third-party risk management and supply chain visibility alongside direct system monitoring.

Future research should emphasize bridging the persistent gap between benchmark dataset performance and operational deployment effectiveness through more realistic evaluation scenarios, dataset shift quantification, and deployment-in-the-loop evaluation protocols. The field would benefit from standardization efforts on medical IoT traffic benchmarks capturing healthcare-specific communication patterns across diverse facility configurations and device manufacturers. Finally, addressing human factors—alert fatigue, clinician trust, security analyst cognitive load—will likely prove as important as further algorithmic refinement for determining real-world anomaly detection system effectiveness.
