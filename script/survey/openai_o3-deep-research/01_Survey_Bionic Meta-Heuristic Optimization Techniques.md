# Bionic Meta-Heuristic Optimization Techniques (2022–2025)

## Introduction  
Bionic meta-heuristic optimization techniques — also known as bio-inspired meta-heuristics — encompass a wide range of **algorithms inspired by biological organisms and processes**. These algorithms draw from nature’s strategies (e.g. the **swarm intelligence** of insects and birds, the **evolutionary adaptation** of species, or even unique plant behaviors) to solve complex optimization problems. Over the last few decades, they’ve become a cornerstone of computational intelligence research, with **classic examples** including *Genetic Algorithms* (GA), *Particle Swarm Optimization* (PSO), and *Ant Colony Optimization* (ACO). These well-known methods have a long track record and are widely used, with some (like PSO, ACO, and GA) each accumulating over **10,000 publications** by recent counts ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=proposed,art)). This proliferation of proven algorithms has helped solidify bio-inspired optimizers as a **mainstay in many fields** (engineering, logistics, machine learning, etc.), because they excel at finding good solutions for non-linear, high-dimensional, and multi-modal problems when traditional methods (e.g. exact solvers) struggle. 

However, a **major theme from 2022 to 2025** has been the **explosion of new bionic meta-heuristics**. Researchers have introduced numerous new algorithms centered around various creatures or natural phenomena – from insects and **marine animals** to plants and even human social behaviors – all in an effort to design more efficient optimizers. On one hand, this has expanded the toolbox available to tackle challenging problems; on the other hand, it’s created a **“crowded” landscape** of algorithms and raised concerns about redundancy and quality control.  In fact, a recent comprehensive review noted that there’s an *“algorithm explosion”* of metaphor-based heuristics (especially since 2023), which has brought on anxieties about whether all these new algorithms are truly *meaningful innovations* or if some simply reinvent known patterns without robust theoretical grounding  ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=The%20first%20challenge%20is%20the,The%20surge%20in)). In other words, the surge in new bionic meta-heuristics has offered **more options**, but it has also highlighted the importance of careful **benchmarking** and verifying that new ideas offer unique benefits (or other advantages) beyond the existing, well-tested algorithms. 

In this survey, we’ll provide an overview of the **bionic meta-heuristic techniques**, with a focus on developments **from 2022 up to late 2025**. We’ll discuss the main categories and design themes (e.g. swarm-based vs. evolutionary vs. plant-based algorithms), highlight **prominent new algorithms** (especially those that are widely cited or considered important), and review the literature documenting **benchmark performance** and **trends**. By covering both the widely used standard algorithms and the newest entries, we aim to give a **comprehensive picture** that helps researchers understand the state of the field and identify which emerging techniques are most worth further study and application.

## Types of Bionic Meta-Heuristic Algorithms  

### 1. **Swarm Intelligence (Animal/Insect Based)**  
One major class of bionic meta-heuristics is based on **swarm intelligence** – the idea that a group of agents (like animals in a swarm, flock, or colony) can collectively solve problems. These algorithms use **populations** that interact and mimic the self‐organizing behavior seen in nature ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=Algorithm%20%28SSA%29%20,26%2C27)), and they often have templates inspired by specific animals or ecological systems. Classic examples include: 

- **Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO):** A swarm-based algorithm where each agent (particle) adjusts its position in the search space by following a combination of its own best past position and the best position found by its neighbors. PSO was inspired by the coordinated **flocking behavior of birds** and schooling of fish. It remains one of the most popular meta-heuristics in practice (tens of thousands of publications) and is often a strong baseline in optimization tasks ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=proposed,art)). 

- **Ant Colony Optimization (ACO):** A population of artificial “ants” search for optimal paths on a graph, mimicking how real ants lay and follow pheromone trails to forage. ACO is well-suited for combinatorial problems (like routing and scheduling) and has been widely studied since its introduction in the 1990s. 

- **Artificial Bee Colony (ABC):** Inspired by the foraging behavior of honeybees, bees in the algorithm explore for good “food” (solution) sources and communicate findings. ABC and other bee-inspired algorithms (like variants of *Bee Colony* or *Queen Bee* algorithms) are used for both continuous and combinatorial optimization with simplicity and effectiveness. 

- **Other Swarm Variants:** Many other creatures and strategies have inspired swarm algorithms. For example, *Firefly Algorithm* (FA) mimics how fireflies flash to attract mates (conversion to a search where bright solutions attract others) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=,inspired%20optimizer%20for)), and the *Emperor Penguin Optimizer* is a bio-inspired algorithm based on the huddling and movement patterns of emperor penguins ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=match%20at%20L3127%20,Based%20Syst%20159%2020%2050)). There are also swarm algorithms based on **fish schooling**, **chaotic insect swarms**, **frogs** (e.g. memetic *Frog Leaping* methods), and even hybrid notions (like mixing features from multiple animals). Each new swarm algorithm tries to encapsulate some advantage of a particular ecological strategy, such as how a predator searches widely and then focuses on a target, or how a group can coordinate to explore an area effectively. 

In 2022–2025, **swarm-based algorithms remained the most prolific category** – with new entries in this domain (some with rather exotic inspirations) accounting for many of the novel proposals. In fact, a large number of newly introduced meta-heuristics are based on **animals** (especially fish, birds, or other fauna). Among the **new swarm or animal-based algorithms** are: 

- **Chameleon Swarm Algorithm (2021)** – A recently introduced swarm optimizer designed to mimic the *cunning camouflage and hunting strategy of chameleons*, which hide and then strike **so as to avoid detection and maximize their catch** ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=optimizer%3A%20a%20novel%20bio,Based%20Syst%20243%20108457)). The Chameleon Swarm showed strong performance in some engineering design problems in a 2021 report, and it embodies an intelligent balance of exploration (slowly scanning while “camouflaged”) and exploitation (snapping fast to seize a found solution). 

- **Osprey Optimization (2022)** – A **2022 proposal** known as the Osprey Optimization Algorithm (OOA) that imitates the way ospreys catch fish ([www.frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fmech.2022.1126450/full#:~:text=Osprey%20optimization%20algorithm%3A%20A%20new,for%20solving%20engineering%20optimization%20problems)). Ospreys (fish hawks) have a unique **hunting strategy** – they soar high to spot fish, then **dive swiftly** to capture them from water. The OOA uses this idea to *structure its two phases*: a broad search phase (surveying like an osprey flying over water) and a focused capture phase (a sudden, decisive *“attack”* on a promising solution) ([www.frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fmech.2022.1126450/full#:~:text=Image%3A%20Mohammad%20DehghaniMohammad%20DehghaniPavel)). In simulations, OOA has been benchmarked and compared against dozens of other algorithms and showed strong performance (in one study, it outperformed many older algorithms on a suite of 29 test functions) ([www.frontiersin.org](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fmech.2022.1126450/full#:~:text=match%20at%20L53%20ospreys%20during,The%20simulation%20results%20show%20that)). 

- **Pufferfish Algorithm (2024)** – A new *marine-inspired* meta-heuristic introduced in 2024, based on how pufferfish behave ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=match%20at%20L2870%20Montazeri%20M,problems%20Biomimetics%209%202%2065)). Pufferfish are known for their unique defensive *“puffing”* (quickly expanding) when threatened and their sophisticated patterns (like how male pufferfish draw circles on the seabed). The Pufferfish Optimization Algorithm uses an abstracted model of these behaviors: it can **switch to a defensive mode** (which correlates with a regime of exploring widely, akin to puffing up to ward off trouble) and a **focused mode** (fine adjustments akin to the precise actions of pufferfish), which helps it solve optimization tasks. Early studies found that this algorithm performed well and validated that it’s a viable new entrant in the swarm-based category ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=Montazeri%20M%20Dehghani%202024%20Pufferfish,problems%20Biomimetics%209%202%2065)). 

- **Arctic Puffin, Black‐Winged Kite, Elk Herd,** and **Pied Kingfisher** – A variety of other animal-based algorithms, often named after the species inspiring them. Many of these were introduced or documented in 2024 (some in *journals and reviews*, and even in **MDPI Biomimetics** which has a focus on nature-inspired innovations). For example, the **Arctic Puffin** algorithm (a bird found in harsh climates) was designed to solve engineering design problems ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=211,Sci%20Rep%2013%281%29%3A5373)) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=method)); the **Black‐Winged Kite** (a bird of prey) was formulated as a new swarm heuristic and published in an *Artificial Intelligence Review* paper ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=identification)) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=220,Artif%20Intell%20Rev%2057%284%29%3A1%E2%80%9353)); and the **Crested Porcupine** algorithm (named after the animal) is a 2024 nature-inspired meta-heuristic that uses the idea of a porcupine’s quills and defensive behaviors to shape its algorithmic strategy ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=222,w)) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=223.%20Abdel,Based%20Syst%20284%3A111257)). These, along with many others (“Goose” algorithms, *Walrus optimizers*, etc.), share the theme of applying some unique *biological strategy or motif* to a generic algorithmic framework. 

The above examples illustrate how many different animal *swarm or behavioral patterns* — from birds and **marine life** to land animals — have become the basis for new algorithms. **All of them share the core idea** of a multi-agent (population) system that can coordinate or self-organize to find good solutions. Some use specific **memorization or communication patterns** (e.g. how the Black‐Winged Kite algorithm might involve a long soaring phase and then a dive, or how a Goose algorithm might alternate leaders in a formation). Since so many new algorithms in 2022–2025 came from this category, one of the **key themes** was that the “swarm”-type methods reached a **saturation** – and the sheer **number of different animal-themed algorithms** became a backdrop that required careful analysis (which we’ll discuss in the next section). 

### 2. **Evolutionary and Human-Inspired Algorithms**  
This category includes methods based on the principles of **biological evolution** (selection, mutation, reproduction) as well as those inspired by *human or social behaviors*.  Many of these are either well-established or have seen important refinements in recent years: 

- **Genetic Algorithms (GA):** The classic evolutionary algorithm, and one of the first bionic meta-heuristics. GAs maintain a population of candidate solutions (individuals) that evolve over generations by using “genetic” operators: crossover (mixing information from two parents) and mutation (randomly altering some parts). Better solutions are more likely to be chosen to create offspring. GAs are versatile and have been widely adopted in countless applications – and they serve as the foundation for many modern variants (e.g. specialized GA kits, hybrid GA + local search schemes, etc). GAs (and related methods like **Evolutionary Strategies** and **Evolutionary Algorithms**) mainly differ from swarm methods in that they explicitly model heredity and aren’t *necessarily* about a large group moving in a space — but they still benefit from having a diverse population and exploring via random variations ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=Algorithm%20%28SSA%29%20,26%2C27)) ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=match%20at%20L199%20Evolution,31)). 

- **Hybrid Evolutionary Approaches:** 2022–2025 saw further development of hybrid evolutionary algorithms – particularly **hybrid algorithms that incorporate advanced heuristics or neural components**. For example, one thread has been the use of *evolutionary algorithms to tune deep neural networks or to prune them*, which has led to new methods blending GA or **evolutionary computation** with modern machine learning ([www.danimolina.net](https://www.danimolina.net/papers/#:~:text=Poyatos%2C%20Javier%20and%20Molina%2C%20Daniel,011)). There’s also been interest in merging evolutionary methods with swarm heuristics (*hybrid meta‐heuristics*), or using *ensemble strategies* (combining multiple algorithms) to capitalize on the strengths of each. In fact, one survey specifically focused on ensemble patterns in population-based optimizers ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=In%20this%20paper%2C%20we%20provide,metaheuristics%20with%20new%20names%20are)), where multiple algorithms or operators can be cycled or merged. The use of ensembles is considered a **state‐of‐the‐art** idea: by having a team of algorithms working together (or switching between them), one can adapt to different problem landscapes more effectively than any single algorithm alone. 

- **“Human” or Social Behavior Inspired:** A number of algorithms draw from human behaviors or social processes, which are still “bionic” in the sense of mimicking a biological organism (humans). For example, *Teaching–Learning Based Optimization (TLBO)* uses a teacher–student metaphor to optimize (the teacher helps elevate learners’ knowledge), and *Simulated Annealing* can be thought of as a metaphor (though it’s based on physics more than biology). Over 2022–2025, several **novel human behavior algorithms** were proposed. One particularly notable example is the **Sewing Training Based Optimization (STBO)**, introduced in 2023 ([www.nature.com](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-22458-9#:~:text=A%20new%20human,of%20STBO%20is%20teaching%20the)). STBO is a *“human-inspired”* meta-heuristic where the idea comes from the process of learning to sew: a person practices stitching through a pattern repeatedly, improving each time. In the STBO algorithm, solutions go through iterative “training” cycles (analogous to practice sessions) that incrementally refine them, much like how a trainee’s stitches become more precise with practice. Another interesting entry is the **Botox Optimization Algorithm (BOA)**, which is a humorous but valid algorithm named after how Botox treatments involve strategically placing injections. BOA was introduced in 2024 as a human-based optimization approach in a *Biomimetics* journal ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=221,Biomimetics%209%283%29%3A137)) — it treats solutions as if they were “injected” (or intervened on) in certain patterns, a metaphor for how a cosmetic procedure makes targeted changes. While these human-inspired algorithms can seem unusual, they follow the same mathematical principles as other meta-heuristics, just with different guiding stories. 

  – **Case In Focus: “Squid Game” and Social Phenomena.**  In 2023, researchers even created a meta‐heuristic based on *a popular social phenomenon*: the **Squid Game Optimizer (SGO)** ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=211,Sci%20Rep%2013%281%29%3A5373)). This algorithm drew inspiration from the survival game scenarios in the *Squid Game* TV series – effectively a dynamic where multiple agents are eliminated or advance through rounds. SGO is a hybrid or multi‐phase optimizer that simulates a series of survival game challenges for its population of solutions (with rounds that gradually narrow down the pool). The idea was published in *Scientific Reports*, and it showed how unique (even quirky) inspirations can give rise to valid algorithms. Notably, SGO and other human/societal metaphors (like **Election Algorithms**, where candidates and voting dynamics form a heuristic ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=228,Mathematics%2012%2810%29%3A1513)), or **Football Team Training Algorithm**, which draws from how a sports team practices ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=233,Expert%20Syst%20Appl%20245%3A123088))) all contribute to the overall landscape of bionic heuristics. They add diversity to what “bio-inspired” means, since human social systems are also complex adaptive systems. 

- *****NOTE:*** See &ensp;Box below**  – ***“Different Inspiration, Same Pattern?”***  
  **Box:** *Different Inspiration, Same Pattern?* — Many of the new algorithms in the Human/Swarm categories share very **similar internal patterns**, even if their themes (inspirations) differ. For instance, the core mathematical *behaviors* of a swarm (averaging the positions of a few top performers, plus random jitter) can be disguised as a story about birds, or a story about a group of humans learning, etc. The result is that while a new animal or human-inspired algorithm may appear novel, it might *functionally emulate* a known algorithm (or not be *much more effective* than a well-tuned established method). This has been pointed out by experts who note that the performance of an algorithm *doesn’t inherently depend on its biological name or story* ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=There%20are%20still%20some%20issues,art%20metaheuristics%20%5B20%20%2C%2033)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=new%20metaheuristic%20algorithms%20is%20questioned,but%20are%20less%20efficient)). In other words, tying an algorithm to a particular animal or human scenario *does not automatically guarantee it will perform better*. Many newly introduced **swarm** or **human-based methods** are, essentially, variants of existing techniques (often modifications of the same formulas). This box is not meant to discredit these new ideas, but it underscores the importance of thoroughly validating each new algorithm with careful **benchmarking** – rather than assuming that a novel metaphor (e.g. a chameleon or squid game) will give notable performance advantages. 

### 3. **Plant-Based Algorithms and Other Unusual Origins**  
Beyond animals and humans, a small but interesting category of meta-heuristics draws from **plant biology and botanical phenomena**. Plants operate under very different constraints (they’re typically stationary, have to optimally allocate resources, etc.), so the algorithms inspired by them often have unique characteristics. They’re also relatively underexplored — **plant-inspired meta-heuristics account for only ~10%** of the nature-inspired algorithms in the literature ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=match%20at%20L85%20Despite%205000%2B,to%20optimize%20resource%20acquisition%2C%20growth)), which is a fraction of what animal-based ones cover. 

- **Flower Pollination Algorithm (FPA):** A well-known plant-based algorithm (introduced in earlier years) that mimics how flowers spread pollen via insects or wind. It uses a mix of local and global search modes (akin to a plant’s local pollination vs. long-distance pollination) and has been used in various optimization tasks. 

- **Plant Growth and Seed Algorithms:** There are algorithms based on how plants *grow or propagate*. For example, the *Phototropic Growth Algorithm (PGA)* is designed around how plants grow toward light (a phenomenon called phototropism) ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=%2A%20Plant)), and a related variant called *Plant Growth Simulation Algorithm (PGSA)* models how a plant’s branches develop ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=%2A%20Plant)). These algorithms incorporate a bias toward growth patterns (simulating how plants explore their environment with branched growth). Another example is the *Tree Seed Algorithm (TSA)*, which models how a tree sends out seeds that sprout into new plants as a way to explore new ground ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=%E2%80%93)). In TSA, each solution is a “seed” that can give rise to new solutions (new seeds) in other regions – an elegant way of representing exploration and exploitation. 

- **Root and Resource Algorithms:** Some algorithms focus on how plants **use resources** or communicate. The *Artificial Root Foraging Algorithm (ARFA)* uses the idea that plant roots search for nutrients and water in an optimal way ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=match%20at%20L227%20Resource%20allocation,4)) – it’s a metaphor for a branching search process that seeks out high‐value regions (like roots growing toward nutrient‐rich patches). There’s also *Plant Communication Optimizer (PCO)* ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=match%20at%20L227%20Resource%20allocation,4)), which is based on how plants might *communicate or share resources* (via fungal networks or other mechanisms). Plant-based algorithms like these have shown that plants – despite being a less common metaphor – can inspire methods that perform **very well** in certain problems. In fact, some recent analyses found that certain plant-based algorithms performed *exceptionally* on standard benchmarks. For example, the *Phototropic* and *Rhizome (root)* algorithms achieved ***major performance gains*** – one review noted they outperformed classical algorithms (like GA) on the majority of tested functions ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=match%20at%20L52%20literature%2C%20plant,superiority%20on%20CEC2017%20benchmarks%20and)) ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=literature%2C%20plant,superiority%20on%20CEC2017%20benchmarks%20and)). This suggests that botanical metaphors (with their focus on *growth patterns and resource allocation*) might offer unique benefits, especially for problems that have a spatial or resource distribution flavor (e.g. network design, multi–objective optimizations of resource usage, etc.). 

- **Other Unusual Origins:** Besides plants, there are a few novel algorithms that come from very unusual origins, often in single reports. **Quantum-Inspired** algorithms (which incorporate quantum physics principles, such as using quantum superposition for exploring multiple possibilities) have been studied — though those are somewhat distinct in that they may require specialized interpretation. **Cultural and artistic metaphors** have also been attempted (like algorithms inspired by how musicians improvise, or how a society’s economy functions – though these are less common). In the 2022–2025 timeframe, one notable example was the ***“Dollmaker” algorithm (2024)*** ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=inspired%20algorithm%20for%20real,Biomimetics%209%283%29%3A130)) – a novel human/creative metaphor where the algorithm is inspired by the craft of assembling a doll, which involves shaping various parts and then bringing them together. The Dollmaker Optimizer was introduced as a whimsical but valid idea: it basically had the algorithm “sculpt” solutions by sequentially refining different facets (just as a dollmaker might separately craft the head, body, etc., then assemble them). A similarly creative notion is the ***“Cooking” algorithm (2024)*** ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=248,Sci%20Rep%2014%281%29%3A13359))，which imagines that the process of preparing a complex recipe (adding ingredients, adjusting heat, tasting and correcting) is a metaphor for iterative optimization; this idea was turned into a *Sci‐Electro* algorithm (the **Learning Cooking Algorithm**), and it achieved good performance in a 2024 trial ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=248,Sci%20Rep%2014%281%29%3A13359)). These unusual origins — while not as commonly studied as the main categories — highlight the diversity of ideas. They also underscore that any process involving sequential improvements or __intelligent adjustments__ (even in art or daily life tasks) can potentially be turned into a meta-heuristic algorithm. 

## Major Developments (2022–2025)  

### The “Explosion” of New Algorithms  
The period ***from 2022 up to 2025*** saw a **record number of new bionic meta‐heuristic designs**, as the earlier discussion highlighted. Researchers introduced a plethora of new algorithms – many of which were based on the swarm or animal themes (e.g. a variety of new birds, ocean creatures, and insects), as well as human and plant concepts. This rapid surge is why many experts refer to it as a **“algorithm explosion”**  ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=The%20first%20challenge%20is%20the,The%20surge%20in)). 

It’s worth cataloging some of the major new introductions:  

- In ***2022***, some of the key new algorithms included the Osprey Optimization (mentioned earlier), as well as **Hunger Games Search (HGS)**, a 2021–2022 algorithm with a very unique design. HGS is interesting because it was one of the earliest (and notable) instances of an algorithm that uses a survival game metaphor – it treats the population like participants in a game where only the strongest survive through a series of challenges ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=109,Expert%20Syst%20Appl%20177%3A114864)). Being introduced around 2021 and reported in 2022, HGS quickly gained popularity and had a *“fan base”* among researchers, because its novel viewpoint helped it perform well on some difficult optimization tasks ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=109,Expert%20Syst%20Appl%20177%3A114864)). 

- In ***2023***, the list of new algorithms was long. Researchers proposed: **Squid Game Optimizer (SGO)** (as mentioned, a Sci‐Rep *2023* report documented SGO ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=211,Sci%20Rep%2013%281%29%3A5373))), **Tree Optimization Algorithm (TOA)** (a 2023 algorithm based on the growth patterns of a tree ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=212,Evol%20Intell%2016%284%29%3A1325%E2%80%931338)) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=212,Evol%20Intell%2016%284%29%3A1325%E2%80%931338))), **Waterwheel Plant** (a 2023 algorithm named for an aquatic plant that traps prey ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=213,Processes%2011%285%29%3A1502))), **Wolf‐Bird Hybrid** (WBO, a 2023 algorithm combining wolf pack and bird swarm strategies ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=214,785)) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=214,785))), and even a **Young Double‐Slit Optimizer** (a 2023 meta‐heuristic inspired by the famous physics experiment of light interference ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=information%20modeling,785)) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=215.%20Abdel,Methods%20Appl%20Mech%20Eng%20403%3A115652))). The **Squid Game, Waterwheel, Wolf-Bird,** etc., were all introduced with very **creative themes**. They also largely fell into one narrative: many 2023 algorithms were exploring whether new metaphors could help — and they served as examples of how far researchers were willing to explore various species, phenomena, and even pop culture (*Squid Game*) to find novel designs.  

- In ***2024***, the trend continued. This year saw a huge variety, including the **Pufferfish** and **Arctic Puffin** algorithms mentioned earlier, plus many others:

### —──┤ **Comparison & Benchmarking** ├──—  
After this rich lineup of new bionic techniques, a **crucial question** is: **“Which algorithms actually perform the best?”** It’s clear that not all swarms are created equal — some established ones (like PSO or advanced *variants* of it) remain extremely effective, and a number of recently introduced algorithms end up being “me-too” (having similar performance patterns to others). 

Throughout 2022–2025, multiple comparative studies and systematic reviews took place to ***pinpoint the top performers*** and measure *just how much advantage* each new design offered. Several important findings came from these comparisons:

- **A Large-Scale Benchmarking (2023):** In 2023, a milestone study was conducted by Ma *et al.* — *“Performance **Assessment** and **Exhaustive Listing** of 500+ Nature‐Inspired Meta-Heuristic Algorithms” ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=In%20this%20paper%2C%20we%20provide,metaheuristics%20with%20new%20names%20are)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=))* — which was a **massive project** to catalog **over 500** known meta‐heuristics (most of them bionic). In that study, the authors not only compiled a taxonomy (to organize the algorithms by their inspirations and by their internal operators), but they also performed a rigorous benchmarking of selected algorithms. They took **15 algorithms** — *11* of the more **recently introduced** (from the new swarms, generally ones that had high citation counts or popularity) and *4* **state‐of‐the‐art algorithms** — and tested them on a standard suite of tasks (the *CEC 2017* function benchmarks, with varied dimensions up to 50) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=generating%20solutions,the%20CEC%202017%20benchmark%20suite)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=We%20perform%20extensive%20experiments%20to,of%20all%20the%20comparative%20algorithms)). This line-up was like a tournament: old vs. new. 

  – The results were eye‐opening. They found that **only one** of the new algorithms (out of the 11 tested) performed on par with the best four — a particular algorithm called *EBOwithCMAR (a.k.a. EBCM)*, which is a variant that combines *Evolution Based Optimizer (EBO)* with a *CMA‐ES* (Covariance Matrix) strategy ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=match%20at%20L947%20,HGSA)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=%5B103%5D%20%20,HGSA)). EBCM’s performance was essentially **equal to the “four champions”** on many metrics, and it had very similar statistical behavior (good balance of exploration vs. exploitation, etc.) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=proposed%20EBCM%20algorithm%20performs%20similarly,art)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=In%20conclusion%2C%20EBCM%20shows%20competitive,art)). In other words, EBCM proved to be a truly high‐quality new design. **However, the other 10 new algorithms fell short.** The researchers documented that those *10 recent algorithms were clearly *inferior* to the four state‐of‐art ones* in terms of solution quality, convergence speed, and consistency ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=bias%20to%20the%20origin%20is,of%20all%20the%20comparative%20algorithms)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=In%20the%20cases%20of%20the,In)). They even analyzed specific shortcomings – for example, many new algorithms had a poorer ability to maintain a smooth *exploration–exploitation balance (their search patterns were “rougher” and more erratic) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=bias%20to%20the%20origin%20is,of%20all%20the%20comparative%20algorithms)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=Fig.%C2%A04%2C%20EBCM%20and%204%20state,convergence%20speed%20and%20the%20worst)), and most new algorithms were more prone to a *“search bias”* (tended to wander toward the origin of the coordinate space by default) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=For%20fair%20comparisons%2C%20we%20first,properties%20and%20behaviors%2C%20such%20as)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=other%2010%20recent%20algorithms%20are,of)). These findings explain why those algorithms performed worse on shifted or slightly misaligned problems, which is a subtle but important flaw. 

  – This comprehensive study *reinforced an important point*: **“New” doesn’t automatically mean **better**.** While creative swarms (like new animal algorithms) can robustly solve problems, the majority did not *outperform* the meticulously refined existing algorithms (such as certain **state‐of‐the‐art variants of *Differential Evolution* and *Swarm**). The 4 algorithms that set the benchmark in that study were basically advanced versions of well-known methods (including a couple of next‐generation *declarative* or self‐adaptive algorithms, like **LSHADE-SPA** (a sophisticated variant of *DE with learning and adaptation) and two others which were leading designs). These remain the “gold standards” to compare against. 

- **Tightly Controlled Comparisons:** Other researchers, in noticing this pattern, began calling for the use of more standardized testing whenever a new algorithm is introduced. A 2021 essay dubbed the proliferation of metaphor-based algorithms as *“the elephant in the room”* that many tools didn’t address ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11721-021-00202-9#:~:text=Metaphor,this%20open%20access%20article%20Download)) — it highlighted the importance of being aware of “algorithm explosion” and the need for caution. And a 2022 analysis pointed out that the mere existence of a new “inspiration” (a new story or animal) does *not* reliably correlate with performance differences ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=There%20are%20still%20some%20issues,art%20metaheuristics%20%5B20%20%2C%2033)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=new%20metaheuristic%20algorithms%20is%20questioned,but%20are%20less%20efficient)). Essentially, if two algorithms are both a combination of (say) “take a weighted average of top performers *plus* some random noise,” then whether one is called a *Sparrow*, a *Dove*, or a *Tiger* algorithm may not matter – they’ll perform similarly. Some sparrow-based algorithms in 2020 (like the *Sparrow Search*) turned out very effective, but newer clones (other small birds) offered no obvious gains. 

- **Emerging Patterns (By 2025):** There are certain strategies and algorithm designs that have stood out as the most effective in all this. **One pattern** is that **algorithms which use *“guided random – plus – adaptive adjustment”** (like modern DE variants, new mix‐and‐match algorithms) have remained extremely reliable. Another pattern is the role of **chaotic or noise‐based enhancements**: many new algorithms incorporate chaotic sequences (like using a Chaotic noise generator instead of simple uniform randomness) or other tricks to diversify their search, which seems to help a bit in busting some “dead spots”. (For example, some new algorithms — potentially the *Hermit Crab* algorithm 【27†L4151-L4154 ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11831-025-10408-3#:~:text=239,Evol%20Intell%2017%282%29%3A771%E2%80%93797)) of a shell exchange, which is essentially a *random swap* between solutions, a trick to introduce a burst of diversity). Over the 2022–2025 timeframe, researchers have observed that using these design elements *in any algorithm* may improve it, so they’re **common performance boosters** that appear across different themes (and they likely contribute to why many new algorithms report solid performance). But as far as how many unique design patterns exist, it appears that a lot of the “swarm” style algorithms are all variations on a few core motifs — which means many of them reach the ***same performance ceilings***. 

- **Shift In Focus:** In light of the above, *especially by 2025*, the research community is showing a ***shift in focus and priorities***:
  • To ***✨ Standardize** and ***Rigorize***.  More authors (particularly in systematic reviews) are advocating for using consistent protocols to compare algorithms, such as always testing on standard benchmarks (like the *CEC* challenges) and using proper statistical analyses (to ensure any differences are meaningful). Some publications — like an authoritative review in 2024 on designing evaluation guidelines ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=match%20at%20L3423%20of%20methodological,Swarm%20Evol%20Comput%2067%20100973)) ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10462-025-11377-6#:~:text=bio,Appl%20Artif%20Intell%2090%20103541)) — have begun to *emphasize that any new algorithm should be thoroughly benchmarked*, and if possible, tried on a variety of real-world tasks, before it’s considered a valid improvement. The development of these guidelines (and the dissemination of them among researchers) is a positive sign that the “algorithm explosion” is being thoughtfully managed. 

  • To ***⚕️ Improve Theoretically***.  There’s an acknowledged need to strengthen the theoretical foundation of many bionic algorithms. For example, a lot of plant-based algorithms lack the theory of their more popular animal-based counterparts ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=empirical%20studies%2C%20we%20document%20that,superiority%20on%20CEC2017%20benchmarks%20and)) ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=Genetic%20Algorithms%20%28p%20,Our%20framework%20maps)) (e.g. many plant algorithms haven’t been thoroughly analyzed for convergence in the literature). The consensus is moving toward figuring out ***which specific operations or structural elements*** (moisture locking? multiple branch growth?) in these algorithms are beneficial and why — so that one can design even better algorithms or combine the best ideas from each.  This also ties into exploring new domains of inspiration (like deeper botanical processes, or more fine-grained aspects of animal behavior) that might contain underutilized principles. 

  • To ***📚 Use (Capitalize On) the existing library**.  Since a huge array of bionic meta‐heuristics now exist, the emphasis is turning to making use of them in practice. There was a time when researchers were focused on proposing new algorithms; now the shift is towards integrating them as tools for various applications (in **optimization across sciences**). Indeed, a 2024 study in *Scientific Reports* documented how nature-inspired meta-heuristics are being successfully applied across many disciplines ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/1999-4893/18/11/686#:~:text=,often%20superior%20performance%20compared%20to))  (from engineering design, to scheduling, to even COVID-19 modeling). The implication is that by having so many algorithms, one can carefully choose the one (or a group) best suited for a task, and there’s a growing knowledge base for doing so. For example, certain algorithms are known to excel at **optimizing under noisy conditions**, others at **quickly finding coarse solutions**, etc. The line that divides one algorithm’s niche from another’s is slowly becoming clearer, which helps practitioners select the optimal heuristic for each scenario. 

## Conclusion  
The recent evolution of **bionic meta‐heuristic optimization techniques** – especially over the last **2–3 years – has delivered a rich, multifaceted toolkit** that covers a wide range of biological inspirations. From classic, widely used methods (like **swarm algorithms** and **evolutionary algorithms**) to an array of **newly invented designs** (chameleons, ospreys, pufferfish, games, and more), the landscape has grown dramatically. This “growth” has allowed many problems (particularly in **engineering, optimization, and intelligent systems**) to be solved more efficiently – since the various **swarm** and **adaptive algorithms** can greatly speed up the discovery of optimal solutions and provide robust performance in complex tasks. 

At the same time, the sheer **quantity and variety** of these bionic techniques has taught us a few important lessons:
- **One**: Many of these algorithms, despite being based on different stories or species, are extremely **similar in their core working principles**. The majority of the **swarm‐based** tools share 80–100% of the same structural paradigms (a phenomenon where a new algorithm’s advantages can be largely explained by known patterns, rather than it unlocking a brand‐new capability). As a result, using a novel animal or plant theme by itself is *not* a guaranteed performance boost – and many new ideas have proven that they ultimately reach the same performance limits as existing methods (unless they incorporate some new trick or unique component). 

- **Two**: ***Quality matters*** – and moving forward, it’s the high‐quality algorithms (whatever their origin) that will be used and studied. The *“explosion”* of new meta‐heuristics has clearly pointed to which algorithms are the most effective: on one side, we have highly refined protocols (like advanced **differential evolution** or “ensemble” approaches) that stand out as extremely efficient, and on the other side, we have a long list of new algorithms that may *only perform well under certain circumstances*. By carefully testing and comparing them, researchers can pinpoint which **swarm patterns** or **growth patterns** are the most productive and which are redundant. This also means that some of the creative “story” based algorithms can be viewed as **motivational scenarios** – they help us think in new ways – but they might *not* become mainstream unless they offer advantages in data (like the Pufferfish algorithm, which seems particularly effective for certain patterns, or the EBCM, which fused a new idea with an established one and achieved strong results). 

- **Three**: The focus is shifting to using these bionic algorithms in combination, and in solving real problems. The years 2022–2025 have given us a massive list of swarms and heuristics (especially ***≥2023*** with the *algorithm explosion* trophy). Now, the next phase is to harness them to produce real **productivity gains** (e.g. design more efficient engineering systems, perform large‐scale scheduling and resource allocation, assist in AI training, etc.), and to use them in synergy (as some studies have started to do using ensembles and hybrid algorithms) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=In%20this%20paper%2C%20we%20provide,metaheuristics%20with%20new%20names%20are)) ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2210650223000226#:~:text=)). Many applications – from optimizing power grids to planning robotic motion – can benefit from these techniques, especially if the correct algorithm is chosen for the job ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/12/15/3263#:~:text=Research%20Progress%20of%20Nature,Style%20Xu%20Y%2C%20Li%20Q)) ([www.mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/12/15/3263#:~:text=Share%20and%20Cite%20MDPI%20and,Style%20Xu%20Y%2C%20Li%20Q)). 

In the final analysis, the **bionic meta‐heuristic techniques** (swarm, evolutionary, plant, human) are a crucial part of modern computational intelligence.  High‐quality swarms (like PSO, advanced *Adaptive Composite* algorithms, etc.) are likely to remain cornerstones ***and*** will be joined by a few of the new entrants (the ones proven to be extremely effective, such as EBCM or certain plant algorithms) – which collectively help push more problems to be solved *efficiently*. The new “**algorithm explosion**” has helped us identify which advancers are worth usage and which are mainly “creative trials.” As this wave of bionic innovation continues, the field is poised to remain a ***key contributor*** – enabling technologies across domains to achieve smarter performance by exploiting the patterns and lessons learned from nature’s wide diversity.
