# Acoustic Wave Resonators and Filters in Next-Generation Wireless Communications: Advances and Future Perspectives (2022-2025)

This comprehensive review examines the recent advances in acoustic wave resonator and filter technologies that are reshaping next-generation wireless communication systems, including 5G, Wi-Fi 6/7, and emerging 6G networks. Over the period 2022-2025, significant technological breakthroughs have emerged across multiple domains including thin-film bulk acoustic wave (BAW) resonators with enhanced piezoelectric materials, surface acoustic wave (SAW) filter optimization, novel device architectures addressing spurious mode suppression, and integration of acoustic technologies with photonic and microwave systems. The field has witnessed a convergence of material science innovations—particularly scandium-doped aluminum nitride (AlScN) and lithium niobate thin films—coupled with advanced microfabrication techniques and novel resonator geometries. These developments have enabled substantial improvements in quality factors, electromechanical coupling coefficients, temperature stability, and power handling capabilities. This review systematically categorizes recent methodological approaches, synthesizes experimental outcomes across platforms, and identifies emerging research trajectories toward fully integrated acoustic-photonic systems and reconfigurable filtering architectures for spectrum-constrained environments.

## Introduction to Acoustic Wave Resonators and Filters in Modern Wireless Systems

The fundamental challenge in contemporary wireless communications stems from increasingly crowded electromagnetic spectrum allocations, where devices must operate across dozens of frequency bands separated by only a few megahertz[3][6]. Acoustic wave resonators have emerged as the preferred solution for RF filtering and signal conditioning due to their exceptionally compact form factors relative to electromagnetic alternatives and their superior frequency selectivity characteristics. The acoustic wavelength at gigahertz frequencies is approximately one million times shorter than the electromagnetic wavelength at equivalent frequencies, enabling resonator structures in the micrometer range compared to centimeter-scale electromagnetic cavities[12][14]. This fundamental physics advantage has driven the near-universal adoption of acoustic resonators across the mobile device ecosystem, from smartphones and tablets to Wi-Fi access points, satellite communication systems, and automotive radar platforms.

The technical landscape of acoustic resonator technologies has traditionally divided into two primary categories: surface acoustic wave (SAW) devices operating effectively up to approximately 2 GHz, and bulk acoustic wave (BAW) resonators designed for higher frequency bands above 1.5 GHz[6][14]. However, the evolution toward 5G networks operating in the 2-6 GHz range, Wi-Fi 6/6E in the 5-6 GHz bands, and emerging 6G proposals extending to millimeter-wave and sub-terahertz frequencies have necessitated fundamental technological innovations. The transition from traditional materials to engineered piezoelectric thin films, from simple resonator geometries to sophisticated multi-mode architectures, and from isolated filter components to integrated front-end modules represents a major inflection point in the field. The market for surface acoustic wave resonators alone was projected to reach $1.2 billion in 2024, with sustained growth driven by these expanding application domains[4].

The core performance metrics governing acoustic resonator design remain fundamentally rooted in two competing parameters: the quality factor (Q), which determines insertion loss and bandwidth characteristics, and the electromechanical coupling coefficient (k²), which governs bandwidth and filter sharpness. Historically, improving one parameter has invariably degraded the other, creating a design space constrained by fundamental material properties[1]. Over the 2022-2025 period, breakthrough innovations in material engineering, particularly through scandium doping of aluminum nitride, have begun to overcome this historical tradeoff by substantially enhancing k² values while maintaining or improving Q factors[8][16]. These material innovations, coupled with novel resonator architectures and advanced microfabrication techniques derived from MEMS technology, have enabled performance enhancements of two to three times relative to conventional designs, fundamentally expanding the operational domains for acoustic-based filtering.

## Advanced Material Systems and Piezoelectric Platforms

### Scandium-Doped Aluminum Nitride and AlScN-Based Resonators

The development of scandium-doped aluminum nitride represents perhaps the most significant materials breakthrough in acoustic resonator technology during the 2022-2025 period. Researchers at the University of Pennsylvania and other institutions have demonstrated that scandium doping can enhance the d₃₃ piezoelectric coefficient of aluminum nitride by up to five times, representing a substantial improvement over pure AlN baseline performance[8]. This enhanced piezoelectric response directly translates to increased electromechanical coupling coefficients (k²), enabling broader filter bandwidths and improved energy conversion efficiency. In work published in IEEE journals, researchers fabricated AlScN-based bulk acoustic wave resonators operating at high frequencies of 17-18 GHz through four-layer periodically poled piezoelectric film structures, demonstrating a greater than twofold increase in the resonator figure-of-merit compared to conventional approaches[8].

The mechanisms underlying scandium doping enhancement involve modification of the material's elastic and dielectric properties. Density functional theory calculations have revealed a superlinear dependency between scandium concentration and piezoelectric properties, suggesting that highly-doped AlScN systems (with scandium concentrations exceeding 30 percent) can achieve electromechanical coupling coefficients exceeding those of traditional thin-film BAW resonators[16]. However, the growth of highly-doped scandium-rich AlScN films presents significant challenges related to abnormally-oriented grain formation, which degrades both quality factors and coupling coefficients. Researchers at various institutions have successfully addressed this challenge through optimized sputtering conditions and seed layer engineering, achieving FWHM values as low as 2.1 degrees for 280-nanometer AlScN films deposited directly on silicon without sacrificial layer structures[16]. This represents the lowest reported value for sub-300-nanometer AlScN films sputter-deposited directly on silicon substrates without bottom electrodes, representing a major manufacturing achievement.

The practical impact of AlScN technology has been demonstrated through filter implementations covering the 7-11 GHz frequency range relevant for 5G and Wi-Fi 6E applications[16]. Nanoacoustic cantilever-loaded mode resonators (CLMRs) based on highly-doped ScAlN have shown unprecedented performance combinations, with quality factors exceeding 1500 at 7-11 GHz frequencies, coupled with electromechanical coupling coefficients enabling steep filter skirts and low insertion losses. The ability to achieve multi-frequency filter banks on the same substrate through lithographic frequency definition represents a fundamental advantage over traditional approaches requiring different material compositions for different frequency bands. These advances have positioned scandium-doped aluminum nitride as the leading material platform for next-generation RF front-end filter modules, with multiple commercial entities and research institutions now focusing on production-scale manufacturing of AlScN-based devices.

### Lithium Niobate Thin Films and Advanced Piezoelectric Architectures

While aluminum nitride-based platforms have dominated the commercial BAW filter landscape, lithium niobate thin films have emerged as an alternative material platform offering complementary advantages, particularly for applications requiring higher electromechanical coupling coefficients and broader design flexibility. Researchers have demonstrated laterally excited bulk acoustic wave (LBAW) and laterally vibrating resonators (LVRs) on thin-film lithium niobate achieving electromechanical coupling coefficients in the 20-40 percent range, substantially exceeding traditional SAW and BAW systems[49][52]. Recent work published in 2024-2025 has extended these capabilities to X-cut lithium niobate, demonstrating both fundamental and higher-order overtone resonances through embedded interdigitated electrodes, enabling multi-band filter implementations on single substrates[52].

The laterally excited bulk acoustic wave architecture represents a significant departure from traditional vertical resonator geometries. By embedding electrodes within the piezoelectric film thickness rather than utilizing surface or vertical electrode structures, these resonators can simultaneously excite symmetric (S0) and shear-horizontal (SH0) vibrational modes through carefully designed electrode geometries[52]. This mode-selective excitation enables figures of merit reaching 437 at 673 megahertz and 53 at 1.05 gigahertz, with potential extension to higher frequencies through systematic optimization of electrode width and pitch parameters. The design flexibility of LBAW structures permits frequency tuning through simple modifications of electrode geometry without requiring different substrate materials, addressing a major limitation of traditional approaches where frequency control depends critically on piezoelectric film thickness.

Recent investigations have explored the role of substrate engineering in optimizing lithium niobate resonator performance. Multi-layered substrate stacks combining lithium tantalate with silicon oxide and polycrystalline silicon have been shown to improve phase velocity characteristics, quality factors, and electromechanical coupling coefficients relative to single-layer approaches[26]. Analysis of multi-layered 42-degree Y-X cut lithium tantalate structures revealed that optimal SiO₂ and aluminum electrode thicknesses can increase quality factors to 3400 for incredibly high-performance (IHP) SAW configurations, representing threefold improvements over standard lithium tantalate SAW devices[26]. These substrate engineering approaches have driven a research focus on understanding how multi-layered structures enable control over energy distribution between acoustic and electrical domains, fundamentally improving resonator efficiency.

## Resonator Architectures and Geometric Innovations

### Hairclip Resonator Design and High-Performance Magnetostatic Wave Systems

A breakthrough resonator geometry developed at Purdue University in collaboration with BAE Systems represents a novel approach to overcoming the traditional tradeoff between signal clarity (quality factor) and signal efficiency (coupling coefficient)[1]. The "hairclip" resonator design brings key electrical components substantially closer together through innovative three-dimensional geometry, dramatically improving efficiency through reduced parasitic couplings. This design was implemented using deep anisotropic etching of gadolinium gallium garnet (GGG) substrates, a microfabrication technique whose transformative potential has been compared to the introduction of anisotropic silicon etching in the 1960s, which enabled the rapid advancement of MEMS devices[1].

The hairclip architecture exploits magnetostatic wave propagation in magnetic materials, specifically yttrium iron garnet (YIG), which offers exceptionally low energy loss characteristics crucial for high-frequency applications. Spin waves—ripples in the magnetic orientation of atomic spins within magnetic materials—differ fundamentally from acoustic waves in that they involve no physical particle movement, yet provide comparable or superior resonance characteristics at high frequencies[1]. The Purdue team overcame historical manufacturing challenges that had prevented YIG-based resonators from achieving the efficiency needed for modern communications systems by developing precision microfabrication processes enabling signal efficiency improvements of two to three times relative to previous designs[1]. The resulting resonators demonstrated exceptional performance in the 6-20 gigahertz frequency range, precisely spanning the critical frequencies for 5G, satellite communications, and emerging 6G applications.

The technical achievement of anisotropic etching in GGG substrates appears likely to have broad implications extending far beyond resonator coupling enhancement. Since yttrium iron garnet is typically grown on gadolinium gallium garnet substrates, this etching technique promises to drive innovation across radio-frequency communications, magnonics (the study of magnon quasiparticles), and quantum information processing applications[1]. The ability to precisely shape magnetic materials at the micron scale represents a fundamental capability that should enable future generations of magnon-based devices and integrated systems currently in early research stages.

### Suppression of Spurious Modes and Multi-Mode Resonator Optimization

Spurious acoustic modes—unintended resonances arising from complex interactions between electrode structures, piezoelectric films, and substrate materials—have historically limited resonator performance and constrained operational bandwidth. Laterally excited bulk acoustic resonators (XBARs) based on lithium niobate thin films exhibit numerous spurious modes with very narrow frequency spacing, particularly challenging in high-frequency regimes above 5 gigahertz where mode density increases substantially[15]. Research published in IEEE Transactions on Ultrasonics, Ferroelectrics, and Frequency Control (2023) provided systematic analysis of spurious mode suppression through a combination of slowness surface optimization, rigorous admittance function simulation, and dispersion curve characterization[15].

The analysis revealed that spurious-free response in lithium niobate XBARs can be achieved through careful selection of substrate cut angles (specifically Euler angles of 0°, 4°-15°, and 90°) combined with optimization of plate thickness as a fraction of acoustic wavelength (ranging between 0.05 and 0.1 wavelengths depending on crystal orientation)[15]. The tangential velocities of 18-37 kilometers per second, combined with electromechanical coupling coefficients of 15-17 percent and feasible electrode duty factors of 0.5, enable practical implementation of high-performance 3-6 gigahertz filters suitable for contemporary wireless applications. This work established that spurious mode suppression is not incidental but rather represents a systematic design parameter that must be optimized alongside primary resonance characteristics.

Recent investigations into laterally excited bulk acoustic wave resonators on X-cut lithium niobate have demonstrated pentagon spiral electrode geometries that effectively suppress spurious modes through deliberate electrode non-parallelism[49]. The non-parallel electrode edges cause transverse acoustic waves to experience different reflection paths at each point along the electrode periphery, effectively spreading the energy that would otherwise accumulate as spurious mode standing waves. Experimental demonstration of these pentagon spiral structures operating at 5.4 gigahertz with 21.3 percent effective electromechanical coupling and minimal spurious mode content represents a significant advance in enabling clean, high-performance filtering in congested spectrum bands[49].

## Integration with Photonic and Microwave Systems

### Hybrid Acousto-Optic Modulators and Photonic-Acoustic Integration

The period 2022-2025 has witnessed emerging integration of acoustic resonators with photonic systems, creating hybrid devices that leverage complementary advantages of acoustic and optical wave phenomena. Researchers at multiple institutions have demonstrated microwave-frequency acousto-optic modulation through excitation of high-overtone bulk acoustic wave resonances (HBAR) in photonic stacks, achieving novel device functionality impossible with either acoustic or optical systems alone[40][37]. These hybrid approaches exploit the tight vertical acoustic confinement enabled by FBAR architectures to create acoustically-induced perturbations in optical waveguides, enabling sub-nanosecond optical modulation with milliwatt-level drive power[40].

Research published in Nature Communications demonstrated Si₃N₄-based optical waveguides integrated with high-overtone BAW resonators, achieving microwave-frequency acousto-optic modulation with unprecedented efficiency[40]. The tight vertical acoustic confinement releases lateral design degrees of freedom, enabling negligible crosstalk between optical and acoustic channels while preserving low optical propagation loss. Applications demonstrated include optical isolation through spatiotemporal modulation, with over 17 decibels of isolation achieved, substantially exceeding previous approaches based on magnetic materials or thermally-tuned resonators[40]. This hybrid platform has also enabled compact opto-electronic oscillators and microwave-to-optical converters with integrated structures smaller than conventional discrete component implementations.

More recent work has extended hybrid integration to lithium niobate photonic platforms, demonstrating acoustically-tunable microring resonators through integration of interdigital acoustic transducers directly within photonic structures[27]. The high piezoelectric properties of lithium niobate enable efficient conversion of radiofrequency electrical signals into traveling acoustic waves, which create dynamically moving Bragg gratings within optical cavities. This approach achieves optical mode strong coupling with normalized cooperativity of 2.46 milliwatts⁻¹, enabling remarkable efficiency in reconfigurable photonic circuits[27]. The demonstrated Möbius band topology transformation through acoustic tuning suggests pathways toward fundamentally new classes of reconfigurable photonic devices for signal routing, programmable filtering, and arbitrary dispersion control applications.

### Acousto-Photonic Microwave-to-Optical Conversion

A particularly promising emerging application involves using acoustic resonators as bridges for microwave-to-optical conversion, addressing a key challenge in quantum information processing and advanced signal processing systems. Recent demonstrations of acousto-optic modulators based on thin-film aluminum scandium nitride have shown effective half-wave voltages (Vπ) as low as 1.21 volts, corresponding to normalized Vπ values of only 0.0242 V·cm[13]. These represent substantial reductions in required drive power relative to conventional electro-optic approaches, enabling practical implementation in integrated circuits. The microring resonator architecture with optical quality factors exceeding 1.8×10⁴ combined with acoustic resonators exhibiting quality factors of 650 at 2.11 gigahertz demonstrates feasible engineering of material loss budgets to achieve efficient photon-phonon conversion[13].

The integration of interdigital transducers directly on the AlScN layer enables simultaneous fabrication of optical waveguides and electromechanical transducers within identical material layers, simplifying manufacturing relative to heterogeneous integration approaches. This monolithic integration strategy promises cost-effective production of integrated photonic-acoustic circuits through CMOS-compatible processing, enabling dense arrays of microwave-to-optical converters suitable for next-generation signal processing architectures. Projected improvements through further optimization of crystal quality and transducer design suggest that effective modulation power requirements could be reduced to sub-milliwatt levels, approaching the sensitivity limits imposed by quantum noise.

## Performance Characterization and Experimental Evaluation

### Quality Factors, Coupling Coefficients, and Figure-of-Merit Analysis

Across diverse resonator implementations reported during 2022-2025, consistent improvements in fundamental performance metrics have been documented through rigorous experimental characterization. Quality factors—measuring the sharpness of resonance and determining insertion loss—have shown systematic improvement through material engineering and geometric optimization. AlScN-based resonators operating at 7-11 gigahertz frequencies have achieved quality factors exceeding 1500, representing substantial improvements over pure aluminum nitride baselines of approximately 1000 at equivalent frequencies[16]. Lithium niobate LBAW devices have demonstrated quality factors of 850-900 at 5.4 gigahertz with remarkably clean resonance characteristics free of spurious modes, enabling practical filter implementation without complex mode-suppression networks[49].

Electromechanical coupling coefficients (k²), representing the fraction of electrical energy converted to acoustic energy, have shown perhaps the most dramatic improvements through scandium doping. Pure aluminum nitride platforms typically achieve k² values in the 3-6 percent range for traditional FBAR and SAW geometries, limiting filter bandwidth to approximately 10-15 percent of center frequency. ScAlN with 30-40 percent scandium concentration has enabled k² values exceeding 15 percent, enabling bandwidth improvements of 2-3 times while maintaining reasonable quality factors[8][16]. Lithium niobate LBAW resonators have demonstrated even more impressive k² values approaching 40 percent for optimized geometries, enabling extraordinary filter bandwidth capabilities at the cost of somewhat reduced quality factors[52].

Figure-of-merit calculations combining these metrics reveal the overall efficiency of resonator implementations. FOM values, calculated as the product of frequency and quality factor, have shown systematic improvements from historical values near 10⁴-10⁵ for conventional 2-GHz SAW devices to values exceeding 10⁶ for optimized 6-20 GHz implementations[1][49]. These improvements reflect fundamental advances in material engineering, geometric optimization, and fabrication precision rather than incremental refinements to existing approaches. Comparative analysis across technology platforms indicates that no single approach dominates across all frequency ranges and applications; rather, each technology (AlScN BAW, lithium niobate LBAW, traditional SAW) occupies an optimized niche addressing specific performance requirements and frequency ranges.

### Temperature Stability and Environmental Robustness

Temperature stability represents a critical performance metric for wireless systems deployed across wide geographic regions and environmental conditions. Historically, piezoelectric materials exhibit negative temperature coefficients in the range of -20 to -40 parts per million per degree Celsius, causing frequency drift that degrades filter selectivity at temperature extremes[14][59]. Temperature-compensated SAW filters addressed this limitation through silicon dioxide deposition or substrate bonding techniques, achieving reduced drift of -22 parts per million per degree Celsius, but these approaches introduced additional insertion loss and spurious resonant modes[59].

Recent advances have substantially improved temperature stability through multiple complementary mechanisms. NoDrift SAW and BAW filter processes have achieved near-zero temperature coefficients of 0±2 parts per million per degree Celsius, representing elimination of frequency drift over standard military temperature ranges (-20 to +85 degrees Celsius)[56]. Scandium doping of aluminum nitride has contributed to improved temperature stability through fundamental changes to elastic and dielectric properties, reducing the net temperature coefficient even without additional compensation layers[8]. Multi-layered substrate structures combining materials with complementary temperature coefficients have enabled thermally-compensated designs maintaining frequency stability across broader temperature ranges than single-layer approaches[26].

Experimental validation across deployed systems has confirmed the practical importance of temperature stability improvements. Band 13 (2.3-2.4 GHz) and WiFi coexistence scenarios require maintaining sub-2-megahertz frequency drift over temperature to prevent adjacent-band interference, achievable only with NoDrift filter technologies operating to ±2 ppm/°C specifications[56]. Similarly, satellite radio filtering applications require temperature-stable filters passing satellite bands while rejecting adjacent allocations, specifications met only through advanced temperature compensation techniques. The commercial availability of temperature-stable filters across frequency ranges from 1.5 to 8 gigahertz has removed a major barrier to deploying high-performance filtering in field-deployed systems.

### Power Handling and Linearity Characteristics

As wireless systems operate at higher power levels and across broader signal bandwidths, acoustic resonator power handling capability and linearity characteristics have assumed critical importance. High intermodulation distortion at elevated power levels can overwhelm sensitive receivers even in interference-free environments through generation of spurious intermodulation products. Research on nonlinear characteristics in SAW and BAW resonators has established that intermodulation distortion scales nonlinearly with input power, with empirical measurements showing approximately 2.5 decibel increase in intermodulation products for every 1 decibel increase in carrier power[22][46].

Modern BAW filter implementations achieve substantially improved power handling through optimized electrode materials, reduced parasitic couplings, and substrate engineering. Solidly mounted resonator (SMR) configurations with scandium-doped aluminum nitride have demonstrated power handling of 5 watts continuous with 40-watt peaks while maintaining low insertion loss and acceptable intermodulation distortion[59]. In-band third-order intermodulation intercept points (IIP3) in the +36 to +40 decibel-milliwatt range have been reported for carefully optimized filter implementations[8], enabling practical deployment in power amplifier chains where intermodulation products must be suppressed by 40-60 decibels relative to carrier levels to prevent receiver desensitization.

Analysis of passive intermodulation distortion mechanisms has revealed that poor solder joints, loose connections, dissimilar metal junctions, and oxidation between metal surfaces represent the dominant sources of nonlinearity in practical filter implementations[22]. Comprehensive manufacturing quality control, proper design of connector and electrode interfaces, and selection of materials minimizing ferromagnetic effects have proven essential to achieving linear filter performance. Commercial implementations now incorporate these design principles, enabling filters suitable for multi-band transmission scenarios where simultaneous high-power signals in adjacent bands could otherwise cause severe receiver desensitization.

## Emerging Applications and Novel Device Configurations

### Frequency Comb Generation in Acoustic Resonators

An emerging application domain involves leveraging the nonlinear properties of high-quality-factor acoustic resonators for microwave frequency comb generation, analogous to optical frequency combs that revolutionized metrology and spectroscopy. Researchers have demonstrated phononic frequency combs in high-overtone bulk acoustic resonators operating in thin-film lithium niobate, exploiting thermal nonlinearity to generate parametric down-conversion and comb generation[31]. By providing input power at 257 megahertz—the sum frequency of two acoustic modes at 86 and 171 megahertz—researchers observed comb generation with spacing varying nonlinearly with drive frequency and power.

These phononic combs offer potential advantages over purely electronic approaches through reduced power consumption, compact form factors, and direct integration into acoustic front-end modules[31]. The comb generation mechanism relies on mode-mode interactions and thermal effects in high-Q resonators, providing pathways to generate stabilized frequency combs without the complex pump-seeding arrangements required in optical implementations. Theoretical analysis and experiments suggest that improved resonator quality factors and optimized overtone spacing should enable even more efficient comb generation at lower drive powers[31]. While phononic frequency combs remain in early research stages, their potential to enable compact, efficient, integrated frequency comb sources positions this as a promising research direction for advanced signal processing applications.

### Directional Surface Acoustic Wave Sensing

Recent innovations in micropillar resonator design have enabled novel directional sensing of surface acoustic waves, potentially enabling new classes of acoustic sensors and signal processing devices[2]. Dr. Taehwa Lee's team at Toyota Advanced Research discovered that subwavelength micropillar resonators exhibit directional sensitivity to incoming surface acoustic waves through interference between distinct resonant modes: bending and longitudinal vibrations. The displacement patterns of micropillars vary markedly depending on the direction of incoming surface acoustic waves, with the interference between vertical and horizontal components driving the observed directional response[2].

The fundamental physics involves excitation of bending resonance through horizontal components of surface acoustic wave motion and longitudinal resonance through vertical components, with careful micropillar geometry alignment enabling constructive interference that maximizes directional response[2]. This directional sensing capability could enable compact, efficient solutions for monitoring surface acoustic waves in applications ranging from wireless communications to next-generation acoustic devices. The micropillar approach represents a significant departure from traditional interdigital transducer designs, offering potential advantages in integration density and multiport sensing architectures. Further development of micropillar resonator arrays could enable novel beamforming and signal processing capabilities in RF front-end modules.

### Reconfigurable Photonic Circuits with Acoustic Tuning

Acoustic resonators integrated with reconfigurable photonic circuits represent an emerging platform for dynamically programmable optical signal processing. Recent demonstrations have shown that gigahertz acoustic waves can create dynamically moving Bragg gratings within optical cavities, enabling electrical switching and continuous tuning of optical path topology[27]. This approach circumvents limitations of conventional electro-optic tuning by enabling control of optical routing rather than merely phase or absorption, opening pathways toward fully reconfigurable photonic circuits with potential applications in dynamic signal routing, programmable optical filtering, and arbitrary dispersion control.

The demonstrated acoustic-optic modulators based on lithium niobate platforms achieve remarkable efficiency through combination of lithium niobate's inherent advantages—high piezoelectric coupling, low optical loss, and CMOS-compatible integration potential[27]. The programmable nature of acoustic tuning, enabled through simple modification of radiofrequency input signals, enables dynamic reconfiguration without requiring mechanical tuning elements or permanent modifications to optical structure. This programmability should enable implementation of complex photonic circuits with multiple functional modes accessible through software control, representing a fundamental advance in photonic system design philosophy.

## Synthesis of Performance Trends and Comparative Technology Analysis

### Technology Comparison and Frequency Domain Allocation

The acoustic resonator field has effectively divided into technology niches optimized for specific frequency ranges and applications. Surface acoustic wave filters remain dominant for frequencies below approximately 2 gigahertz, particularly in systems requiring ultra-compact implementations and applications where power handling requirements remain modest[5][14][59]. The continuing evolution toward incredibly high-performance SAW devices with quality factors exceeding 4000 at frequencies above 5 gigahertz demonstrates that SAW technology remains viable even at higher frequencies when substrate materials and electrode designs are carefully optimized[14][59].

Thin-film bulk acoustic wave resonators (FBARs) based on aluminum nitride have established clear dominance in the 2-6 gigahertz frequency range corresponding to 4G LTE and 5G applications. With quality factors routinely exceeding 2000 and established manufacturing processes integrated into semiconductor fabrication workflows, AlN-based BAW technology provides compelling cost-performance for mass-market applications[6][14]. The emergence of scandium-doped aluminum nitride platforms promises to extend BAW capabilities to higher frequencies and broader bandwidths while maintaining CMOS compatibility and established manufacturing infrastructure[8][16].

Lithium niobate-based technologies have emerged as a specialized platform for ultra-high bandwidth applications and research demonstrations where electromechanical coupling exceeding 30 percent justifies increased manufacturing complexity. While primarily demonstrating laboratory prototypes to date, the potential for lithium niobate to enable fundamentally broader filter bandwidths and higher-frequency operation makes this platform attractive for next-generation systems where spectrum efficiency reaches critical importance[49][52]. The increasing commercial availability of lithium niobate on insulator wafers from multiple suppliers suggests maturation of this platform toward volume production.

### Frequency Range Extensions and mmWave/Sub-THz Operations

Historical acoustic resonator implementations have operated primarily below 10 gigahertz, constrained by mechanical limitations of traditional thickness-resonance modes requiring ever-thinner piezoelectric films at higher frequencies. Recent innovations enabling operation at 17-18 gigahertz through higher-order modes and beyond to 24 gigahertz through extreme thinning of piezoelectric films have opened new applications in millimeter-wave communications[1][8]. Magnetostatic wave resonators based on yttrium iron garnet operating in the 6-20 gigahertz range represent an alternative high-frequency approach leveraging spin wave physics rather than conventional acoustic mechanics[1].

The extension to mmWave frequencies creates new design challenges including accommodation of increased spurious mode density, thermal management in highly integrated systems, and packaging/integration complexity[44][47]. Commercial implementations of 28-gigahertz filters for 5G millimeter-wave applications from companies including Johanson Technology and Knowles Precision Devices demonstrate that practical mmWave filter solutions are now feasible through careful engineering of resonator geometries and substrate configurations[44][47]. Further progress toward sub-terahertz operation above 100 gigahertz remains largely in research phases, with fundamental limitations including acoustic attenuation and piezoelectric material properties requiring revolutionary advances rather than incremental improvements.

## Challenges, Limitations, and Future Research Directions

### Manufacturing Scalability and Yield Enhancement

The transition from laboratory demonstrations to high-volume manufacturing represents a critical challenge facing acoustic resonator technology, particularly for advanced AlScN and lithium niobate platforms. While traditional aluminum nitride BAW manufacturing has achieved mature status through integration into semiconductor fabrication workflows, scandium-doped aluminum nitride requires process modifications including specialized sputter equipment configurations and modified deposition parameters to manage abnormally-oriented grain formation[16]. The cost adder for scandium doping relative to traditional aluminum nitride remains significant, creating potential barriers to widespread commercial deployment unless volume production substantially reduces material and processing costs.

Lithium niobate platforms face distinct manufacturing challenges related to thinning of substrates without introducing mechanical damage or defects, integration of acoustic and photonic structures with compatible processing sequences, and development of packaging solutions maintaining acoustic performance while enabling electrical and optical input/output connections[49][52]. The recent achievement of commercially-available eight-inch diameter lithium niobate on insulator wafers represents significant progress, but integration of these substrates into established semiconductor manufacturing infrastructure remains incomplete. Further progress requires coordinated development of compatible photolithography processes, etch techniques, and metallization approaches suitable for these specialized materials.

### Spurious Mode Suppression at Higher Frequencies

As operating frequencies increase and resonator structures become more compact, spurious acoustic modes present increasingly severe challenges. The density of potential spurious modes scales with frequency, creating challenges to achieving filter selectivity and phase linearity when multiple modes exist in close frequency proximity. While recent work has demonstrated spurious-free designs through careful substrate orientation and geometric optimization, the empirical nature of spurious mode suppression—requiring extensive simulation and iterative refinement—limits design efficiency[15][49]. Development of improved physical models and design methodologies accounting for mode interactions across broader frequency spans would substantially accelerate future device development.

The extension to higher frequencies beyond 10 gigahertz requires developing deeper understanding of how overtone modes interact with substrate effects, package inductances, and electrical feed lines. Overmodded BAW (OBAR) resonators intentionally exploit higher-order modes to achieve frequency operation impossible with fundamental modes, but engineering of these devices requires sophisticated design tools and validation methodologies not yet standardized across the industry[59]. Future progress likely requires integrated computational frameworks combining rigorous acoustic modeling with electrical circuit simulation and manufacturing process variations.

### Integration Complexity and System-Level Challenges

While individual resonators and filter components have achieved remarkable performance improvements, integration into complete RF front-end modules introducing reduced parasitic effects and system-level optimization remains challenging. The traditional integration approach stacking pre-assembled BAW filters with duplexer and antenna switching components creates impedance discontinuities and coupling paths that degrade performance relative to theoretical component-level specifications[3]. Monolithic integration combining resonators, filters, amplifiers, and switching functions on single substrates offers potential solutions but requires simultaneous optimization of acoustic, electrical, thermal, and electromagnetic performance across dissimilar material systems.

Advanced packaging approaches including 3D heterogeneous integration of lithium niobate photonic-acoustic circuits with silicon CMOS electronics represent promising but technically complex pathways forward. The thermal management challenges arising from close proximity of high-power amplifiers, microwave signal processing circuits, and sensitive resonator structures require innovative thermal design. Furthermore, the increasing importance of software-defined reconfigurable filtering capabilities demands integration of tunable acoustic resonators with digital control circuitry, creating additional design complexity[27].

## Future Research Trajectories and 6G-Era Opportunities

### 6G Wireless Systems and Sub-THz Integrated Circuits

The emergence of 6G research initiatives globally has begun defining new requirements driving acoustic resonator development toward higher frequencies, broader bandwidths, and novel integration paradigms. Unlike 5G systems where spectrum efficiency remained achievable through multi-band filtering and advanced signal processing, 6G concepts increasingly emphasize sub-terahertz frequencies (100-300 gigahertz) where new physics and manufacturing approaches become necessary[1][8]. While acoustic resonator operation at such extreme frequencies faces fundamental challenges related to acoustic attenuation in piezoelectric materials and thermal effects, research initiatives are exploring promising pathways including integration of sub-terahertz sources with acoustic front-end modules through heterogeneous integration.

The 6G wireless era will likely demand integrated photonic-acoustic-microwave systems where resonators serve not merely as passive filters but as active signal processing elements enabling dynamic spectrum adaptation and reconfigurable beamforming. Hybrid systems combining millimeter-wave resonators with optical frequency conversion and detection enable signal processing pathways impossible with conventional approaches. These vision-level concepts remain largely in research phases, but the trajectory of recent innovations suggests practical 6G prototypes incorporating acoustic resonators operating at 20-40 gigahertz coupled with photonic signal processing could emerge within 2025-2027 timeframe.

### Nonlinear Acoustic Phenomena and Programmable Devices

The discovery of frequency comb generation in high-Q acoustic resonators and recent demonstrations of topological mode transformations through acoustic tuning suggest that nonlinear acoustic phenomena could enable revolutionary signal processing architectures. The ability to generate tunable frequency combs through parametric mixing in compact acoustic resonators could enable highly integrated signal generation and processing systems currently requiring separate oscillator, multiplier, and filter components[31]. Similarly, the control of optical path topology through acoustic Bragg gratings opens opportunities for programmable photonic circuits requiring no mechanical tuning or permanent modifications[27].

Future research should investigate the potential to exploit nonlinear mode coupling, thermal effects, and acoustic streaming phenomena to realize novel signal processing functions currently implemented through distributed component approaches. Machine learning assisted design methodologies could enable optimization of complex resonator geometries for specific nonlinear functions, leveraging computational approaches to navigate the vast design space too large for traditional analytical methods. The integration of programmable resonators with digital control enables software-defined acoustic signal processing, potentially revolutionizing RF front-end architectures toward fully flexible, dynamically reconfigurable systems.

### Materials Innovation and Exotic Piezoelectric Platforms

While scandium-doped aluminum nitride and lithium niobate represent the leading platforms for next-generation resonators, longer-term progress likely requires fundamental materials innovation enabling new combinations of piezoelectric coupling, acoustic losses, temperature stability, and integration potential. Research into polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF)-based resonators offers promise for flexible, wearable acoustic devices, though achieving sufficient quality factors and coupling coefficients remains challenging. Exploration of wurtzite nitrides beyond aluminum nitride and gallium nitride, including boron nitride and mixed alloys, could enable engineered material properties optimized for specific frequency ranges and applications[11][17].

The discovery of two-dimensional materials exhibiting acoustic wave propagation characteristics has opened unexplored research territory. Suspended graphene and transition metal dichalcogenide membranes show potential as ultra-sensitive acoustic transducers and resonators, though scaling to practical frequency ranges and power handling capabilities remains speculative. Integration of 2D materials with conventional piezoelectric substrates through heterogeneous approaches could enable hybrid resonators combining advantages of multiple platforms. While 2D material resonators likely remain in research phases for 5G/6G deployment, their potential for sensing and signal processing applications justifies continued investigation[51].

## Conclusion

The period 2022-2025 has witnessed fundamental advances in acoustic wave resonator and filter technologies reshaping the landscape of RF signal processing across wireless communication systems. Breakthrough material innovations particularly through scandium doping of aluminum nitride, coupled with novel resonator architectures such as hairclip designs and pentagon spiral electrodes, have overcome historical tradeoffs between quality factor and electromechanical coupling coefficient. These advances have enabled performance improvements of 2-3 times relative to conventional approaches, expanding operational frequency ranges to 17-18 gigahertz and beyond while maintaining integrated circuit compatibility and establishing pathways toward sub-terahertz operation.

The convergence of acoustic resonator technology with photonic and microwave systems represents a transformative development enabling hybrid devices and circuits impossible with isolated technologies. Demonstrations of acousto-optic modulators, reconfigurable photonic circuits with acoustic tuning, and integrated photonic-acoustic signal processing suggest that future wireless systems will seamlessly combine acoustic, photonic, and microwave technologies rather than segregating these domains into separate functional blocks. The emergence of acoustic frequency combs and novel topological mode transformations indicates that nonlinear acoustic phenomena could enable revolutionary signal processing capabilities previously considered exclusive to optical systems.

Looking toward 6G-era systems and beyond 2025, the critical research challenges involve extending acoustic resonator operation into sub-terahertz frequencies while maintaining practical fabrication and integration capabilities, developing programmable resonator arrays enabling software-defined signal processing, and integrating acoustic components into fully heterogeneous photonic-microwave-acoustic systems. Materials innovation toward exotic piezoelectric platforms and exploration of 2D materials represent longer-term opportunities for revolutionary advances beyond incremental improvements to established technologies. The continued convergence of materials science, nanotechnology, and photonics with traditional RF engineering will likely characterize the evolution of acoustic resonator technology toward increasingly sophisticated and integrated implementations supporting next-generation wireless communications.

The path forward demands continued interdisciplinary collaboration between materials scientists, device engineers, circuit designers, and systems architects to translate laboratory demonstrations into practical, manufacturable solutions meeting the demanding requirements of next-generation wireless networks. The accumulating evidence from 2022-2025 research suggests that acoustic wave resonators and filters will remain central to signal processing in wireless systems for the foreseeable future, with their importance expanding rather than diminishing as spectrum congestion and performance requirements continue their relentless escalation.
