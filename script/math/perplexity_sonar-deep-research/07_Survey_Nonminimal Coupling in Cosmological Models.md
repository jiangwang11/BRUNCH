# Nonminimal Coupling in Cosmological Models: A Comprehensive Review of Recent Observational and Theoretical Advances (2022-2025)

Recent cosmological observations from the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument, Planck satellite, and Atacama Cosmology Telescope have increasingly pointed toward significant deviations from the standard Lambda Cold Dark Matter model, with mounting evidence suggesting that scalar field theories nonminimally coupled to gravity provide superior fits to observational data compared to minimally coupled alternatives[1][4][10]. The discovery that current cosmological data strongly favor quintessence models featuring nonminimal coupling to the Ricci curvature scalar, manifested through coupling terms of the form \(\xi\varphi^2 R\) in the action, has profound implications for our understanding of dark energy and the fundamental nature of gravitational interactions[1][4]. This coupling mechanism leads to time-varying gravitational constants on cosmological scales and predicts fifth forces on smaller scales, creating an intriguing tension between cosmological observations and local tests of general relativity that demands careful theoretical and observational investigation[1][10]. The present review synthesizes recent advances in nonminimal coupling cosmology spanning from 2022 to early 2025, examining both the theoretical frameworks that support these models and the observational evidence from multiple independent cosmological probes that increasingly favor such couplings over conventional minimally coupled scenarios.

## Introduction to Nonminimal Coupling in Gravity and Cosmology

Nonminimal coupling between scalar fields and gravitational degrees of freedom represents a natural extension of general relativity that emerges in multiple theoretical contexts spanning from quantum field theory to string theory and quantum gravity frameworks[2][3][7]. The concept of nonminimal coupling fundamentally deviates from the minimal coupling prescription where matter fields interact only through the metric tensor, introducing instead direct interactions between matter fields and curvature invariants such as the Ricci scalar \(R\), the Ricci tensor \(R_{\mu\nu}\), or higher-order curvature combinations[7][13][16]. This theoretical generalization has received renewed attention in recent years because contemporary cosmological observations reveal persistent tensions in the Lambda Cold Dark Matter model, particularly the Hubble tension indicating a discrepancy between local and early-universe measurements of the Hubble constant, anomalies in the growth rate of cosmic structures parameterized by the sigma-eight tension, and hints of evolving dark energy rather than a cosmological constant[1][10][44]. The nonminimal coupling framework offers a theoretically motivated approach to addressing these observational challenges while maintaining consistency with high-energy physics principles and quantum gravity considerations[3][7][19][22].

The physical consequences of nonminimal coupling in gravitational systems manifest across multiple scales and physical regimes. At cosmological scales, nonminimal coupling modifies the expansion history of the universe and the evolution of dark energy, allowing for equation of state parameters that cross the phantom divide where the equation of state parameter transitions from \(w > -1\) to \(w < -1\), a behavior impossible in minimally coupled scalar field models[4][10][25]. On intermediate scales relevant to large-scale structure formation, nonminimal coupling introduces modifications to the growth of cosmic structure and affects both the growth rate and the effective gravitational constant relevant for structure formation, as parameterized by the function \(\mu(z)\) which measures deviations from general relativity[4][57]. At local gravitational scales within galaxies and the solar system, nonminimal coupling generically predicts additional long-range scalar forces that mediate gravitational-strength interactions, commonly referred to as fifth forces[1][31][34]. The tension between these different observational regimes creates a profound puzzle: if quintessence with nonminimal coupling drives cosmic acceleration, why do we not observe the fifth forces predicted by such models at local scales? This puzzle has motivated the development of screening mechanisms that suppress fifth forces in high-density environments while preserving them in the cosmological context[31][34].

The mathematical framework describing nonminimal coupling typically begins with an action principle where the Lagrangian density contains interaction terms between scalar fields and curvature invariants[1][2][3][4][7]. The simplest and most commonly studied form involves a coupling of a scalar field \(\varphi\) to the Ricci scalar \(R\) through a term proportional to \(\xi\varphi^2 R\) in the action, where \(\xi\) is a dimensionless coupling constant[1][4]. More general frameworks, including the effective field theory approach to dark energy, accommodate arbitrary nonminimal couplings through systematic expansions in terms of independent field operators and their derivatives[9][20]. The scalar-tensor theories of gravity, exemplified by Brans-Dicke theory and its modern generalizations including Horndeski theories, constitute the most thoroughly studied class of theories implementing nonminimal coupling[8][11][18][20][38]. These frameworks have been extensively constrained by observations of gravitational waves from binary neutron star mergers, which place stringent bounds on the speed of gravitational waves and consequently on the allowed parameter space of nonminimal coupling models[18][40].

## Observational Evidence from Recent Cosmological Data

The 2024-2025 release of data from the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument represents a watershed moment for cosmological observations, providing the first high-significance indication that nonminimal coupling of gravity may be necessary to explain the observed cosmic expansion history[1][4][10][56]. Previous baryon acoustic oscillation measurements, while suggesting deviations from the standard cosmological model, were not sufficiently constraining to definitively favor any particular alternative theory[1][4]. However, when combining DESI baryon acoustic oscillation data with complementary observations from Planck satellite measurements of the cosmic microwave background, Atacama Cosmology Telescope lensing measurements, and multiple supernova surveys, the statistical preference for nonminimally coupled dark energy becomes decisive[1][4][57]. Wolf et al. in their August 2025 Physical Review Letters publication conducting a comprehensive Bayesian model comparison found that nonminimally coupled quintessence is strongly favored compared to both the standard cosmological constant and minimally coupled quintessence models across all tested data combinations, with Bayes factors exceeding five on the Jeffrey's scale indicating decisive evidence[1]. This result represents a remarkable shift in the observational support for theoretical frameworks that have been developed over decades but remained largely speculative.

The specific pattern of observational support for nonminimal coupling reveals important clues about the underlying physics driving cosmic acceleration. Ye et al., analyzing the DESI baryon acoustic oscillation measurements within the framework of Horndeski gravity and other scalar-tensor theories, found that the DESI observations can rule out simple quintessence models by indicating a crossing of the phantom divide—a transition from ordinary dark energy with equation of state parameter \(w > -1\) to phantom-like behavior with \(w < -1\)—at redshift approximately 0.3[10]. Such phantom crossing behavior is impossible within minimally coupled quintessence models but emerges naturally when nonminimal coupling to gravity is included, as the coupling introduces an effective back-reaction that drives the field into the phantom regime during matter domination[1][4]. The combination of multiple independent datasets, including baryon acoustic oscillation measurements at various redshifts from DESI, cosmic microwave background temperature and polarization measurements from Planck, lensing measurements from the Atacama Cosmology Telescope, and supernova distance measurements from Dark Energy Survey Year Five observations, provides complementary constraints on the dark energy equation of state and its evolution[1][4][57]. The fact that nonminimal coupling is favored not by any single dataset alone but by the specific combination of early and late-time cosmological probes suggests a systematic deviation from Lambda Cold Dark Matter that nonminimal coupling naturally explains.

The modified gravitational constant inferred from nonminimally coupled dark energy models represents another striking observational signature. When fitting nonminimally coupled dark energy models to the combined DESI, Planck, and Atacama Cosmology Telescope dataset supplemented with Dark Energy Survey Year Five supernovae, the inferred effective gravitational constant becomes significantly enhanced, with \(\mu(z=0) = 1.77^{+0.23}_{-0.18}\), representing a deviation from the general relativity prediction of \(\mu = 1\) that amounts to 4.3 standard deviations[4][57]. This enhancement indicates that gravity is effectively stronger in the Newtonian regime than predicted by classical general relativity, a consequence of the nonminimal coupling imprinting modifications on gravitational clustering at low redshifts where the coupling becomes significant[4][57]. Such modifications to the effective gravitational constant in the matter perturbation equations have profound implications for structure formation, affecting the growth rate of cosmic structures and the matter power spectrum on large scales[33][36][57]. Furthermore, the theoretical requirement that nonminimally coupled models must explain these observations while simultaneously passing stringent constraints from gravitational wave observations, which restrict the allowed strength of nonminimal couplings through constraints on the speed of gravitational wave propagation, creates a delicate balance that has motivated careful refinement of the parameter space[18][40].

## Theoretical Frameworks for Nonminimal Coupling

The theoretical foundations for nonminimal coupling in cosmology rest on several independent pillars spanning fundamental physics considerations and observational requirements[1][2][3][4][7]. From the perspective of quantum field theory and renormalization group analysis, nonminimal coupling of scalar fields to the Ricci curvature scalar appears naturally when one performs a perturbative expansion around a curved spacetime background, with the coupling strength determined by the requirements of renormalizability and asymptotic properties at different energy scales[2][7]. The scalar-tensor class of theories, which includes Brans-Dicke theory as the simplest example and Horndeski theories as the most general scalar-tensor theories involving a single scalar field without Ostrogradsky ghosts, provide systematic frameworks for studying nonminimal coupling[8][11][18][20][38]. In Brans-Dicke theory, the scalar field couples universally to all matter through a redefinition of the gravitational coupling strength, encoded in the parameter \(\omega\) which characterizes the strength of the nonminimal coupling[8][11]. The generalization to Horndeski theories permits more complex coupling structures where the scalar field couples not only to the Ricci scalar but also to the Einstein tensor, the Gauss-Bonnet invariant, or higher-order curvature combinations, with each coupling channel contributing distinct physical effects[18][20][40].

Recent theoretical developments have explored nonminimal coupling within the broader framework of effective field theory of dark energy, which provides a model-independent parametrization of modifications to general relativity arising from additional light scalar degrees of freedom[9][20]. This effective field theory approach represents any dark energy or modified gravity model with a single scalar degree of freedom through an action involving coefficients that depend on the cosmic expansion history, allowing systematic exploration of the parameter space without committing to any particular fundamental theory[9][20]. Within this framework, nonminimal coupling corresponds to specific choices of the effective field theory coefficients that relate the scalar field to spacetime curvature[9][20]. The effective field theory methodology has proven particularly valuable for assessing the consistency of various dark energy models with observational constraints while remaining agnostic about the underlying microphysical origin of these couplings[9]. Constraints derived from various observational probes, including baryon acoustic oscillations, cosmic microwave background measurements, large-scale structure observations, and weak gravitational lensing, have been systematically encoded into the parameter space of the effective field theory, revealing which combinations of coupling strengths remain viable given current observational precision[9].

The mechanism by which nonminimal coupling generates phantom crossing and evolving dark energy properties has received detailed theoretical scrutiny. During radiation domination when the Ricci scalar \(R \simeq 0\), a nonminimally coupled scalar field evolves identically to a minimally coupled field, maintaining an equation of state parameter \(w \simeq -1\)[4]. However, once matter domination begins, the nonzero value of the Ricci scalar during matter domination creates an effective back-reaction that drives the scalar field into the phantom regime with \(w < -1\), as the nonminimal coupling term effectively acts as an additional contribution to the scalar field's equation of motion[4]. This mechanism is purely gravitational in origin, requiring no exotic matter or phenomenological adjustments, and represents a clean realization of phantom crossing within general relativity with minimally modified degrees of freedom[4][25]. As the universe transitions to dark energy domination, the rapid decrease in the Ricci scalar causes the equation of state to transition back across the phantom divide, creating a distinctive evolution pattern in the dark energy equation of state that matches the observational preference revealed by DESI and other recent datasets[1][4][57].

The Jordan and Einstein frame duality provides an important theoretical insight for understanding nonminimal coupling[38]. Physical theories can be equivalently expressed in the Jordan frame where the scalar field multiplies the Ricci scalar in the action (nonminimal coupling in the Einstein sense) or in the Einstein frame where the scalar field couples minimally to a redefined metric after appropriate field transformations[38]. While the Einstein and Jordan frames are physically equivalent and predict identical observable quantities, they provide different geometric interpretations, with the Jordan frame rendering the equivalence principle manifest through geodesic motion and the Einstein frame making the field equations resemble Einstein's equations with additional source terms[38]. The choice of frame influences which formulation is most convenient for physical calculations and interpretation, but cannot affect any observable quantity[38]. Recent work applying Jordan frame methods to the cosmological analysis of nonminimal coupling data found that the Jordan frame provides a more transparent description of the effective gravitational constant modifications and their impact on structure formation[1][4][38].

## Scalar Field Models and Quintessence with Nonminimal Coupling

The simplest and most thoroughly investigated realization of nonminimal coupling in cosmology involves a single scalar field—the quintessence field—with a polynomial potential and nonminimal coupling to the Ricci scalar[1][2][4][6]. The action for such a system takes the form \(S = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g} \left[\frac{1}{2\kappa^2}(1 + \xi\varphi^2)R - \frac{1}{2}(\partial\varphi)^2 - V(\varphi)\right]\) where \(\kappa^2 = 8\pi G\), \(\xi\) is the nonminimal coupling strength, and \(V(\varphi)\) is the scalar field potential[1][4]. The analysis of Wolf et al. examined three distinct potential forms: a linear potential, an exponential potential, and a quadratic potential, finding that observational data from DESI, Planck, and Atacama Cosmology Telescope surveys favor concave potentials with stronger curvature, consistent with quadratic or polynomial potentials rather than exponential forms[1]. This preference for concave potentials reflects the requirement that the nonminimal coupling drive rapid evolution in the dark energy equation of state within the redshift range where most observational constraints are concentrated, approximately \(0.2 < z < 2\), where exponential potentials produce insufficient variation[1][57].

The oscillating scalar field acting as dark matter coupled nonminimally to quintessence dark energy represents another important class of scalar field models receiving recent theoretical development[2][45]. In this scenario, a rapidly oscillating scalar field with mass parameter \(m\) and quadratic potential \(V(\phi) = \frac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2\) behaves as pressure-less cold dark matter when \(m >> H\) where \(H\) is the Hubble parameter, while a second scalar field with either quintessence or phantom-like properties acts as dark energy[2][45]. The interaction between these two scalar fields manifests through a nonminimal coupling term in the Lagrangian, typically of the form \(\mathcal{L}_{int} \propto q\phi^2\psi^2\) where \(q\) is a dimensionless coupling constant[2][45]. The analysis of such coupled systems revealed that the nonminimal coupling strength determines whether dark energy becomes clustered or unclustered during gravitational collapse of overdense regions, with strong coupling favoring clustered dark energy configurations[2][45]. This behavior contrasts with the unclustered dark energy typically assumed in cosmological perturbation theory, suggesting that nonminimal coupling in the dark sector could have observable consequences for the formation of structure at scales where dark energy density perturbations become important[2][45].

Chaotic inflation models with nonminimal coupling to gravity have experienced renewed attention following recent releases of cosmic microwave background data from the Planck and Atacama Cosmology Telescope experiments[6][50]. The simplest chaotic inflation model based on a mass-squared potential \(M^2|\Phi|^2\) predicts an unfortunate combination: a spectral index \(n_s \approx 0.96\) consistent with observations but an unacceptably large tensor-to-scalar ratio \(r \approx 0.16\) that current observations disfavor[6]. However, including nonminimal coupling to gravity through a term proportional to \(\xi|\Phi|^2 R^2\) in the action significantly reduces the predicted tensor-to-scalar ratio, bringing it into accord with observational constraints[6]. Keus and King's November 2025 analysis of chaotic inflation with nonminimal coupling demonstrated that inclusion of radiative corrections introducing a logarithmic potential, combined with the nonminimal coupling and a small quartic coupling correction, yields predictions consistent with both Planck and recent Atacama Cosmology Telescope datasets[6]. This reconstruction of observational viability for chaotic inflation through nonminimal coupling demonstrates how such couplings can serve as crucial ingredients in reconciling inflationary predictions with precision cosmological observations.

The parameter space constraints on scalar field potentials and coupling strengths have become increasingly tight with each new release of cosmological data[1][4][9][57]. For the simplest nonminimally coupled quintessence model, the combination of DESI baryon acoustic oscillation data, Planck cosmic microwave background observations, and Atacama Cosmology Telescope lensing measurements constrains the nonminimal coupling strength to \(\xi > 10^{13}\) GeV in some analyses, indicating that if nonminimal coupling exists, its effects must be subtle and nearly perturbative relative to minimal coupling[3]. Conversely, other analyses accounting for rapid evolution of the dark energy equation of state find coupling strengths that produce non-negligible deviations from general relativity on cosmological scales[1][4][57]. This apparent tension between different analyses stems from the complicated interplay between the coupling strength, the potential form, and the expansion history: stronger coupling drives more rapid equation of state evolution but also produces stronger modifications to structure formation[1][4][57]. The resolution of this tension through careful analysis of the complete observational dataset and thorough exploration of the parameter space remains an active area of research[1][4][9][57].

## Modified Gravity Theories with Nonminimal Curvature-Matter Couplings

Beyond scalar field models, a broader class of modified gravity theories introduces nonminimal coupling between curvature invariants and matter fields, moving beyond the scalar-tensor framework to encompass more exotic gravitational theories[7][19][22][32][36]. The generalized f(R) theories extending the Einstein-Hilbert action to arbitrary functions of the Ricci scalar, when supplemented with nonminimal coupling between the curvature scalar and the matter Lagrangian density, create a large family of modified gravity scenarios with rich phenomenology[3][7][19][22]. These f(R, L_m) theories, where the gravitational action depends both on the Ricci scalar R and the matter Lagrangian density L_m, naturally incorporate nonminimal coupling through the explicit dependence on matter fields in the gravitational action[7][19][22]. The equations of motion for such theories contain novel terms arising from the functional derivatives with respect to matter fields, leading to non-conservation of the energy-momentum tensor and energy exchange between matter and geometry[7][22].

The theoretical investigation of particle creation in modified gravity with nonminimal curvature-matter coupling reveals fascinating connections to thermodynamics and cosmological acceleration[7][22]. Lobo, Harko, and Pinto demonstrated that gravitationally induced particle creation arising from the non-conservation of the stress-energy tensor in nonminimally coupled theories can, under appropriate conditions, drive late-time cosmic acceleration through negative creation pressure without requiring an explicit cosmological constant term[7][22]. This mechanism grounds cosmic acceleration in a fundamental thermodynamic process—particle creation in the expanding universe—rather than introducing an ad hoc dark energy component[7][22]. The formulation of this process using irreversible thermodynamics of open systems provides a rigorous framework for analyzing particle creation rates, creation pressures, and entropy production, with the constraint that the generalized second law of thermodynamics must be satisfied[7][22]. Such thermodynamic approaches to nonminimally coupled gravity extend beyond quintessence to encompass more general modifications to gravity, offering alternative physical mechanisms for generating cosmic acceleration.

The teleparallel formulation of gravity, which describes gravitational effects through torsion rather than curvature, has been extended to include nonminimal coupling with promising results[21][32]. In teleparallel gravity, the fundamental field variables are tetrad fields \(e_a^\mu\) related to the metric through \(g^{\mu\nu} = \eta_{ab}e^a_\mu e^b_\nu\), with the gravitational action depending on the torsion scalar \(T\) rather than the Ricci scalar[21][32]. Nonminimal coupling in this framework takes the form of interactions between scalar fields and the torsion scalar, as well as couplings to the boundary term that emerges in the teleparallel formulation[21][32]. Kadam, Duchaniya, and Mishra's detailed analysis of nonminimally coupled scalar fields in teleparallel gravity revealed that such models accommodate diverse cosmic expansion histories while maintaining freedom in the form of the scalar potential[21]. The phase-space analysis of the dynamical system derived from teleparallel gravity with nonminimal coupling showed multiple stable critical points corresponding to different cosmological eras, with dark energy domination achieved through the coupling mechanism rather than a bare cosmological constant[21].

The implications of nonminimal curvature-matter coupling for Big Bang nucleosynthesis provide important early-universe constraints on modified gravity[32][35]. The modifications to the Friedmann equations introduced by f(T, L_m) gravity and related theories with nonminimal coupling alter the expansion rate during the nucleosynthesis era, which in turn affects the neutron-proton freeze-out temperature and the predicted abundances of light elements[32]. By comparing predicted primordial abundances of deuterium, helium-4, and lithium-7 with observational measurements, stringent constraints can be derived on the parameter space of modified gravity theories with nonminimal coupling[32]. The analysis of three representative f(T, L_m) models revealed that Big Bang nucleosynthesis observations provide the most stringent constraints from lithium abundances, restricting the allowed parameter ranges to narrow intervals[32]. These early-universe constraints prove complementary to the late-time cosmological constraints from baryon acoustic oscillations and other large-scale structure observations, creating a comprehensive picture of allowed modified gravity parameter space spanning from the early universe through the present epoch.

## Constraining Nonminimal Coupling through Gravitational Wave Observations

The detection of gravitational waves from the merger of neutron stars in GW170817 and associated gamma-ray burst observation GRB170817A provided unprecedented constraints on scalar-tensor theories and modified gravity theories with nonminimal coupling[18][40]. These observations allowed precision measurements of the speed of gravitational wave propagation relative to the speed of light, determining that \(c_T/c = 1\) to within approximately one part in \(10^{15}\) across the gravitational wave frequency band[18][40]. Since scalar-tensor theories and models with nonminimal derivative coupling generally predict modifications to the speed of gravitational wave propagation, with \(c_T \neq c\) for generic parameter choices, the gravitational wave constraints rule out large regions of parameter space for such theories[18][40]. Gong, Papantonopoulos, and Yi systematically analyzed the implications of gravitational wave speed constraints for Horndeski theories featuring nonminimal derivative coupling of scalar fields to the Einstein tensor, finding that the mass scale characterizing the strength of non-minimal derivative coupling must satisfy \(10^{15}\text{ GeV} \gg M \gtrsim 2 \times 10^{-35}\text{ GeV}[18]. This enormous range of allowed mass scales arises because gravitational wave speed constraints become stringent only when the nonminimal coupling produces observable effects on gravitational wave propagation, which occurs only for coupling scales approaching either the Planck scale or the inflationary scale.

The implications of gravitational wave constraints for cosmological models extend beyond the direct speed of propagation measurements to encompass subtle effects on the evolution of the coupling strength and its impact on primordial gravitational waves. Models featuring non-minimal derivative coupling between a scalar field and the Einstein tensor can evade gravitational wave constraints if the Einstein-frame gravitational coupling remains sufficiently close to its general relativity value[18][20][40]. Careful analysis revealed that certain subclasses of Horndeski theories, particularly those with photon-Galileon couplings inspired by higher-dimensional Kaluza-Klein reductions, can maintain luminal gravitational wave propagation even with significant nonminimal coupling present[23]. These geometrically motivated couplings preserve the speed of gravitational wave propagation at unity while allowing scalar field modifications to gravity that could be observable in other ways, such as through effects on large-scale structure formation and cosmic expansion[23][40].

The generation of primordial gravitational waves in inflationary models with nonminimal coupling has been analyzed in detail for both conventional slow-roll inflation and more exotic scenarios[6][27][30]. The inclusion of nonminimal coupling to gravity in chaotic inflation models reduces the predicted tensor-to-scalar ratio, bringing models that would otherwise be ruled out into accord with current observational constraints[6][30]. For chromo-natural inflation, where an axion-like particle couples to an SU(2) gauge sector through a Chern-Simons term, nonminimal coupling to the Einstein tensor provides a mechanism for suppressing the large tensor modes that conflicted with cosmic microwave background observations while preserving the distinctive chiral gravitational wave signal predicted at small scales[27]. This suppression of tensor modes through nonminimal coupling represents a promising avenue for reconciling axion inflation models with observations, potentially allowing such models to produce detectable gravitational wave signatures while remaining consistent with cosmic microwave background constraints[27][30].

## Screening Mechanisms and Local Gravity Tests

A critical puzzle emerges from the combination of cosmological observations favoring nonminimal coupling with stringent local tests of general relativity: if nonminimal coupling produces observable effects on cosmic scales, why do we not observe the fifth forces predicted by such couplings at solar system and terrestrial scales[1][31][34]? The resolution to this apparent tension rests on screening mechanisms that suppress fifth forces in high-density environments while preserving them in low-density cosmological settings[31][34]. The chameleon mechanism provides one systematic realization of such screening, where the effective potential for the scalar field depends on the local matter density, creating a density-dependent scalar field mass that becomes very large in high-density regions, confining the scalar field to the minimum of the effective potential and suppressing its spatial gradients[31][34]. In low-density environments including cosmological voids, the effective potential is shallower, the scalar field mass is smaller, and the field can vary spatially, mediating long-range forces[31][34].

The symmetron screening mechanism offers an alternative approach based on symmetry restoration in high-density environments[31][34]. In symmetron models, the bare coupling between the scalar field and matter vanishes when the matter density exceeds a critical threshold due to Z2 symmetry restoration, completely suppressing the fifth force in dense regions while allowing gravitational-strength forces in the cosmological background and low-density regions[31][34]. These screening mechanisms rely on nonlinear self-interactions in the scalar field potential or coupling function, making classical fifth-force searches ineffective at discovering screened scalar fields since the classical force law predicted by linear perturbation theory does not apply in the high-density regimes accessible to laboratory tests[31][34]. The development of novel fifth-force search strategies specifically designed to probe screened scalar fields, including tests of the equivalence principle using lunar retroreflector data, measurements of gravitational effects using test masses in ultra-high vacuum chambers, and astrophysical constraints from stellar oscillations and neutron star properties, represents an active area of experimental physics[31]. Current bounds from these diverse approaches constrain the effective scalar field coupling strength and mass in the low-density limit, with the strongest constraints emerging from equivalence principle tests and stellar physics considerations[31][34].

The theoretical consistency of screening mechanisms with cosmological observations of nonminimal coupling remains an ongoing topic of investigation[31][34][42]. For cosmological models driven by nonminimally coupled quintessence, the screening mechanism must suppress the fifth force sufficiently to evade local tests while preserving the modifications to cosmic expansion and structure formation observable at large scales[31][34]. The interplay between screening efficacy and cosmological observability creates complex constraints on the scalar field potential and coupling strength[31][34]. Recent work has explored whether quintessence-like scalar fields with appropriate screening mechanisms can simultaneously explain cosmic acceleration, produce the observable growth rate modifications inferred from structure formation measurements, and satisfy all local fifth-force constraints[31][34][42]. The results indicate that achieving this simultaneous satisfaction of all constraints requires either fine-tuning of model parameters or introduction of additional physics beyond simple scalar field models[31][34][42].

## Implications for Cosmological Tensions and Dark Matter-Dark Energy Interactions

The Hubble tension—the persistent discrepancy between measurements of the expansion rate from early-universe observations using cosmic microwave background temperature and polarization data combined with Big Bang nucleosynthesis constraints, versus late-time measurements from supernovae and gravitational lensing—has motivated investigations into whether dark energy or modified gravity could play a role[10][44][47][55][56]. Several theoretical scenarios involving nonminimal coupling have been proposed as potential resolutions[10][44][47][55]. Ye et al. demonstrated that nonminimally coupled gravity with phantom crossing provides an effective mechanism for enhancing the late-time expansion rate, thereby alleviating the Hubble tension when combined with cosmic microwave background constraints[10][56]. Pitrou and Uzan showed that extending the standard cosmological model with a scalar interaction in the dark matter sector—separate from the dark energy nonminimal coupling—can ameliorate the Hubble tension while maintaining consistency with Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions and solar system tests of gravity[47]. The key insight is that modifications to the gravitational interaction within the dark matter sector can alter the low-redshift expansion rate without affecting early-universe physics, creating the flexibility needed to resolve the Hubble tension through modified late-time physics[47].

The possibility of direct couplings between dark matter and dark energy through nonminimal interactions has received theoretical development and observational investigation[43][46]. In coupled quintessence models where dark energy experiences an interaction with the dark matter component through energy exchange, the expansion history and structure formation predictions differ systematically from the standard picture[43][46]. Böhmer et al. investigated dark energy and dark matter coupling through exponential quintessence with energy exchange mechanisms, finding that such couplings can produce accelerated scaling solutions where the ratio of dark matter to dark energy density remains approximately constant over extended periods of cosmic evolution[46]. More recent work by Giovanakis examined coupled quintessence inspired by warm inflation phenomenology, where a dissipative coupling term similar to inflaton-radiation interactions in the early universe mediates energy transfer between dark energy and dark matter[43]. These coupled dark sector models address the cosmological coincidence problem—the question of why dark matter and dark energy densities are comparable at present despite evolving independently in uncoupled models—through mechanism where the coupling enforces an approximate scaling between the two components[43][46].

The observational constraints on dark matter-dark energy interactions from combined cosmological datasets have become increasingly stringent. Recent analyses combining baryon acoustic oscillation measurements with cosmic microwave background and supernova data reveal that while direct coupling in the dark sector cannot be ruled out, models with particular coupling forms can be distinguished and some parameter ranges are disfavored[43][46]. The effective field theory framework for dark energy interactions permits systematic exploration of coupling parameter space, with constraints deriving from the requirement that cosmological perturbations remain stable against exponential growth and that the sound speed of propagating modes remains real and less than the speed of light[9][20]. The interplay between dark sector couplings and nonminimal coupling of dark energy to gravity creates a complex multi-parameter space where observational resolution between competing models requires precision measurements across multiple independent cosmological probes[9][43][46].

## Frontier Developments: Quantum Gravity Perspectives and Transplanckian Physics

Recent theoretical developments have begun exploring how nonminimal coupling emerges naturally from quantum gravity frameworks, providing a deeper microscopic foundation for the phenomenological models studied in cosmological observations[55]. In the group field theory approach to quantum gravity, where spacetime geometry emerges from quantum entanglement of fundamental geometric degrees of freedom, cosmological solutions coupled with scalar matter fields naturally develop nonminimal couplings between matter and geometry[55]. Oriti and collaborators demonstrated that quantum gravity dynamics in group field theory condensate cosmology can produce late-time cosmic acceleration through a phantom phase without introducing any phantom field, with the phantom behavior emerging purely from quantum gravitational effects[55]. The emergence of phantom crossing from quantum gravity through the dynamics of coherent condensate states provides a striking realization of how nonminimal coupling arises from fundamental physics rather than being introduced phenomenologically[55].

The relationship between effective field theory descriptions of dark energy and the swampland conjectures from quantum gravity offers another perspective on nonminimal coupling[39][42]. The swampland program proposes criteria that consistent effective field theories must satisfy if they are to arise as the low-energy limit of quantum gravity, imposing constraints on the allowed form of scalar field potentials and their coupling to gravity[39][42]. Recent analysis examining whether nonminimally coupled dark energy models satisfy the swampland conjectures revealed that consistency with quantum gravity requirements restricts the allowed parameter space, particularly concerning the field excursion and potential gradient[39]. The tension between observational evidence favoring nonminimal coupling and the restrictions imposed by swampland conjectures motivates careful investigation of which nonminimal coupling models remain consistent with quantum gravity principles[39][42].

Higher-derivative scalar-vector-tensor theories emerging from Kaluza-Klein reductions of higher-dimensional Horndeski theories represent another frontier direction, where nonminimal couplings between scalars, vectors, and tensors arise naturally from dimensional reduction of fundamental theories[23]. The construction of four-dimensional theories incorporating photon-Galileon couplings derived from five-dimensional Horndeski theory provides a systematic framework for exploring couplings of dark energy to other particle sectors beyond gravity[23]. Such extensions to the dark sector coupling structure could leave signatures in precision measurements of cosmic expansion and structure formation while maintaining consistency with gravitational wave speed constraints[23].

## Conclusion and Future Research Directions

The convergence of observational evidence from multiple independent cosmological probes toward nonminimal coupling of dark energy and dark matter to gravity represents a profound development in modern cosmology. The strong statistical preference revealed by recent DESI, Planck, and Atacama Cosmology Telescope observations for nonminimally coupled quintessence compared to both the standard cosmological constant and minimally coupled alternatives signals that future standard models of cosmology may necessarily incorporate such couplings as fundamental features[1][4][10]. The resolution of the puzzle posed by missing fifth forces at local scales likely involves sophisticated screening mechanisms that require careful additional theoretical development and experimental investigation[31][34]. The implications of nonminimal coupling extend throughout cosmology, from early-universe inflation to late-time cosmic acceleration, potentially providing a unified framework for understanding diverse observational phenomena within modified gravity and dark energy.

Looking toward the remainder of the 2020s, several critical research directions will determine whether nonminimal coupling represents a genuine feature of fundamental physics or merely a computational artifact of statistical analyses applied to incomplete observational datasets. First, the combination of next-generation baryon acoustic oscillation surveys including the final Data Release of DESI, supplemented by redshift space distortion measurements and large-scale structure observations, will dramatically increase the precision of constraints on dark energy evolution and modifications to the growth of cosmic structure, enabling definitive tests distinguishing nonminimal coupling models from alternatives[1][4][56][57]. Second, the development and deployment of improved local tests of gravity sensitive to fifth forces mediated by light scalar fields, including equivalence principle tests using space-based platforms, laboratory-scale tests with quantum-enhanced precision, and astrophysical probes using compact objects, will constrain the allowed parameter space of screened nonminimal coupling models and test whether observationally favored cosmological models can evade local gravity constraints[31][34]. Third, the theoretical unification of nonminimal coupling phenomenology with quantum gravity frameworks, particularly through development of nonminimal coupling models emerging naturally from loop quantum cosmology and group field theory, will establish whether these couplings represent fundamental features of quantum gravity or merely effective descriptions of other physics[7][22][55].

The field of nonminimal coupling cosmology stands at an inflection point where observational advances are beginning to resolve long-standing theoretical questions. The mechanisms by which scalar fields acquire nonminimal couplings to gravity, the fundamental nature of dark energy and its interactions with other cosmic constituents, and the ultimate validity of general relativity as the correct framework for cosmology remain among the most profound questions in physics. Recent observational developments have transformed these questions from matters of academic curiosity into pressing empirical questions demanding both theoretical refinement and experimental investigation. The next several years promise remarkable advances in our understanding of cosmic gravity and dark energy through the combination of improved observations, refined theoretical frameworks, and deeper connections to fundamental physics. Whether nonminimal coupling represents a clue pointing toward deeper physical principles or a statistical fluctuation in the data will only become clear through sustained interdisciplinary effort combining observational astronomy, theoretical physics, and experimental tests of gravity across diverse regimes from laboratory scales to cosmological distances.
