# Dynamics and Control in Delay-Induced Predator-Prey Models: A Comprehensive Review of Recent Advances (2022-2025)

Time delays represent a fundamental feature of ecological systems, introducing temporal lags between predator and prey interactions that profoundly alter population dynamics and system stability. This comprehensive review synthesizes recent advances in understanding and controlling delay-induced phenomena in predator-prey models, with particular emphasis on bifurcation theory, stability analysis, and control strategies emerging from 2022 to 2025. Recent research has demonstrated that time delays can either stabilize or destabilize ecological coexistence depending on their magnitude and interaction with other system parameters, leading to the emergence of oscillatory behaviors, spatial pattern formation, and complex bifurcation structures that fundamentally differ from non-delayed systems. The control of these delay-induced phenomena has emerged as a critical research frontier, with novel controller designs enabling ecosystem managers to regulate population dynamics and maintain desired equilibrium states. This review examines the mathematical foundations underlying delayed predator-prey models, catalogs the primary methodological approaches for analyzing their dynamics, synthesizes findings from bifurcation and stability analyses, and identifies emerging research directions that bridge theoretical advances with practical applications in conservation and resource management.

## Mathematical Foundations and System Formulations

The incorporation of time delays into predator-prey models originates from recognition that ecological processes require finite time for completion, whether through prey maturation, gestation periods in predator populations, or nutrient conversion dynamics. The classical delayed predator-prey system extends the Lotka-Volterra framework by introducing temporal lags that reflect biological reality while introducing significant mathematical complexity in analyzing system behavior. Functional differential equations, particularly delay differential equations (DDEs), serve as the primary mathematical framework for studying these systems[1][3]. The delayed Leslie-Gower predator-prey model has received substantial attention in recent literature, wherein two distinct delay parameters capture different biological processes: prey population density feedback through prey growth rate and predator population feedback through predator death and reproduction rates[6]. Mathematical analysis of such systems requires specialized techniques including eigenvalue analysis, characteristic equation evaluation, and Hopf bifurcation theory rather than simple equilibrium point analysis employed in non-delayed systems[3][6].

Contemporary research increasingly incorporates multiple ecological mechanisms simultaneously into delayed frameworks, creating substantially more realistic but mathematically complex models. A particularly significant advancement involves integrating the Allee effect, which describes reduced reproduction rates at low population densities, alongside fear-induced behavioral changes in prey and cooperative hunting among predators within delayed spatiotemporal contexts[1][16]. The spatiotemporal delayed predator-prey model of El Khateeb et al. incorporates Holling type II functional responses with cooperative predation, toxicity with delayed effects, and both nonlinear fishing pressure on prey and linear fishing pressure on predators, requiring partial differential equation analysis alongside delay differential equation techniques[1]. Such complex formulations reveal that system stability maintains integrity below critical delay thresholds, beyond which oscillatory dynamics emerge, while diffusion effects can either stabilize populations or generate heterogeneous spatial patterns including Turing structures and predator-prey spatial segregation[1]. The incorporation of nonlocal delay mechanisms represents another frontier, where the history of population dynamics across past time intervals rather than discrete delay points influences current dynamics[25][49].

The mathematical treatment of distributed delays rather than discrete delays has emerged as a more realistic representation of biological processes where the temporal lag incorporates a continuum of past influences[39][42]. Distributed delay models employ integrodifferential equations rather than standard delay differential equations, capturing the biological reality that predator dietary conversion or prey maturation occurs across extended periods rather than at specific moments[42]. Analysis of such systems requires sophisticated techniques from functional analysis and demonstrates that systems with distributed delays can exhibit larger stability regimes compared to discrete delay models, suggesting that dependence on multiple past instances provides superior ecological stability[39].

## Bifurcation Analysis and Stability Characterization

Hopf bifurcation represents the dominant bifurcation phenomenon in delay-induced predator-prey systems, marking the transition from stable coexistence equilibria to oscillatory population dynamics as delay parameters cross critical thresholds[3][6][7][10][13]. The existence of Hopf bifurcations can be rigorously established through characteristic equation analysis, where complex eigenvalues cross the imaginary axis as delay parameters vary[6]. For the Leslie-Gower system with two delays, investigations by recent authors have demonstrated that Hopf bifurcation occurs when delay parameters τ₁ and τ₂ cross specific critical values τ₁₀ and τ₂₀, with the direction and stability of bifurcating periodic solutions determined through normal form theory and center manifold techniques[6][10]. The classical result demonstrates that below critical delay thresholds, equilibrium stability is preserved, whereas beyond these thresholds, oscillatory limit cycles emerge, fundamentally altering ecological coexistence patterns[1][3].

Global Hopf bifurcation analysis, recently advanced through functional differential equation theory combined with continuation methods, reveals that bifurcation branches extend globally and maintain nested structure under appropriate conditions[3]. These global bifurcation results demonstrate that the limit cycle of the corresponding non-delayed system belongs to a connected component of the global Hopf bifurcation structure, providing theoretical insight into how delays fundamentally reorganize the bifurcation landscape[3]. Notably, delays can induce oscillatory coexistence at substantially lower carrying capacities than corresponding non-delayed ordinary differential equation models, yielding counterintuitive biological insights regarding the stabilizing or destabilizing effects of temporal lags depending on parameter regimes[3].

Multiple complementary bifurcation phenomena emerge in delayed predator-prey systems beyond Hopf bifurcation. The modified Leslie-Gower model with weak Allee effect in predators exhibits transcritical, saddle-node, generalized Hopf, and Bogdanov-Takens bifurcations[31]. Turing bifurcation, corresponding to spatial pattern formation through diffusion-driven instability, can occur independently or coupled with Hopf bifurcation in spatially extended systems[1][52]. Turing-Hopf bifurcations, where temporal oscillations and spatial patterns emerge simultaneously, represent particularly complex dynamical phenomena that characterize certain delayed reaction-diffusion predator-prey systems with toxins[52]. The bifurcation structure becomes increasingly intricate when multiple delays operate on different biological processes, with bifurcation diagrams displaying rich scenarios of saddle-node, Hopf, and cusp bifurcations depending on delay magnitudes and ecological parameters[38][41][43].

Stability switching phenomena, wherein equilibrium stability alternates between stable and unstable regimes as delay parameters increase, have been rigorously characterized for delayed predator-prey systems[47]. Such stability switches fundamentally differ from non-delayed systems where monotonic transitions typically occur. The mathematical characterization of stability regions requires solving transcendental characteristic equations, often yielding multiple critical delay values where stability is lost and regained cyclically[47]. These theoretical insights possess immediate practical significance for ecosystem management, as they delineate parameter ranges where uncontrolled population dynamics risk oscillatory or chaotic behavior, motivating the development of active control strategies.

## Spatial Patterns and Reaction-Diffusion Dynamics

The integration of spatial heterogeneity through reaction-diffusion frameworks introduces diffusion-driven instabilities that interact complexly with delay-induced destabilization mechanisms[1][48]. In spatially extended predator-prey systems, even when the spatially homogeneous equilibrium remains stable, diffusion can induce spatial heterogeneity through Turing bifurcation, characterized by the emergence of periodic spatial patterns in population distribution[1][52]. The critical insight that diffusion can either stabilize or destabilize populations depending on the relative magnitudes of prey and predator diffusion coefficients fundamentally contradicts classical understanding and motivates careful analysis of coupled spatiotemporal dynamics[1][48].

Delay-driven spatial pattern formation in predator-prey models has been investigated within frameworks incorporating constant prey harvesting, revealing that temporal delays substantially influence the stability landscape and the critical conditions for pattern emergence[4]. The mathematical analysis employs linear stability theory of spatially homogeneous equilibria combined with weakly nonlinear analysis to characterize pattern formation mechanisms. Diffusion-induced chaos represents another important phenomenon in spatial predator-prey systems where both species disperse along spatial gradients; mathematical models demonstrate that temporal chaos can emerge at fixed spatial points despite spatial homogeneity in parameter fields[48]. Such results emphasize that spatial heterogeneity, rather than reducing system complexity through averaging, can generate dynamics substantially more intricate than spatially homogeneous models.

Predator-prey segregation, wherein predators and prey occupy distinct spatial regions rather than coexisting uniformly, emerges when diffusion is restricted and delays are significant[1]. This phenomenon reflects genuine ecological processes where species spatial distributions result from the interplay between movement rates and interaction dynamics modified by temporal lags. The mathematical characterization of predator-prey segregation requires analysis of heterogeneous spatial patterns and their stability to perturbations, techniques drawing from bifurcation theory adapted to infinite-dimensional spaces. Cross-diffusion mechanisms, where predator movement is influenced by prey density gradients (prey-taxis), introduce additional complexity and can fundamentally alter bifurcation structures compared to systems with standard diffusion[41][55].

## Functional Response Mechanisms and Cooperative Hunting

The functional response, describing predator consumption rate as a function of prey availability, profoundly influences predator-prey dynamics in delayed systems. The Holling type II functional response, characterized by predator saturation at high prey densities, modifies population regulation mechanisms compared to linear predation[1][3][5]. When combined with cooperative hunting, wherein predator capture efficiency increases with predator density due to collaborative mechanisms, the effective predation rate becomes a more complex function of both prey and predator densities[1][38][41]. This modification to the functional response fundamentally alters bifurcation structures and stability regimes, with research demonstrating that cooperative hunting can enhance successful predation rates when prey abundance is substantial, yet simultaneously induce Allee effects through over-exploitation[38].

The generalized Allee effect formulation with parameterized functional forms permits exploration of how different behavioral mechanisms underlying reduced reproduction at low densities influence delayed system dynamics[16]. Non-monotonic functional responses, such as the Holling type IV response characterized by group defense or anti-predator vigilance mechanisms, introduce additional complexity where predation rate initially increases then decreases with prey density[5]. Analysis of delayed predator-prey systems incorporating such non-monotonic responses demonstrates multiple bifurcation scenarios including distinct periodic solutions depending on parameter values and delay magnitudes[5]. The integration of ratio-dependent functional responses with stage-structured populations and delays represents another frontier, capturing the biological reality that predation depends on predator-to-prey density ratios rather than absolute predator density[14].

## Control Strategies and Bifurcation Manipulation

The control of Hopf bifurcation and oscillatory behavior through active feedback mechanisms has emerged as a central research theme, motivated by conservation objectives of maintaining stable coexistence and avoiding population oscillations that risk extinction. Hybrid controllers combining linear time-delayed state feedback with parametric regulation effectively extend the stability region and delay the onset of bifurcation phenomena[7][10][44][47]. Such controllers modify system stability by introducing negative feedback proportional to past population deviations, creating an active stabilization mechanism that counteracts delay-induced destabilization[44]. Mathematical analysis establishes that appropriately designed hybrid controllers can extend the maximum stable delay interval, permitting delayed systems to maintain equilibrium stability over substantially larger delay ranges than uncontrolled systems[47].

Extended delay feedback controllers represent another control strategy wherein the control input incorporates delayed feedback of system state, creating multilevel stabilization mechanisms[10]. By adjusting feedback gain parameters and control delay values, such controllers effectively regulate the position of bifurcation curves and can suppress Hopf bifurcation entirely over desired parameter ranges[10]. The theoretical framework underlying controller design employs Lyapunov function methods and characteristic equation analysis to derive explicit conditions on controller parameters guaranteeing stability over target delay intervals. Numerical simulations validate that proposed controllers maintain system stability and eliminate population extinction risks across substantially wider parameter ranges[44].

State feedback controllers with fixed residual parameters and fractional-order adjustments provide additional control flexibility[7]. The control of bifurcation in three-dimensional delayed predator-prey models demonstrates that mixed controllers and extended delay feedback controllers effectively adjust both the stability domain and bifurcation onset timing[7]. Singularity-induced bifurcation control in bioeconomic predator-prey models with economic thresholds employs state feedback controllers designed to eliminate singular bifurcations and stabilize systems near interior equilibria[19]. The practical implementation of such control strategies requires reliable population monitoring to provide feedback signals, motivating research on robust controllers that maintain performance despite incomplete or noisy population data.

## Allee Effects and Population Thresholds

The Allee effect, describing reduced individual fitness at low population densities, fundamentally alters delayed predator-prey dynamics compared to classical models absent density-dependent reproduction reduction[1][16][21]. The mathematical incorporation of the Allee effect employs multiplicative scaling terms reducing reproduction rates below critical density thresholds, mechanistically capturing cooperation requirements or mate-finding difficulties in sparse populations[1][21]. Bifurcation analysis of predator-prey models incorporating Allee effects reveals substantially more complex bifurcation structures including multiple coexistence states with bistability regimes where system behavior depends on initial conditions[21]. The Allee effect in predators creates particularly destabilizing influences on population dynamics, with research demonstrating that achieving very low predator densities results in eventual extinction as reproduction becomes critically impaired[21].

The interplay between fear-induced prey behavior and the Allee effect amplifies population destabilization mechanisms[1]. Fear effects, wherein prey activity and reproduction decline in predator presence even without direct predation, reduce effective prey availability for predator consumption while simultaneously constraining prey population growth[1]. When combined with Allee-effect reproduction thresholds, fear effects can reduce prey populations below critical densities where reproduction becomes insufficient for population persistence, creating complex extinction scenarios not present in classical models[1]. The delayed manifestation of Allee-effect impacts, incorporating temporal lags between initial density reduction and reproductive impairment, creates qualitatively distinct dynamics with delayed bifurcations occurring at modified critical parameters[24].

Weak Allee effects, where reproduction declines modestly at low densities rather than becoming severely impaired, generate distinct bifurcation phenomena including canard limit cycles and relaxation oscillations in modified Leslie-Gower frameworks[31]. The theoretical analysis employs singular perturbation theory and blow-up techniques to characterize canard dynamics, revealing that delayed systems exhibit canard explosions where limit cycle amplitude increases rapidly over narrow parameter ranges[31]. These phenomena underscore the mathematical subtlety introduced by Allee effects in delayed systems, where nonlinearities interact with temporal lags to generate bifurcation structures qualitatively distinct from both classical Lotka-Volterra and non-delayed Allee-effect models.

## Stage Structure and Maturation Delays

The incorporation of age or stage structure, recognizing that predators and prey progress through immature and mature life stages with differential reproduction and predation capabilities, adds biological realism while increasing mathematical complexity[5][38]. Maturation delays, representing the time required for offspring maturation into reproducing adults, directly connect to temporal delays in the underlying population equations[35][38]. In delayed predator-prey models with stage structure, mature predators attack only mature prey, while immature organisms typically possess restricted capabilities, substantially modifying predation rates and population regulation mechanisms[5]. The existence of two positive periodic solutions in delayed stage-structured predator-prey systems with non-monotonic functional responses has been rigorously established using Mawhin's coincidence degree theory[5], establishing that environmental periodicity can substantially increase the multiplicity of coexisting population dynamics[5].

Maturation delay in predator growth has been systematically analyzed for predator-prey systems with cooperative hunting, demonstrating that longer maturation delays generally hinder predator population growth and increase predator extinction likelihood[38]. The bifurcation diagram analysis reveals that as maturation delay increases, bifurcation curves shift and bifurcation thresholds move, fundamentally altering the parameter regions supporting coexistence versus predator extinction[38]. Extended maturation delays disrupt ecological balance, favoring predator-free equilibria over coexistence states[38]. Conversely, reduced cooperation among predators results in intricate bifurcation dynamics where hunting time serves as bifurcation parameter, suggesting that behavioral factors and maturation timing interact to create unpredictable population variations[38].

## Fear Effects and Predation-Induced Behavioral Changes

Recent investigations have extensively characterized how fear of predation alters prey behavior through reduced foraging efficiency, increased antipredator vigilance, and modified reproduction rates, effects often equaling or exceeding direct predation mortality[1][37][40]. The mathematical modeling of fear employs multiplicative scaling terms in prey reproduction equations, effectively reducing per capita growth rates proportional to predator density or predator-induced stress[1][37]. In three-species food chains incorporating Beddington-DeAngelis functional responses alongside fear effects and gestation delays, Hopf bifurcation occurs when delay parameters exceed critical thresholds, creating oscillatory coexistence dynamics with substantially modified bifurcation conditions compared to non-fear systems[37]. The fear-induced behavioral plasticity coupled with temporal delays creates feedback mechanisms fundamentally altering population stability and equilibrium characteristics.

The nonlocal fear effect, where prey behavioral responses incorporate spatial information regarding predator proximity across extended regions rather than only local interactions, introduces substantially greater mathematical complexity[49]. Reaction-diffusion predator-prey models with nonlocal fear alongside delay mechanisms exhibit bifurcation phenomena qualitatively distinct from local fear models, with increased parameter flexibility potentially permitting coexistence stability over substantially broader parameter ranges[49]. The interplay between fear, delay, and spatial heterogeneity creates multiscale dynamics where behavioral responses occur on faster timescales than population growth and predation, requiring multiscale mathematical analysis to properly characterize system behavior[49].

## Toxicity and Delayed Mortality

The incorporation of toxicity, wherein prey accumulates toxic compounds that do not immediately but cause delayed mortality effects, introduces additional memory into predator-prey systems[1][52]. Predators consuming toxic prey experience instantaneous toxicity effects, while prey populations suffer delayed mortality as toxins accumulate, creating asymmetric time delays in population regulation mechanisms[1]. This dual-delay structure, where different species experience toxin effects on different timescales, fundamentally alters stability conditions and bifurcation characteristics[1][52]. Mathematical analysis of delayed reaction-diffusion predator-prey systems incorporating toxin effects demonstrates substantial effects on system dynamics, with toxin-mediated effects generating bifurcation phenomena qualitatively distinct from classical poisoning or predation frameworks[52].

The toxicity-induced bifurcation structure reveals that Turing and Hopf bifurcations can occur individually or coupled as toxin parameters vary, with theoretical and numerical results in excellent agreement[52]. The biological interpretation suggests that toxin availability substantially influences whether populations stabilize, exhibit oscillations, or display spatial pattern formation. The interaction between toxin dynamics and time delays creates complex feedback structures where delayed toxin effects amplify oscillatory tendencies while potentially generating spatial patterns through diffusion-driven instability. These findings emphasize the necessity of incorporating realistic mechanisms of indirect mortality and toxin accumulation when modeling ecological systems where such processes operate at significant biological scales.

## Prey Refuge and Alternative Food Resources

Prey refuges, where a proportion of prey population occupies spatial refuges inaccessible to predators, fundamentally alter predation-driven population regulation and stability characteristics[1][2][15]. The mathematical incorporation of prey refuge employs a refuge parameter m ∈ [0,1) representing the fraction of prey unavailable to predation[6]. Recent investigations demonstrate that high prey refuge levels can paradoxically negate supplemental food benefits, making prey control difficult, while optimal combinations of limited refuge and supplemental food enable effective prey population regulation[1]. In delayed Leslie-Gower predator-prey systems with prey refuge, the presence of refuge substantially modifies bifurcation structures and shifts critical delay values determining stability[6].

Alternative food resources for predators, supporting predator persistence independent of prey availability, create qualitatively distinct population dynamics compared to exclusively prey-dependent predator growth[15]. The investigation of predator-prey dynamics in two-layered water bodies where surface-layer prey migrate to deeper refuges while predators access alternative food resources reveals that nonlinear harvesting strategies better control chaos compared to linear harvesting, suggesting that harvesting strategy substantially influences population stability[15]. The bifurcation analysis demonstrates transcritical and Hopf bifurcations occurring as additional food parameters vary, fundamentally altering the feasible parameter space for stable coexistence[15]. These findings underscore that prey refuge and alternative resource availability represent critical ecological mechanisms substantially influencing delayed predator-prey dynamics and feasibility of population control objectives.

## Harvesting and Exploitation Models

The economic harvesting of predator-prey populations introduces additional state variables representing harvesting effort and economic profit alongside traditional population dynamics[50][53][57][32]. The optimization of harvesting to maximize sustainable yield while maintaining ecosystem stability represents a classical problem substantially complicated by temporal delays and bifurcation phenomena[50][32]. Delayed impulsive harvesting, wherein harvesting decisions depend on population data from previous time points rather than current populations, demonstrates that sustainability can be substantially delay-dependent despite optimality remaining relatively insensitive to delay magnitude[32]. The analysis reveals that delayed harvesting policies can induce finite-time extinction not present in non-delayed models, emphasizing risks of outdated harvest data in population management[32].

The bifurcation analysis of predator-prey models incorporating herd behavior, multiple delays, and prey harvesting reveals that singularity-induced bifurcation occurs when economic profit reaches zero, with explicit formulas determining bifurcation direction and periodic orbit stability[50][53]. Online control algorithms for population dynamics demonstrate effectiveness for nonlinear models including Leslie-Gower predator-prey systems, offering potential approaches for adaptive harvest management responding to real-time population monitoring[57]. The optimal tax policy analysis of delayed predator-prey harvesting models provides theoretical guidance for designing economic incentives aligning harvest decisions with sustainability objectives[50]. These frameworks integrate ecological dynamics with economic objectives, creating substantially more realistic models applicable to actual fishery and wildlife management scenarios.

## Stochastic and Environmental Fluctuation Effects

The incorporation of random environmental fluctuations through stochastic modeling reveals that time delays interact with environmental randomness to substantially alter population dynamics compared to deterministic models[20][23]. The analysis of time-delayed predator-prey ecosystems subject to Poisson white noise excitations demonstrates that increasing delay parameters enhances population fluctuations and reduces ecosystem stability[20]. The stationary probability density functions of prey and predator populations broaden with increasing delay, indicating that populations spread more widely across density ranges, reducing predictability[20]. The stochastic analysis demonstrates that self-competition parameters can reduce system fluctuations, suggesting that intraspecific resource competition provides stabilizing effects even under environmental randomness[20].

Stochastic hybrid delay models incorporating one-predator-two-prey dynamics with harvesting and Lévy jumps demonstrate conditions for stochastic persistence in mean versus extinction, with optimal harvesting strategies substantially modified by environmental noise patterns[23]. The analysis reveals that both time delays and environmental noise affect species persistence, with delay-dependent persistence conditions creating complex parameter landscapes for harvest optimization[23]. The maximum expectation of sustainable yield depends nonmonotonically on harvest effort due to bifurcation phenomena, suggesting that naive intensification of harvest effort can paradoxically reduce long-term yield through destabilization of population dynamics. These results underscore that stochastic environmental variability fundamentally alters both the bifurcation structure and the optimal management strategies for delayed predator-prey systems.

## Discrete-Time and Computational Models

The discretization of continuous delayed predator-prey systems using piecewise constant argument methods preserves bifurcation phenomena while facilitating computational analysis and numerical simulation[59]. The analysis of discrete predator-prey models demonstrates the existence of transcritical and Neimark-Sacker bifurcations alongside chaotic dynamics controlled through feedback and hybrid control methods[59]. The chaotic dynamics in simple Gause-type predator-prey models with discrete delay demonstrate that delay-induced chaos proceeds through supercritical Hopf bifurcation followed by saddle-node bifurcations of limit cycles and period-doubling cascades, ultimately generating strange attractors[56]. These bifurcation sequences substantially resemble those of delayed differential equations, suggesting that discretization preserves essential nonlinear phenomena[56][59].

The periodic orbit dynamics and strange attractors in delayed predator-prey systems exhibit sensitivity to initial conditions characteristic of true chaos rather than merely complicated periodic behavior[56]. The numerical evidence demonstrates that the cascade of bifurcations from stable equilibrium through periodic orbits to chaos occurs as delay magnitude increases, with period-doubling bifurcation sequences remarkably similar to those observed in the Mackey-Glass equation from fluid dynamics[56]. The computational exploration reveals that control of bifurcating and chaotic behavior in discrete models can be achieved through feedback control of population state variables, with hybrid control methods proving particularly effective at stabilizing systems across wide delay ranges[59].

## Machine Learning and Data-Driven Approaches

Recent investigations have begun integrating machine learning methodologies with dynamical systems approaches for analyzing and controlling population dynamics[60]. Nonlinear dynamics-based machine learning combines the representational flexibility of dynamical systems exhibiting diverse bifurcation phenomena with evolutionary algorithms for learning optimal control parameters[60]. The approach leverages the inherent diversity of different dynamical patterns available within nonlinear systems to select appropriate population dynamics representations matching observed ecological data. The genetic algorithm optimization of control parameters demonstrates robustness to noise and room temperature variations in physical circuit implementations, suggesting viability of evolved control strategies despite environmental perturbations[60].

The mean field game framework for modeling ideal free distribution in predator-prey systems provides a game-theoretic perspective on adaptive habitat selection and population dispersal strategies[33]. The mean field game analysis establishes existence of classical solutions for ergodic systems and demonstrates convergence of population density to ideal free distribution as control costs approach zero[33]. This approach bridges optimal control theory with evolutionary game theory, enabling analysis of how individual behavioral optimization generates population-level spatial patterns and coexistence dynamics[33]. The integration of such data-driven and game-theoretic approaches with traditional bifurcation analysis represents an emerging frontier permitting substantially more realistic modeling of ecological systems where organisms make adaptive decisions responding to local conditions and population densities.

## Experimental and Numerical Validation

The numerical simulations supporting theoretical findings across diverse delayed predator-prey models consistently demonstrate excellent agreement between analytical bifurcation conditions and computational results[1][7][10][38][52]. The phase portraits, bifurcation diagrams, and time series plots reveal the anticipated population dynamics at various parameter regimes, validating theoretical predictions regarding Hopf bifurcations, transcritical bifurcations, and stable limit cycle emergence[1][38][47]. The visualization of predator-prey segregation patterns in spatially extended systems confirms theoretical predictions regarding Turing instability and spatial heterogeneity[1]. The Monte Carlo simulations of stochastic delayed predator-prey systems demonstrate convergence of stationary probability distributions to theoretical predictions, validating stochastic averaging and perturbation methods employed in analytical derivations[20].

Controller effectiveness has been systematically evaluated across diverse delayed predator-prey models, demonstrating that hybrid controllers and extended feedback controllers consistently extend stability regions and delay bifurcation onset compared to uncontrolled systems[7][10][44][47]. The bifurcation value comparisons reveal that appropriately designed controllers shift critical delay values substantially, with stability domains extended across substantially broader parameter ranges[10][44]. The sensitivity analyses of controller parameters demonstrate that modest adjustments to feedback gains and control delays can dramatically alter system behavior, providing ecosystem managers with fine-grained control over population dynamics. These comprehensive validations across theoretical predictions, numerical simulations, and parameter sensitivity analyses provide strong evidence supporting the efficacy of proposed control strategies for practical ecosystem management applications.

## Integration of Multiple Ecological Mechanisms

Contemporary research increasingly recognizes that realistic ecosystem dynamics involve simultaneous operation of numerous ecological mechanisms rather than isolated processes studied individually. The comprehensive delayed spatiotemporal predator-prey model incorporating Allee effects, fear-induced prey behavior, Holling type II responses with cooperative hunting, toxicity with delayed effects, and differential fishing pressures represents perhaps the most sophisticated current framework[1]. The theoretical analysis of such complex systems requires integrating techniques from partial differential equations, bifurcation theory, and stability analysis of functional differential equations. The numerical simulations reveal that system behavior emerges from intricate interactions between individual mechanisms, with bifurcation structures substantially modified compared to systems incorporating isolated mechanisms[1].

The practical implication is that understanding and controlling ecological systems requires acknowledging ecological complexity rather than oversimplifying through single-mechanism models. The systematic variation of individual parameters while maintaining fixed values for others permits elucidation of individual mechanism contributions, yet real ecosystem management must simultaneously consider multiple mechanisms and their interactions[1][38][50]. The research demonstrates that seemingly minor ecological features such as weak Allee effects, moderate fear effects, or limited prey refuge can substantially alter population dynamics through interaction with temporal delays, necessitating comprehensive characterization of ecological systems before implementing management interventions[1][21][38].

## Future Research Directions and Challenges

The analysis of delayed predator-prey systems has revealed substantial theoretical progress yet continues confronting significant challenges regarding the translation of theoretical insights to practical ecosystem management. The incorporation of network structures representing metapopulations across multiple patches with complex connectivity patterns remains insufficiently developed despite ecological relevance[35]. The mathematical analysis of age-structured populations in multi-patch environments with impulsive culling control, while beginning to advance, requires substantially more development to address realistic management scenarios involving selective harvesting of age classes across spatially distributed populations[35]. The extension of bifurcation control methodologies to higher-dimensional systems with more realistic food webs containing multiple predators and prey remains theoretically challenging and computationally demanding[1].

The development of robust control strategies tolerating incomplete and noisy population data represents a practical challenge limiting implementation of theoretically-designed controllers[26]. Most theoretical control designs assume perfect knowledge of system state through continuous monitoring, an assumption rarely satisfied in actual ecological systems where population surveys are infrequent, costly, and subject to measurement error[26]. The integration of adaptive control strategies that learn appropriate feedback parameters from limited historical data while maintaining stability guarantees despite uncertainty represents a critical frontier[57]. The extension of mean field game approaches to realistic heterogeneous populations where individual organisms exhibit trait variation and behavioral heterogeneity, rather than perfect homogeneity assumed in current mean field models[33], represents another significant challenge.

The investigation of evolutionary dynamics where predator hunting cooperation, prey defense mechanisms, and dispersal strategies evolve through natural selection rather than remaining fixed represents an emerging frontier substantially complicating analysis[11]. The incorporation of strategy-dependent time delays, where different evolutionary strategies possess distinct temporal characteristics, generates novel replicator dynamics qualitatively distinct from classical evolutionary game theory[11]. The connection between optimal control theory for harvesting and conservation objectives with evolutionary dynamics creating tension between population control and evolutionary adaptation remains largely unexplored. These challenges suggest that the next research frontiers will involve increasing integration of evolutionary perspectives, data-driven learning approaches, and practical implementation considerations alongside continued theoretical development.

## Implications for Conservation and Resource Management

The theoretical and computational advances in understanding delayed predator-prey dynamics provide increasingly practical guidance for conservation and sustainable resource management. The establishment of critical delay thresholds determining stability transitions permits predicting population vulnerability to perturbations based on system parameters such as life history traits affecting maturation times[3][6][38]. The characterization of safe parameter ranges where stable coexistence persists across extended delay ranges guides harvest policy design ensuring sustainability despite incomplete real-time information[32][50]. The demonstration that prey refuge availability and alternative predator food resources substantially modify population control effectiveness informs habitat management and supplementation strategies[1][15]. The systematic control of bifurcation through appropriately designed feedback mechanisms provides theoretical justification for implementing harvesting quotas, stocking programs, or selective removal policies designed to stabilize population dynamics[7][10][44].

The integration of economic considerations through bioeconomic models incorporating harvesting effort, profit dynamics, and optimal yield calculations enables design of incentive structures aligning private harvest decisions with public sustainability objectives[50][53]. The analysis of stochastic models subject to environmental fluctuations and economic uncertainty provides probabilistic guidance regarding persistence likelihoods and optimal strategies under incomplete information[20][23]. The extension to discrete harvesting scenarios reflecting realistic fishing or wildlife management practices, rather than continuous harvest assumptions, improves practical applicability[32][56]. However, the translation of these theoretical insights to actual management decisions requires engagement with practitioners, incorporation of institutional constraints, and recognition that human behavior often deviates from theoretical optima through diverse psychological and economic factors.

## Current State of Knowledge and Open Questions

The contemporary research landscape reveals substantial theoretical understanding of bifurcation phenomena in delayed predator-prey systems alongside recognition of persistent gaps between theory and practice. The local bifurcation analysis through Hopf bifurcation conditions, characteristic equation methods, and normal form calculations has achieved high sophistication, with rigorous theorems now available for diverse system classes[3][6][10][13]. The global bifurcation analysis through continuation methods and functional differential equation theory continues advancing, with nested structures of bifurcation branches now tractable for certain model classes[3]. The numerical simulation technology permitting visualization and analysis of complex bifurcation structures has become sufficiently developed that qualitative agreement with analytical results routinely occurs across diverse models[1][38][56].

Yet substantial questions remain regarding the organization of high-codimension bifurcations in multi-parameter delayed systems, the analysis of global dynamics beyond bifurcation neighborhoods, and the prediction of which bifurcation scenarios actually occur in natural populations. The mathematical analysis of delayed systems incorporating numerous simultaneous ecological mechanisms, realistic spatial structure, and temporal environmental fluctuations continues confronting substantial computational and analytical challenges. The fundamental understanding of why certain bifurcation phenomena emerge robustly across diverse models, suggesting underlying universal principles, while other phenomena appear specific to particular mechanism combinations remains incomplete. The characterization of which theoretical predictions prove practically achievable through actual ecosystem management, versus which remain infeasible due to institutional, economic, or technical constraints, requires substantially more integration between theoretical and applied research communities.

This review identifies several high-priority research directions deserving increased attention from the mathematical biology and applied mathematics communities. ## Conclusion

The dynamics and control of delay-induced predator-prey models have emerged as a richly developed research frontier combining sophisticated mathematical theory with genuine ecological relevance. Recent advances from 2022 to 2025 demonstrate that temporal delays substantially alter population dynamics compared to classical non-delayed systems through bifurcation phenomena creating oscillatory coexistence, spatial patterns, and complex regime transitions. The incorporation of realistic ecological mechanisms including Allee effects, fear-induced behavior, cooperative hunting, toxicity, and prey refuges alongside multiple simultaneous delays generates bifurcation structures of remarkable complexity. The development of active control strategies employing hybrid controllers and extended feedback mechanisms has advanced substantially, enabling theoretical stabilization of population dynamics across substantially broader parameter ranges than uncontrolled systems.

The mathematical techniques underlying contemporary bifurcation analysis including Hopf bifurcation theory, characteristic equation methods, normal form analysis, and continuation methods provide rigorous tools for understanding local and global dynamics. The integration of spatial dimensions through reaction-diffusion frameworks reveals Turing instability and spatial pattern formation alongside delay-induced temporal complexity. The incorporation of stochastic environmental fluctuations demonstrates that noise interacts with delays to generate qualitatively distinct population distributions and extinction probabilities. The exploration of stage-structured populations, age-dependent processes, and realistic harvesting scenarios increasingly bridges the gap between theoretical models and practical applications.

Yet substantial challenges remain regarding the analysis of high-dimensional systems with numerous simultaneous mechanisms, the practical implementation of theoretically-designed controllers under realistic constraints, and the integration of evolutionary dynamics where population traits adapt to altered ecological conditions created by delays and control interventions. The translation of theoretical insights into actual ecosystem management practices requires engagement with practitioners and recognition of institutional, economic, and technical constraints limiting theoretical ideals. The emerging integration of machine learning, data-driven methods, and game-theoretic approaches alongside traditional bifurcation analysis suggests future research will become increasingly interdisciplinary and computationally sophisticated.

The comprehensive review of recent literature demonstrates that delayed predator-prey dynamics represents a mature yet actively advancing research field. The theoretical foundations are now sufficiently developed that practical applications increasingly appear feasible, particularly for well-studied systems such as fisheries management and pest control where population data collection and intervention capabilities permit implementation of theoretically-designed management strategies. Continued progress requires sustained mathematical development alongside enhanced dialogue between theoretical researchers and practitioners seeking to apply these insights to real-world conservation and resource management challenges.
