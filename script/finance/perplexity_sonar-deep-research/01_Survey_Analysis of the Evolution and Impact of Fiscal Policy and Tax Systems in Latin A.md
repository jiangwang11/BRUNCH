# Analysis of the Evolution and Impact of Fiscal Policy and Tax Systems in Latin America and the Caribbean: A Comprehensive Academic Review of 2022-2025 Developments

This review synthesizes recent academic and policy research on fiscal policy and tax systems in Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC), examining structural challenges, reform initiatives, and emerging policy approaches during the 2022-2025 period. The region faces a critical fiscal sustainability challenge, with tax revenues averaging 21.5% of GDP in 2022—significantly below the OECD average of 34%—while simultaneously managing elevated public debt burdens, persistent informality, and structural inequality[1][2]. Recent scholarship reveals that fiscal policy in LAC has remained largely procyclical despite adoption of formal fiscal rules, that direct taxation mechanisms remain underdeveloped relative to regressive indirect taxes, and that climate-related fiscal risks are emerging as critical constraints on medium-term sustainability. This review identifies three main analytical dimensions: the cyclical properties and institutional constraints of fiscal policy, the distributional effects and reform trajectories of tax systems, and the evolving policy responses to climate risk and digitalization. The synthesis demonstrates that while some countries have implemented progressive reforms addressing tax progressivity and base-broadening, structural obstacles including high informality, weak state capacity, and political constraints continue to limit the fiscal transformation required for sustainable development financing.

## Fiscal Policy Cyclicality and the Role of Financing Conditions in Latin America

The cyclical behavior of fiscal policy remains a persistent challenge in LAC despite two decades of institutional reforms. A foundational empirical analysis by Klemm and others demonstrates that fiscal policy in the region has been predominantly procyclical on average, contrasting sharply with countercyclical patterns observed in advanced economies[3]. The paper employs panel data methods and instrumental variable approaches to overcome simultaneity bias, finding that fiscal policy responds negatively to output gaps—that is, governments tend to expand spending during booms and contract during recessions, precisely the opposite of demand-stabilizing behavior[3]. This procyclicality appears concentrated in specific countries including Uruguay, Argentina, and Mexico, with significance levels varying across the region. The analysis reveals important evolution over time: while fiscal policy became countercyclical during the 2008-2009 financial crisis, this positive development demonstrated limited persistence, with procyclical patterns reasserting themselves in subsequent years[3]. Notably, the study demonstrates that commodity price shocks exert substantial influence on fiscal outcomes, with commodity revenues introducing systematic procyclical bias in countries dependent on primary product exports.

Research by Carranza, Daude and Melguizo extends this analysis by examining the role of financing conditions and fiscal rules in shaping fiscal policy outcomes over the 1990-2014 period[31]. Their comprehensive study of eight LAC economies reveals three critical findings: first, financing conditions act as a powerful driver of fiscal stance, with tightening credit conditions constraining fiscal expansion during downturns; second, fiscal rules demonstrate statistical association with more stabilizing fiscal performance, though their effectiveness varies significantly across institutional contexts; and third, the relationship between financing conditions and fiscal outcomes has recently diminished in importance, suggesting potential shifts in credit market dynamics[31]. The study constructs a fiscal rule index from the IMF Fiscal Rule Dataset and examines key qualitative features including rule coverage, enforcement mechanisms, and flexibility provisions. Evidence indicates that fiscal rules can facilitate countercyclical policy by constraining expenditure growth in good times and allowing automatic stabilizers to function during downturns, yet the empirical strength of this relationship remains contested and context-dependent.

Recent World Bank analysis confirms that fiscal deficits have narrowed from pandemic peaks but remain substantial across most LAC economies, with mounting debt servicing costs constraining fiscal space for development investment[4]. The report highlights a critical mechanism: rising interest rates increase debt-servicing obligations even when nominal deficits decline, forcing difficult fiscal trade-offs between consumption-oriented spending, investment, and debt service. In 2022, the debt service-to-tax revenue ratio reached 12.2% in LAC, up from 9.8% in 2012 and substantially above the OECD average of 4.8%[1]. This pattern reveals the interaction between fiscal policy cyclicality and financial market conditions: procyclical fiscal expansions during commodity booms have accumulated debt stocks that now require increasing fiscal resources to service, regardless of current economic conditions. Consequently, many LAC governments maintain tight monetary stances to contain inflation expectations while simultaneously undergoing fiscal consolidation phases, leaving limited policy space for expansionary measures to support growth or social objectives[1].

## Tax Structure, Distributional Capacity, and Progressive Reform Initiatives

The tax systems of LAC countries exhibit distinctly regressive characteristics relative to OECD benchmarks, reflecting both structural composition and effective implementation gaps. According to comprehensive comparative data compiled by the OECD, ECLAC, and Inter-American institutions, indirect taxes account for 48% of total LAC tax revenues in 2022, compared to only 32% in OECD countries, with value-added tax representing a particularly large share[1][13]. Conversely, personal income taxes and social security contributions account for only 26.7% of LAC revenues versus 48.4% in OECD countries, while corporate income taxes contribute 16% in LAC compared to 12% in OECD countries[13]. This structural imbalance creates substantial regressivity: the VAT burden falls disproportionately on lower-income households whose consumption represents a larger share of income, while the reliance on corporate income tax—despite ostensibly higher statutory rates—coexists with widespread tax planning, transfer pricing manipulation, and base erosion.

A major empirical contribution analyzing distributional effects across 18 LAC countries demonstrates that fiscal systems, when considering both taxes and transfers, are equalizing in all cases, yet the magnitude of redistribution remains modest relative to OECD countries[8]. Using detailed microsimulation methodology that incorporates statutory incidence assumptions for personal and corporate income taxes, value-added taxes, and specific consumption taxes, researchers find that the average reduction in the Gini coefficient ranges from 9.3 to 10.1 points (depending on pension treatment), substantially below typical OECD reductions[8]. Critically, direct taxes including personal income tax contribute to equalization, yet approximately 72% of the total equalizing effect derives from government spending on education and health, with tax contribution relatively limited[8]. The same analysis reveals that VAT—constituting nearly half of total tax revenue—is unequalizing in all cases, with low-income deciles experiencing tax burdens of approximately 17% compared to 10% for the highest income decile, demonstrating clear regressivity[8].

Three national case studies of progressive tax reforms—Uruguay (2006), Chile (2014), and Colombia (2022)—provide evidence on political feasibility and implementation challenges of redistributive tax reform in the region[33]. Uruguay's 2006 reform introduced a personal income tax (IRPF) after decades without direct taxation, adjusted VAT rates, and reformed corporate tax treatment, creating a dual income tax system that maintained relatively low top marginal rates while improving base broadness[33]. Chile's 2014 reform increased statutory corporate income tax rates, implemented an integrated system to prevent double taxation, introduced environmental taxes, and strengthened anti-evasion measures, generating approximately 4% of GDP in additional revenue that supported social spending expansion[33]. Colombia's 2022 reform, the most recent and comprehensive, introduced a wealth tax targeting net assets exceeding 3 billion pesos, expanded personal income tax base and increased rates, implemented a minimum 15% corporate rate following OECD guidelines, and significantly enhanced anti-evasion measures including beneficial ownership registries and transfer pricing enforcement[33]. All three reforms succeeded partially because technocratic tax administrations led reform design and defense, favorable macroeconomic conditions during reform implementation reduced political opposition, and reforms occurred shortly after electoral mandates provided policy window opportunity[33].

The implementation record reveals persistent constraints limiting redistributive tax reform success. Evidence from statutory incidence analysis demonstrates that personal income taxes remain characterized by narrow bases due to high non-taxable income thresholds, extensive tax expenditures favoring higher-income groups, and preferential treatment of capital income[8]. The effective structure ensures that salaried workers—particularly in the formal economy—bear a disproportionate share of income tax burden (at least 60% of personal income tax revenue in countries with available data), while self-employed workers and capital income recipients enjoy greater evasion opportunities and preferential treatment[33]. Furthermore, exemptions and simplified tax regimes specifically exempt low-income informal workers, creating a system where the poorest formal workers effectively subsidize informal and capital income earners[8][33]. This counterintuitive outcome—where the bottom income deciles face higher effective tax rates than the top decile—reflects the regressive structure overlaid upon an already narrow tax base concentrated among salaried formal employees.

## Informality, Tax Compliance, and the Fiscal-Inequality Nexus

High informality in LAC labor markets represents a critical structural constraint on both tax capacity and social protection coverage, creating what recent scholarship characterizes as a fundamental "fiscal-inequality trap."[19][35] The informal economy in LAC comprises economic activities operating outside formal legal frameworks, encompassing small informal enterprises, self-employed workers, and street vendors conducting predominantly cash transactions while evading tax and regulatory obligations[19]. Comprehensive systematic review methodology incorporating 22 pertinent articles from Web of Science, Scopus, and SciELO databases reveals that informality detrimentally affects regional economic development through multiple channels: regulatory deficiencies prevent effective monitoring and enforcement; low productivity in informal sectors reduces aggregate output growth and wage levels; systematic tax evasion erodes state revenue capacity; and inadequate labor protections create precarious employment conditions[19].

The quantitative scope of informality challenges is substantial and persistent. Labor force informality rates exceed 75% of employment in several LAC countries and have exceeded pre-pandemic levels despite economic recovery[19]. This high informality creates a fundamental asymmetry: formal sector workers, predominantly salaried and subject to withholding, bear the visible and unavoidable income tax burden, while informal workers, self-employed professionals, and capital income recipients enjoy substantial evasion opportunities. The OECD's 2024 public trust report examining twenty-six countries (primarily LAC) demonstrates that public perceptions of corruption, widespread mistrust of state capacity, and elite domination significantly undermine willingness to expand the fiscal contract through higher taxation[5]. Survey evidence across eight LAC countries reveals that while 80% of respondents believe income distribution is unjust and 77% support redistribution from wealthy to middle-class groups, few accept higher personal taxes as the financing mechanism, instead preferring corporate tax increases that they perceive as less visible[8].

Research on tax morale—the intrinsic willingness to pay tax independent of enforcement—reveals that public efficiency perceptions and governance quality exert substantial influence on tax compliance behavior[10]. Analysis across 18 LAC countries demonstrates that public efficiency levels and government responsiveness to citizen preferences significantly influence tax morale, with citizens more willing to comply with tax obligations when they perceive effective delivery of public goods and services[10]. Conversely, perception of corruption and state capacity weakness substantially reduce tax morale, creating self-reinforcing cycles where low compliance erodes state capacity to deliver services, further reducing public trust and tax morale. Gender dimensions of tax morale and compliance remain understudied in LAC, though research suggests that women's differential roles in unpaid care work and gender-segregated labor markets create distinct compliance incentives and tax incidence experiences relative to men[10].

## Digitalization, Tax Administration Modernization, and Compliance Enhancement

Recent technological transformation of tax administrations across LAC represents a potentially consequential shift in tax system capacity and enforcement effectiveness. The CIAT and IDB framework characterizing "Tax Administration 3.0" envisions a modern tax ecosystem based on automation, interconnectivity, and advanced data analytics that transcends electronic invoicing implementation toward comprehensive digital transformation[56]. Electronic invoicing has achieved near-universal adoption across LAC, creating real-time transaction visibility that previous paper-based systems never achieved, and enabling tracking of supply chains, cross-referencing of declared income with vendor records, and detection of inconsistencies[56]. Beyond invoicing, advanced technological implementations include pre-filled tax return systems using third-party reported information, artificial intelligence-based audit lead selection, blockchain applications for transaction verification, robotic process automation for repetitive tasks, and virtual assistants providing automated taxpayer guidance[56].

Implementation of machine learning for audit selection demonstrates measurable improvements in audit yield and efficiency. Case study evidence from California's restaurant tax audit program, trained on 93,413 unique tax records over a ten-year period, achieved 40.1% precision and 58.7% recall using neural networks, substantially outperforming traditional rule-based and randomization approaches[43]. The system identifies audit leads by recognizing patterns in transaction data that correlate with audit adjustment likelihood, enabling tax administrations to prioritize audits toward highest-yield cases, reducing manual review burden by 60% while maintaining or improving audit discovery rates[43]. Similar applications in Latin American contexts show promise: Colombia's tax fraud detection research employing cluster-based unsupervised learning approaches demonstrated accurate prediction of underreporting returns with significantly reduced administrative burden[43].

However, digitalization implementation across LAC varies substantially in maturity and effectiveness. The IDB digital maturity model identifies tiered progression where less advanced tax administrations should prioritize foundational services (taxpayer registration, e-filing, e-payment) before moving toward data integration and automated decision-making, while more advanced administrations can implement sophisticated analytics and cross-government data sharing[59]. Assessment of tax administration digital maturity reveals that most LAC countries remain in intermediate stages, having achieved basic e-services and electronic invoicing implementation but lacking comprehensive data integration, advanced analytics, or autonomous decision systems[56][59]. Constraint analysis identifies critical bottlenecks: insufficient technological infrastructure in countries with large informal sectors; limited capacity and training for tax administration personnel; data governance challenges including data quality, interoperability standards, and privacy protection; and legal-institutional frameworks that either restrict digitalization or lack mechanisms to enforce digital compliance[56][59].

## Climate Risk, Green Fiscal Policy, and Medium-Term Sustainability Frameworks

Climate change introduces unprecedented fiscal risks to LAC economies through multiple transmission channels: direct losses from extreme weather events estimated at 1.7% of GDP annually; devaluation of carbon-intensive assets during low-carbon transition; loss of fiscal revenues from fossil fuel exports; and increased spending requirements for climate adaptation and disaster recovery[44]. The Inter-American Development Bank's assessment of climate integration into LAC fiscal frameworks reveals that while most finance ministries demonstrate some progress in integrating climate considerations into fiscal policy, very few do so comprehensively, and progress remains concentrated in strategic planning rather than operational implementation[9]. Comparative performance analysis identifies Chile, Colombia, and Costa Rica as regional leaders in climate fiscal policy integration, yet even these countries face substantial implementation gaps in expenditure policy redesign and innovative financing deployment[9].

Carbon pricing mechanisms—including explicit instruments (carbon taxes, emissions trading systems) and implicit mechanisms (fuel excise taxes, fossil fuel subsidy reduction)—represent critical fiscal tools for climate transition while simultaneously generating revenue. Evidence from five LAC countries implementing carbon taxation (Argentina, Chile, Colombia, Mexico, Uruguay) demonstrates that tax rate levels show statistically significant correlation with emissions reductions, yet effectiveness depends critically on complementary policies including regulatory frameworks, fossil fuel subsidy reform, and institutional capacity strengthening[26]. Mexico's carbon tax covering 42.4% of greenhouse gas emissions represents the highest coverage in the region, while Chile's implementation at \(US $5\) per ton CO₂ remains substantially below levels recommended by IMF analysis for achieving transition objectives (ranging from \(US $15\) to \(US $75\) depending on policy scenario)[29].

Green, social, and sustainability-linked bonds have emerged as rapidly expanding financing mechanisms, increasing from 9.3% of LAC bond issuance in international markets in 2020 to almost 35% in 2023[1][24]. This expansion reflects growing investor demand for alignment with sustainability objectives and potential role of such instruments in mobilizing private capital for climate transition. However, implementation challenges including absence of harmonized frameworks, weak monitoring and supervision mechanisms, and risks of "greenwashing" (misclassification of activities as sustainable) limit their effectiveness[1]. The ECLAC carbon pricing overview evaluates effectiveness of carbon pricing across multiple jurisdictions and finds that while explicit carbon taxes show statistical effectiveness in emissions reduction, the effectiveness of individual instruments depends on design details, baseline conditions, and complementary policy integration rather than instrument type alone[26].

## Social Protection, Fiscal Space, and Distributional Challenges

Conditional cash transfer (CCT) programs in LAC represent the region's most significant social protection innovation, achieving coverage expansion from approximately 22 million households in the early 2010s to 73 million beneficiaries across eight countries by FY 2023[32]. Impact evaluation evidence demonstrates consistent poverty-reduction effects, with program-participating households experiencing 8% poverty reduction in Honduras, 20% decline in child labor participation, and 7-10% school enrollment increases[32]. Additionally, programs demonstrate secondary benefits including improved child nutritional status, cognitive development gains particularly among children participating from early ages, and increased probability of formal employment for young women in rural areas (2.5 percentage points higher than non-participants)[32]. Argentina's Universal Child Allowance program expanded coverage from 3.8 million beneficiaries in 2018 to 4.3 million by January 2023, demonstrating capacity for rapid scale-up when political commitment exists[32].

However, CCT program design and implementation reveal significant limitations constraining their redistributive capacity. Coverage remains imperfect with substantial exclusion errors, leaving relevant proportions of the poorest population without benefit access[35]. Additionally, design features targeting programs exclusively to poor populations create stratified social protection systems where poor households receive lower-benefit, means-tested assistance while higher-income groups access comprehensive contributory social insurance[35]. Gender dimensions of social protection reveal persistent biases: women's disproportionate engagement in unpaid care work creates asymmetric dependency on social protection transfers, while VAT-based program financing falls disproportionately on consumption-focused households including women and low-income families[35]. Adequacy of CCT benefits, measured as percentage of mean earnings, remains substantially below contributory pension adequacy—examples include Ecuador's CCT benefit representing 1.9% of mean earnings compared to 7.8% for contributory pensions[35].

The structural bias toward old-age coverage in LAC social protection systems stands in stark contrast to demographic realities and development needs. ILOSTAT data demonstrates that LAC social protection systems spend approximately 11 times more resources on older people than children, a proportion substantially exceeding other regional patterns[35]. This old-age bias reflects historical development of social insurance systems protecting formal sector retirees while relegating poverty-focused assistance to lower benefit, less adequate programs. Pension system sustainability challenges across Colombia, Peru, Uruguay, and Mexico reflect this imbalance: Colombia's recent 2024 pension reform with July 1, 2025 implementation date proposes splitting the contributory system and adding semi-contributory and voluntary pillars, yet debates continue regarding whether reforms strengthen rather than undermine system sustainability[25]. Broader analysis demonstrates that addressing persistent poverty and inequality requires reorienting fiscal priorities toward social protection adequacy and coverage expansion, particularly for children and informal workers, yet political economy constraints and debt servicing obligations sharply limit fiscal space for such reorientation.

## Debt Sustainability, Fiscal Rules, and the Political Economy of Constraint

Fiscal sustainability in LAC has become increasingly contested analytically and politically, with formal fiscal rules demonstrating substantial implementation challenges and unexpected economic effects. Brazil's constitutional spending cap adopted in 2016 froze real government expenditure growth for twenty years, intended to ensure debt sustainability through rigid expenditure constraint[38]. However, practical implementation revealed fundamental contradiction between spending ceiling and state obligations: the 2020 pandemic demonstration that the cap could not accommodate emergency expenditures generated broad recognition of inflexibility problems, while from 2016-2022 the cap effectively constrained social spending expansion and public investment[38]. President Lula's reelection in 2022 enabled fiscal rule recalibration, with new proposal establishing minimum investment requirement alongside revenue-dependent spending limits and countercyclical movement capacity—representing recognition that absolute spending caps create unacceptable constraints on state capacity[38].

Germany's constitutional debt brake (Schuldenbremse) and EU fiscal frameworks demonstrate comparable tensions between institutional rules and economic flexibility. Constitutionalization of fiscal rules transforms them from policy mechanisms into legal-constitutional structures that effectively remove them from normal democratic adjustment processes[38]. The political consequence of constitutional rules extends beyond immediate fiscal outcomes: classification of spending categories, calculation of cyclical versus structural components, and definition of eligible exceptions become intensely contested legal and political questions rather than technical fiscal management issues[38]. Research on fiscal rule compliance across LAC reveals that while most countries adopted formal fiscal frameworks during the past two decades, enforcement mechanisms vary substantially, with some rules maintained merely as soft constraints rather than binding requirements[15]. Debt sustainability analysis across the region suggests that debt is likely sustainable at baseline projections, though confidence intervals at 95% certainty reveal substantial uncertainty, and "fiscal fatigue" (decreasing responsiveness of primary balances to rising debt) poses emerging sustainability risk[15].

## External Vulnerability, Trade Policy Shock, and Export Dependency

The 2025 Trump administration implementation of elevated tariffs on LAC exports introduces acute external vulnerability to the region's fiscal and macroeconomic stability. Tariff structures impose highly differentiated impacts across countries: Brazil and Venezuela face 50% and 15% rates respectively, while most other LAC countries face 10% baseline rates, with additional specific sectoral tariffs on products including steel, aluminum, copper, and agricultural goods reaching 50% on targeted items[4][49]. These tariff measures interact with underlying structural dependencies: LAC export profiles concentrate heavily on primary products (agricultural commodities, minerals, energy) subject to volatile global pricing; manufacturing sectors in Mexico and Central America face direct competition with Chinese production; and oil exporters including Argentina, Colombia, and Brazil experience heightened vulnerability to price volatility[49].

The fiscal transmission mechanisms through which external tariffs constrain LAC public finances operate through multiple channels: direct reduction in export revenues for tariff-affected sectors; second-round commodity price declines as global demand shifts following tariff implementation; reduced foreign direct investment inflows if tariffs induce uncertainty about medium-term market access; and exchange rate depreciation pressures as capital outflows mount, increasing debt servicing costs for external debt holders[4][49]. World Bank growth projections for 2025-2026 incorporate downward revisions anticipating tariff effects: regional growth expected at 1.9% in 2025 and 2.0% in 2026, down 0.2 percentage points from prior estimates, with heterogeneous country impacts[4]. Countries most dependent on US and Chinese markets—Mexico, Central America, and South American commodity exporters—face greatest exposure. Tariff-induced commodity price declines create direct fiscal impacts for oil-exporting countries while simultaneously reducing government revenues from extractive industry taxation, requiring rapid fiscal adjustment precisely when political space for adjustment faces maximum pressure from social demands following commodity price shocks.

## Synthesis and Emerging Research Directions

The evolving fiscal policy and tax system literature on LAC for 2022-2025 demonstrates increasing analytical sophistication in understanding complex interactions between institutional frameworks, distributional outcomes, and political economy constraints. Four critical research frontiers have emerged requiring continued investigation. First, the digitalization of tax administration represents both genuine efficiency opportunity and potential inequality mechanism: while advanced analytics and automation can improve compliance and reduce administrative burden, implementation risks skewing audit and enforcement activities toward vulnerable populations and informal workers lacking technological capacity to respond[56][59]. Second, climate fiscal integration remains substantially incomplete despite urgency, with medium-term fiscal frameworks inadequately incorporating climate risk pricing, green infrastructure investment requirements, and transition financing needs, creating medium-term fiscal surprises if climate damages accelerate faster than current projections[44].

Third, the gender dimensions of fiscal policy warrant substantially expanded research attention: tax system design creates differential impacts on women through multiple channels including joint taxation provisions discouraging second-earner labor force participation, VAT incidence concentrated on consumption by women and lower-income households, tax incentive design for unpaid childcare reproducing gender role specialization, and public spending reductions during fiscal consolidation disproportionately affecting care services and social assistance programs utilized by women[45][48]. Fourth, the interaction between fiscal rules and genuine democratic deliberation over distributional priorities remains inadequately studied: constitutionalization of spending constraints transforms distributional questions into technical-legal disputes remote from citizen participation, potentially undermining fiscal legitimacy and social contract foundations precisely when fiscal reform requires broad political support[38].

The broader regional fiscal trajectory suggests mounting tension between external constraints (rising debt servicing costs amid elevated interest rates, commodity price vulnerability, tariff exposure) and internal pressures (unmet social needs, climate transition requirements, infrastructure gaps) with limited fiscal space to accommodate both simultaneously. Recent OECD-ECLAC analysis identifies annual financing gap of USD 99 billion for meeting sustainable development objectives, requiring comprehensive mobilization of public and private resources, improved tax administration capacity, and potentially expanded social contract support for additional taxation[1][13]. Yet political economy analysis across multiple LAC countries demonstrates that public willingness to support expanded taxation remains constrained by low state trust, perceptions of corruption and elite capture, and experience of fiscal austerity undermining public service quality. Resolving this paradox—substantial resource needs coexisting with limited political capacity for resource mobilization—represents the central challenge for LAC fiscal policy during the period 2025-2030.

## Conclusion

Fiscal policy and tax systems in Latin America and the Caribbean face historically unprecedented combination of structural challenges and reform opportunities during 2022-2025. Low tax revenues relative to development needs, regressive tax structure composition, persistent informality limiting base broadness and compliance, elevated public debt with rising servicing costs, incomplete climate transition integration, and external vulnerability to tariff and commodity shocks all constrain fiscal policy effectiveness and development financing capacity. Recent research demonstrates that fiscal policy in the region remains fundamentally procyclical despite formal adoption of fiscal rules, indicating that institutional frameworks require deeper transformation than technical modifications. Tax system progressivity remains limited despite some reforming countries' efforts, with regressive indirect taxation offsetting modest direct tax improvements, and the burden of visible tax collection concentrated among formal sector salaried employees while capital income and informal activities retain substantial evasion opportunities.

Social protection systems demonstrate measurable poverty reduction through expanded conditional cash transfer coverage, yet stratified system design and inadequate benefit levels limit broader equality impacts, while demographic pressure from aging populations threatens fiscal sustainability of existing pension systems without fundamental parametric reform. Digitalization of tax administration introduces genuine opportunity for improved efficiency and compliance, though implementation risks remain substantial and distributed unevenly across countries with different technological capacity levels. Climate fiscal integration remains inadequate given ecological urgency, with green finance mechanisms expanding rapidly yet insufficient to meet transition costs absent substantially larger mobilization of public and private resources. 

The critical research and policy agenda for 2025-2030 requires renewed focus on: first, understanding political economy constraints limiting fiscal reform despite acknowledged need, moving beyond technical policy analysis toward grounded institutional analysis of how elite interests, political coalitions, and historical precedents structure feasible reform pathways; second, integrated analysis of fiscal policy interactions with inequality, climate risk, and social contract legitimacy rather than treating these as separate policy domains; third, genuine partnership between LAC policy makers and international institutions that respects national policy space while providing evidence on reform effectiveness; and fourth, investment in strengthening tax administration capacity, particularly digital modernization and data integration, as potentially higher-leverage intervention than statutory rate increases alone. The region's 2025-2030 fiscal trajectory will substantially determine whether LAC achieves inclusive, sustainable development objectives or converges toward continued inequality and fiscal stress.
