# Tensions in Interreligious Relations: Social, Economic, and Cultural Dimensions in Contemporary Research (2022–2025)

This comprehensive review synthesizes recent scholarship examining the interconnected social, economic, and cultural tensions that characterize interreligious relations in the contemporary period. Through analysis of representative works published between 2022 and 2025, this report identifies five primary research orientations: (1) computational and data-driven approaches to detecting religious tensions through online discourse analysis, (2) empirical investigations of economic globalization's role in exacerbating religious conflict, (3) peace-building and dialogue initiatives addressing faith community polarization, (4) algorithmic bias in AI systems affecting religious communities, and (5) sociological analysis of religious demographic shifts and polarization. The synthesis reveals that while technological solutions for monitoring and mitigating religious tensions have proliferated, fundamental gaps persist in understanding how economic inequality, cultural marginalization, and structural injustice drive interreligious conflict. Emerging research suggests that future scholarship must move beyond isolating religious factors to examine the material and institutional conditions shaping interfaith dynamics.

## The Structural Foundations of Religious Conflict: Beyond Theological Explanations

Contemporary research increasingly rejects earlier frameworks that treated religious conflict as primarily theological or identity-based phenomena. Instead, scholarship now demonstrates that what appear as religious tensions mask deeper structures of political and economic competition. A critical perspective emerges from United Nations analysis of Middle Eastern conflicts, which explicitly argues that "what look like religious conflicts are normally the product of political or geostrategic manipulation, or proxies for other antagonisms."[5] This analytical reorientation has profound implications for understanding interreligious relations, shifting focus from doctrinal differences to material conditions shaping community interactions.

The relationship between economic structures and religious tension finds perhaps its clearest articulation in research on globalization's effects in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. A comprehensive study of globalization as a fuel for religious and ethnic conflicts demonstrates that the uneven distribution of wealth resulting from economic liberalization—leaving 1.2 billion people surviving on $1 per day and 2.8 billion on $2 per day—creates conditions in which "societies fracture along ethnic or religious lines, which are being manipulated for political advantage."[2] The research on Nigeria particularly illustrates this pattern: while conflicts are characterized as "tribal" or "ethnic," underlying causes consistently point to "social and economic marginalization and degradation of the people."[2] This framing suggests that interventions focusing purely on interfaith dialogue without addressing economic justice face fundamental limitations.

## Data-Driven Approaches to Understanding Religious Tensions and Online Discourse

### Hate Speech Detection and Religious Targeting

One major research direction involves computational linguistics approaches to detecting hateful expressions targeting religious communities online. A survey on combating hate speech through detection and classification methodologies reveals that religious identity constitutes a major category of targeting in online abusive content.[13] The research employs multi-modal approaches integrating text, images, and contextual discussion threads to achieve more nuanced detection of hateful content.[21] Specifically, work on multi-modal discussion transformers demonstrates that isolating comments from their discussion context produces inferior performance compared to holistic analysis that captures "multi-modal comment representations actively grounded in the discussion context."[21] These approaches achieve F-1 scores ranging from 0.49 to 0.92 depending on modality and language, suggesting that religious hatred expressed online exhibits complex linguistic and visual markers that simple keyword-matching systems cannot reliably capture.

### Religious Misinformation and Community Polarization

Complementary research investigates how religious misinformation drives social polarization and violence. A study examining social media users' engagement with religious misinformation in South Asian contexts reveals that religious misinformation-led violence often operates through what researchers term "communalism"—"extreme support for a particular religion that produces interreligious tension between communities and inspires religious violence."[46] The research identifies that "radical issues" constitute 60.4 percent of discourse when users engage with religious misinformation, compared to 37.1 percent for political issues.[46] Significantly, the study found that religious sentiments can be deliberately mobilized by "various interest groups (e.g., Islamic groups, ruling party men, and local groups)" who "frequently exploit ordinary Muslims' religious sentiments and mobilize them toward violence, encouraging vigilante behavior."[46] This suggests that religious tensions, while expressed in theological or communal terms, often serve instrumental functions in political competition.

Research specifically on correcting religious misinformation demonstrates that religiously-framed corrections can outperform standard corrections in shifting misinformed beliefs about conspiracy theories, with "all religiously-framed treatments successful at correcting misinformation, compared to a placebo control condition."[43] However, this effectiveness varies by misinformation type: for medical misinformation, religiously-framed moral messages alone fail to reduce endorsement unless combined with group identity manipulations.[43] This finding suggests that religious frameworks operate not as autonomous belief systems but as identity-linked structures that activate or deactivate based on social context.

### Extremism Detection and Religious Content

Researchers have developed datasets for identifying extremist content across multiple ideologies, creating balanced extremism text datasets "versatile with multiple ideologies verified by robust data validation methods for classifying extremism text into popular extremism types such as propaganda, radicalization, and recruitment."[15] The multi-ideology approach reveals that extremist narratives cross-cut religious categories, with support vector machine classifiers achieving F-1 scores of 0.67 on this challenging task.[15] A complementary systematic literature review examining online extremism, radicalization, and politicized hate speech identifies significant overlap between these categories—26.3 percent of data appears across multiple systematic literature reviews on these topics despite their apparent distinction.[18] This finding indicates that computational approaches often struggle to differentiate religious extremism from politically motivated hateful speech, reflecting underlying ambiguity in how religious and political antagonism relate.

## Algorithmic Bias and Religious Communities in Digital Systems

### Bias in Language and Multimodal Models

A systematic investigation of religious bias in both language models and text-to-image generation models reveals concerning patterns of stereotyping across religious groups.[47] The study identifies that in 23 percent of test cases, models equate "Muslim" with "terrorist," while in 5 percent of test cases linking "Jewish" with "money" occurs.[47] Testing across BERT, RoBERTa, ALBERT, and DistilBERT models demonstrates that these biases persist across multiple architectures.[47] Significantly, the research shows bias intersects with demographic factors—the same models exhibit differential bias patterns when gender, age, and nationality interact with religious identity.[47] For instance, models may exhibit greater anti-Islamic bias in responses mentioning "male" or "Middle Eastern" contexts compared to other demographic configurations.[47] This reveals that religious bias in AI systems operates not as a monolithic phenomenon but through complex interactions with other social categories.

### Content Moderation and Religious Expression

Research on religious freedom in the digital age reveals that "AI-driven content moderation is designed to combat hate speech and disinformation, yet these systems frequently misinterpret religious language, symbols, or practices—especially in multilingual contexts."[44] Studies document cases where "benign religious posts have been misclassified as extremist content, disproportionately affecting minority faiths."[44] The underlying problem stems from the fact that "AI systems are only as fair as the data they are trained on—and religious diversity is often underrepresented," leading to "algorithmic bias" where "facial recognition tools misidentifying individuals wearing religious attire, recommendation systems amplifying stereotypes, or predictive policing disproportionately targeting certain faith groups" become systematic outcomes.[44] This technological discrimination operates alongside and potentially amplifies existing social marginalization of minority religious communities.

### Ethical Dimensions of Using Religious Texts in NLP

A comprehensive position paper examining ethical considerations when using religious texts in natural language processing identifies that "thousands of papers in the ACL Anthology seem to use religious texts" with "a strong bias towards the texts of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam."[10][31] The research argues that NLP's use of such texts "raises considerations that go beyond model biases, including data provenance, cultural contexts, and their use in proselytism."[10] Critically, many religious texts used as training data for machine translation systems derive from missionary translation projects, meaning that NLP "repurposes the translations from their original uses and motivations, which often involve attracting new followers."[10] This reveals how technological systems encoding religious texts may inadvertently perpetuate colonial and evangelical agendas in their design assumptions. The paper calls for "responsible secularism" that "demands engaging with the ethical considerations of the use of these texts" and attending "to concerns of marginalized cultures" whose languages and religions remain underrepresented in training data.[10]

## Interfaith Dialogue and Peace-Building Initiatives

### Structural Approaches to Dialogue

Research on interfaith dialogue in Southeast Asia reveals that successful peace-building requires movement beyond reactive dialogue toward structured institutional approaches.[39] The Asia and Pacific Interfaith Youth Network (APIYN) and King Abdullah Bin Abdulaziz International Centre for Interreligious and Intercultural Dialogue (KAICIID) exemplify complementary models: youth-led grassroots networks and top-down institutional initiatives both demonstrate effectiveness in specific contexts.[39] The Dialogue Cities Southeast Asia initiative employs a "5Cs framework"—collaboration with media, connecting generations, creative arts, common spiritual values, and environmental conservation—to bridge divides "without resorting to erasure."[39] Significantly, APIYN's 2024 youth camp trained participants in "interfaith literacy and digital storytelling, fostering relationships across faith lines," while youth-led media campaigns "highlighted religion's role in peacebuilding, challenging secular ideologies that dismiss faith as irrelevant."[39] This demonstrates that effective dialogue requires acknowledging religious motivations rather than suppressing them.

### Tensions in Interfaith Work: The Gaza Conflict Case

Recent research documents how geopolitical crises fundamentally test interfaith relationships and institutions.[42] The conflict in Gaza strained "decades of interfaith work between Muslim and Jewish groups," with numerous "bridges built and allyships nurtured" suffering "permanent—possibly irreparable—damage."[42] Student protests reached "fever pitch at the end of the 2024 spring semester," bringing "numerous challenges for students and university and college chaplains."[42] However, the research identifies counter-examples where "some groups and individuals continued to do the hard work of hearing and recognizing each other's humanity, grievances, and hopes," with leaders like Najeeba Syeed promoting "a chaplaincy of democracy" that involves "listen[ing]" and "hold[ing] space for the trauma of whoever is speaking."[42] This reveals the fragility of interfaith structures when underlying political conflicts escalate, while also documenting mechanisms through which sustained relational commitment can preserve dialogue across profound disagreement.

## Religious Demographic Shifts and Polarization in the United States

### Declining Christian Affiliation and Ideological Divergence

Recent comprehensive survey data reveals significant demographic reorientation in American religious life.[40] The 2023-24 Religious Landscape Study found that 62 percent of U.S. adults identify as Christian, down from 78 percent in 2007 and 71 percent in 2014, while the religiously unaffiliated population now comprises 29 percent, including 5 percent atheist, 6 percent agnostic, and 19 percent "nothing in particular."[40] Critically, these shifts differ dramatically by ideology: among self-described liberals, Christian identification fell from 62 percent in 2007 to 37 percent in 2024, a 25-point decline, while "nones" rose from 27 percent to 51 percent.[40][22] Among conservatives, by contrast, 82 percent remain Christian, compared to 89 percent in 2007—a 7-point decline.[40] This ideological bifurcation suggests that religious polarization in the United States increasingly aligns with partisan divisions, transforming religious identity from a cross-cutting category into a marker of political tribe.

### Interfaith Families and Conservative Movement Divisions

Research on Conservative Judaism's approach to interfaith marriage reveals internal divisions reflecting broader tensions between institutional preservation and inclusive welcome.[1] A working group report noted conflict between those "want[ing] the movement to allow more participation" of Conservative rabbis in interfaith wedding ceremonies and traditionalists maintaining that "halakhah that recognizes boundaries" should enforce restrictions.[1] The underlying concern involves whether "avoiding change…also carries risks," particularly given that "fewer than half of Jews raised Conservative still identify with the movement."[1] This suggests that theological positions on interfaith relationships connect to institutional survival anxieties, where stricter enforcement of religious boundaries correlates with declining affiliation. The research indicates that "fears of a 'mass defection' if the movement were to allow its rabbis to officiate have long been overstated," implying that institutional flexibility may better serve long-term community vitality than boundary protection.[1]

## Cross-Cutting Dimensions: Social Media, Digital Infrastructure, and Religious Polarization

### Social Media as Infrastructure for Both Dialogue and Polarization

Research examining social media language in interfaith discourse in Pakistan reveals that platforms "have emerged as powerful tools for mobilizing support, raising awareness, and uniting diverse communities, although challenges such as algorithmic biases and content amplification persist."[50] The analysis identified four key dimensions structuring online interfaith conversations: religious tradition and history (RTH), community values and priorities (CVP), negotiation with technology (NT), and communal discourse and justification (CDJ).[50] Significantly, "religious teachings and historical references are frequently invoked to promote peace and tolerance, while inclusive language helps to build community solidarity and a shared sense of responsibility for protecting minorities."[50] However, the same platforms enable "risks of misrepresentation and digital polarization," suggesting that social media's role in interfaith relations depends entirely on algorithmic design and content moderation decisions controlled by technology companies.

### Religious Freedom and State Control

Research on religious freedom and social polarization in comparative perspective documents how "religious liberty disputes in the United States map closely onto existing ideological and partisan divisions, deepening societal fractures."[37] The research identifies that "recent years have seen religious liberty disputes…from pandemic restrictions on worship to conflicts over LGBTQ+ rights," reflecting how religious freedom claims have become politicized.[37] Critically, the threat to religious freedom varies by context: "In Southeast Asia, religious freedom often comes from state control or nationalist movements seeking religious conformity," whereas "in the U.S., the threat often comes from secular movements seeking religious conformity."[37] This suggests that religious freedom and religious polarization represent two sides of a single phenomenon—when religious communities perceive exclusion or marginalization, they mobilize politically, intensifying polarization.

## Synthesis: Common Patterns and Limitations Across Approaches

The reviewed research reveals three consistent patterns across disparate methodologies. First, computational approaches to detecting religious hatred and misinformation consistently outperform rule-based systems by incorporating contextual information, yet all such systems exhibit significant bias against minority religious communities, whom they disproportionately flag as extremist.[13][21][44] Second, research on peace-building consistently demonstrates that interfaith dialogue requires acknowledging genuine theological and communal differences rather than erasing them through false universalism, yet such dialogue proves fragile when underlying political or economic conflicts escalate.[39][42] Third, and most fundamentally, research spanning multiple disciplines converges on the observation that religious conflict fundamentally reflects struggles over political power, economic resources, and cultural recognition, with theological differences serving more often as mobilization frames than as primary causes.[2][5][46]

However, significant limitations constrain current scholarship. Most computational approaches treat religious content as a distinct category amenable to technical intervention, overlooking how religious polarization emerges from and reflects broader political economy. Research on dialogue initiatives rarely examines power asymmetries in who participates and whose perspectives gain legitimacy in such spaces. Scholarship on demographic shifts provides statistical documentation of religious disaffiliation without adequately explaining mechanisms linking economic precarity, cultural status anxiety, and religious change. Most critically, research treating religious tensions as problems to be solved through better dialogue, more accurate detection algorithms, or fairer AI systems implicitly accepts the premise that technical interventions can address fundamentally structural conflicts rooted in material inequality.

## Anticipated Research Trajectories and Emerging Challenges (2025 and Beyond)

Based on contemporary research patterns, at least three major research trajectories warrant prediction. First, scholarship will increasingly integrate computational analysis of religious discourse with ethnographic and sociological methods examining the material conditions producing religious polarization, moving beyond treating religious tensions as autonomous phenomena.[2][46][50] This reflects recognition that machine learning systems alone cannot explain why particular populations embrace religious fundamentalism, reject organized religion, or engage in interfaith violence—such explanations require understanding economic opportunity structures, cultural status hierarchies, and institutional discrimination.

Second, research will necessarily address the paradox that increasingly sophisticated AI systems for detecting religious hatred simultaneously reproduce and amplify algorithmic discrimination against minority faiths.[44][47] As content moderation systems become more prevalent, religious communities will face systematic suppression of their speech not through explicit censorship but through ostensibly neutral technical systems that disproportionately classify minority religious expression as hateful or extreme. This will likely generate new scholarship examining how to design "religiously literate" AI systems, though success remains uncertain given the fundamental tension between universal fairness standards and the particularity of religious meaning.

Third, and most consequentially, the next five years will likely witness renewed scholarly attention to the economic foundations of religious polarization, particularly as economic inequality continues rising globally.[2] Research linking globalization, resource competition, and religious conflict will expand beyond Africa and South Asia to examine religious polarization in high-income countries, documenting how economic precarity reshapes religious identity and interfaith relations. This reorientation would represent a fundamental shift from treating religious polarization as a problem internal to faith communities to examining how external structural conditions produce and sustain such polarization.

## Conclusion

Research from 2022–2025 on social, economic, and cultural tensions in interreligious relations reveals a field in transition. Older frameworks treating religious conflict as stemming from theological disagreement or identity distinctiveness have given way to approaches recognizing that economic marginalization, political competition, and structural inequality drive interfaith antagonism. Simultaneously, technological interventions—from AI-powered hate speech detection to digital dialogue platforms—have proliferated without substantially reducing religious polarization, while in some cases reinforcing algorithmic discrimination against minority faiths. The most promising emerging work integrates multiple methodologies, examining how material structures, computational systems, and institutional arrangements jointly produce contemporary interfaith tensions.

Future scholarship must resist the temptation to treat religious polarization as amenable to technical solution. The core drivers of such polarization—competition for scarce resources, political struggles over cultural representation, institutional discrimination against religious minorities—cannot be addressed through improved dialogue techniques or fairer algorithms. Instead, scholarship must increasingly interrogate the material foundations of religious polarization while examining how existing interventions inadvertently reproduce and amplify existing injustices. Such work would reorient the field from attempting to reduce religious tension within existing structures toward questioning those structures themselves.
