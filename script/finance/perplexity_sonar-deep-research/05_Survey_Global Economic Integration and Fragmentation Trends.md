# Global Economic Integration and Fragmentation Trends: A Comprehensive Research Review of 2022-2025

This comprehensive review examines the complex dynamics of global economic integration and fragmentation from 2022 to 2025, synthesizing recent empirical findings on geoeconomic fragmentation, trade policy shifts, supply chain restructuring, and regional bloc formation. The research landscape reveals a fundamental transformation in international trade architecture, characterized by simultaneous processes of strategic decoupling among geopolitical rivals, intensified regionalization through emerging trade agreements, and critical reassessment of supply chain vulnerabilities. Key findings indicate that while global trade remains resilient in nominal terms, the underlying structure of value chains is undergoing substantial reconfiguration driven by policy interventions, geopolitical tensions, and technological competition, with consequential implications for economic diversification benefits, innovation ecosystems, and long-term productivity growth across both advanced and emerging economies.

## Introduction: The Paradox of Interconnectedness and Fragmentation

The contemporary global economy presents a paradoxical situation where unprecedented levels of economic interdependence coexist with increasingly fragmented trade relationships along geopolitical lines. The post-Cold War consensus on trade liberalization and multilateral integration, which dominated the 1990s through early 2010s, has given way to a more contested and strategically calculated approach to international commerce. This transition, often termed "geoeconomic fragmentation," represents not merely a cyclical fluctuation in protectionist sentiment but rather a structural recalibration of how states conceive of economic relationships in relation to national security, technological sovereignty, and strategic autonomy.[1][4]

The rise of geoeconomic tensions among major economies has intensified concerns about the reversal of globalization gains, with particular focus on how policy-driven trade barriers affect financial openness and macroeconomic stability.[1] Contemporary interdependence is characterized increasingly by trade in intermediate goods flowing through complex global supply chains rather than finished products, fundamentally altering the calculus of economic vulnerability and resilience.[3] The COVID-19 pandemic exposed critical bottlenecks in these supply chains, demonstrating that even among wealthy industrial nations, dependencies could rapidly transform into critical vulnerabilities when supply sources faced unexpected disruptions.[3] Simultaneously, explicit strategic competition between the United States and China has accelerated the adoption of technological export controls, forcing multinational enterprises to reconsider their sourcing strategies and investment locations.[13][14]

This scholarly review synthesizes empirical research from 2022 to 2025 examining these dynamics across multiple analytical dimensions. The evidence reveals that geoeconomic fragmentation operates through distinct but interconnected mechanisms: direct policy interventions such as tariffs and export controls; financial market fragmentation driven by geopolitical distance; supply chain reconfiguration through nearshoring and reshoring; the emergence of competing regional trade blocs; and strategic investment decisions by multinational enterprises responding to increased uncertainty and policy volatility. Understanding these mechanisms requires integrating insights from trade economics, international finance, political economy, and organizational theory, as the drivers of fragmentation extend beyond traditional tariff economics to encompass broader questions of strategic autonomy, technological competition, and systemic resilience.

## Geoeconomic Fragmentation and Diversification Benefits: Quantifying the Macroeconomic Stakes

The quantitative assessment of geoeconomic fragmentation's impact on macroeconomic outcomes represents one of the most developed areas of recent research. Okuda and Tsuruga (2024) developed a structural macroeconomic model calibrated to G7 countries to assess how geopolitical tensions constrain international portfolio diversification and thereby reduce macroeconomic resilience.[1] Their framework models scenarios in which G7 countries become unable to transact with foreign economies lacking geopolitical alignment, forcing them to concentrate investments in geopolitically proximate countries that typically exhibit highly synchronized business cycles. The simulation results reveal that while full economic integration produces diversification benefits in the range of 5 to 15 percent (measured as the reduction in macro-financial volatility compared to autarky), geopolitical fragmentation scenarios generate substantial losses of 20 to 50 percent of these integration gains.[1]

This finding carries profound implications for central banks and policymakers because it suggests that fragmentation-induced losses exceed the direct gains from trade cost reduction. The mechanism operates through restricted access to countries with asynchronous business cycles, such as China and commodity-exporting nations, which precisely the economies that provide the greatest diversification value during synchronized downturns in advanced economies.[1] When G7 countries concentrate their external portfolio allocations only among fellow industrial economies in Europe, they sacrifice the offsetting cyclical dynamics that would otherwise stabilize aggregate demand and consumption smoothing during recession periods.[1] Furthermore, the model incorporates feedback effects through reduced risk-sharing in goods trade as economies increase domestic production to substitute for imports, amplifying volatility in macro-financial variables beyond the direct portfolio channel.

The European Central Bank's comprehensive assessment of fragmenting global trading systems extends this analysis by modeling multiple fragmentation scenarios for the EU and globally interconnected economies.[4] Their analysis distinguishes between moderate fragmentation scenarios, in which some commodity-exporting countries and emerging markets are excluded from trade relationships, and extreme fragmentation scenarios in which trade remains concentrated among industrial economies. Under extreme fragmentation scenarios in which Western blocs cease trade with Eastern blocs, global GDP could decline by up to 9 percent coupled with an increase in global inflation of 4 percentage points in the first year, with impacts persisting for at least five years.[4] Critically, the research documents that these estimates represent conservative lower bounds because they do not incorporate secondary channels such as impaired knowledge diffusion, adverse confidence effects, financial amplification effects, and macroeconomic uncertainty.[4]

The empirical mechanisms connecting geopolitical distance to reduced international diversification operate through multiple channels. Aiyar and colleagues (2024) demonstrate that the impact of geopolitical distance on foreign direct investment has doubled over the past decade, with a one standard deviation drop in UN voting similarity reducing greenfield FDI by 16 percent in 2022 compared to only 8 percent in 2011.[14] More strikingly, the impact of differences in public opinion has increased from an 11 percent reduction in greenfield FDI in 2011 to a 38 percent reduction in 2022, indicating that geopolitical alignment has become progressively more important for investment location decisions.[14] Being in a different geopolitical bloc reduced greenfield FDI flows by approximately 11 percent in 2011 but by 22 percent in 2021, nearly doubling in impact.[14] These findings suggest that firms increasingly perceive geopolitical distance as a material business risk factor comparable to traditional determinants such as transportation costs or institutional quality.

The international monetary implications of fragmentation warrant particular attention from monetary policymakers. As countries pursue "friendshoring" strategies to align supply chains with geopolitical allies, the currencies of trusted trading partners gain prominence in reserve accumulation and settlement systems.[3][4] This dynamic potentially creates self-reinforcing cycles in which geopolitical alignment determines both trade patterns and financial architecture, further entrenching fragmentation. The heterogeneity of fragmentation impacts across countries suggests that middle-income emerging markets and commodity exporters face particularly acute vulnerabilities, as they often occupy intermediate positions in global value chains and lack the technological capacity to substitute for disrupted inputs through rapid domestic innovation.[4] Advanced economies in Europe and Asia-Pacific region demonstrate greater flexibility in reorienting trade toward allied partners, though at the cost of higher adjustment costs and lower productivity growth from reduced competitive pressure.

## Trade Policy Fragmentation and Supply Chain Reconfiguration: From Globalization to Regionalization

The period from 2022 to 2025 witnessed unprecedented escalation in explicitly protectionist trade policies, reversing decades of gradual liberalization. The number of new trade restrictions reached record highs during 2023-2025, with more than 2,500 trade restrictions imposed worldwide in just the first ten months of 2025 alone, almost five times as many as during the same period in 2015.[41] This represents not merely cyclical protectionist sentiment but rather a structural shift in how governments employ trade policy as an instrument of strategic economic competition. The Trump administration's tariff escalations beginning in 2018 established a precedent that subsequent administrations have maintained and expanded, creating a new baseline of elevated policy uncertainty that persists far above historical norms.[41]

Research on de-globalization and global value chain (GVC) restructuring reveals that trade protectionism functions as a driver reshaping the geographic organization of production networks. Rather than simply reducing trade volumes, protectionist measures force multinational enterprises to make structural decisions about where to locate production, which suppliers to engage, and how to organize component sourcing.[2] The literature distinguishes between shallow globalization characterized by trade in finished products and deep integration through supply chains involving dozens of countries contributing specialized components or processing stages.[2][3] Approximately 80 percent of global trade is now undertaken through GVCs, making disruptions in this system consequential for employment, innovation diffusion, and productivity growth.[2]

China's position in global value chains has undergone dramatic transformation, with profound implications for fragmentation dynamics. In 1995, the United States, Germany, and Japan collectively dominated intermediate goods trade, but by 2020 China emerged as the leading provider at 11 percent of total trade value, having surpassed Japan in 2006, Germany in 2014, and the United States in 2015.[3] Over the past decade, the rest of the world has grown increasingly dependent on China for intermediate goods while China has simultaneously become more self-sufficient except in a few advanced industries.[3] As China's influence in global supply chains grew and geopolitical tensions with the United States escalated, intermediate goods trade became a central focus in foreign policymaking, with states crafting economic security strategies aimed simultaneously at reducing vulnerabilities tied to reliance on China while limiting China's access to advanced technological manufacturing lines.[3]

The policy responses to supply chain vulnerabilities have coalesced around three distinct but complementary strategies: reshoring, nearshoring, and friendshoring. Reshoring involves returning manufacturing to the home country, while nearshoring shifts production to neighboring countries such as Mexico or Canada, and friendshoring relocates supply chains from adversaries to geopolitical allies.[3][8] Mexico has now surpassed China as the United States' top trading partner, reflecting this strategic reorientation.[8] A 2024 Kearney report documented the largest annual spike in reshoring activity to date, while Bain & Company found that over 80 percent of large manufacturers plan to shift supply chains closer to market.[8] However, execution lags significantly behind intent, with most companies currently in planning and investment phases rather than having completed substantial production reallocation.[8]

The economic rationale for supply chain reconfiguration extends beyond simple cost considerations to encompass a broader constellation of resilience concerns. While moving production closer to home initially appears costly due to higher labor and regulatory expenses, the fuller accounting recognizes that reshoring and nearshoring reduce logistics costs through shorter transportation distances, eliminate tariff exposure between geopolitical blocs, mitigate currency fluctuation risks from volatile exchange rates, and reduce vulnerability to geopolitically motivated supply disruptions.[11] Shorter supply chains enable faster delivery and just-in-time production, reducing inventory holding costs that had expanded substantially during pandemic-driven precautionary over-ordering.[11] However, the transition faces significant headwinds, particularly acute labor shortages in advanced economies; a Deloitte study estimates a 1.9 million-worker gap in U.S. manufacturing, a figure expected to double by 2035.[8]

The restructuring of supply chains operates through firm-level decision-making in response to geopolitical uncertainty rather than through centralized government planning. Aiyar and colleagues document that geopolitical differences affect not only greenfield FDI (new facility construction) but also brownfield investments through merger and acquisition activities, with the effect more pronounced for brownfield investments in recent years.[14] This suggests that existing multinational operations in geopolitically distant countries face pressures toward divestment or gradual run-down, while new investment disproportionately flows toward allied countries.[14] Critically, these effects extend beyond sectors explicitly designated as strategic for national security or intensive in global value chain participation; comparable geopolitical effects appear across diverse sectors including contract-intensive industries traditionally characterized by relationship-specific investments less vulnerable to policy shocks.[14] This universality of geopolitical effects suggests that the phenomenon exceeds narrow economic security concerns and reflects broader business uncertainty and institutional risk perceptions.

## Trade Bloc Formation and Regional Integration: The Emergence of Competing Architectures

The period 2022-2025 witnessed simultaneous processes of trade bloc formation and proliferation of bilateral agreements, creating an increasingly complex landscape of overlapping and sometimes contradictory trade frameworks. The Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP), which entered into force in January 2022, represents the world's largest free trade agreement by GDP, encompassing 15 countries accounting for approximately 30 percent of global GDP.[9][10][12] RCEP covers 30 percent of both global GDP and population, with SwissRe Institute projections indicating it will account for 35 percent of global GDP by 2030 as member economies continue growth trajectories.[10] The agreement establishes common rules of origin throughout the bloc, which facilitate international supply chains by reducing administrative complexity in determining tariff treatment.[9]

RCEP's institutional design reflects a pragmatic approach to deep integration without requiring the comprehensive regulatory harmonization characteristic of the European Union or earlier bilateral trade agreements. The agreement aims to eliminate approximately 90 percent of tariffs on imports between signatories within 20 years of coming into force, with complete elimination of tariffs and quotas on over 65 percent of goods trade.[9] However, tariff schedules reflect each member's political economy, with concessions varying substantially based on pre-existing tariff levels.[9] Notably, RCEP does not include labor standards, environmental protection disciplines, or governance of state-owned enterprises, representing a more flexible approach emphasizing trade and investment liberalization over regulatory convergence.[9][12] This flexibility likely contributed to its successful negotiation and ratification despite the participation of countries with widely divergent development levels and institutional characteristics.

The Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP) represents an alternative model of regional integration emphasizing higher trade standards and deeper regulatory coordination. The CPTPP, which originally included 11 Asia-Pacific countries before expanding to include the United Kingdom in 2023 and now comprises 12 members representing 14.5 percent of global GDP, establishes more stringent requirements for trade liberalization and regulatory alignment than RCEP.[10][12] Members have pledged to eliminate almost all tariffs and import charges on each other's products over time while accepting common obligations on food regulations, environmental protections, the digital economy, and investment governance.[12] Seven CPTPP signatories are simultaneously RCEP members, creating overlapping commitments that require countries to navigate complex rules of origin and potentially conflicting obligations.[9][12]

The Indo-Pacific Economic Framework for Prosperity (IPEF), launched by the Biden administration in May 2022, represents a distinctly different approach to regional economic architecture. Unlike traditional free trade agreements, IPEF does not include market access for goods or services through tariff schedules.[31][34] Instead, it emphasizes supply chain resilience, clean economy transition, and fair economy principles, addressing non-tariff barriers and regulatory cooperation rather than negotiating preferential market access.[31][34] The framework's supply chain agreement, which entered into force in February 2024 after ratification by five member countries, establishes mechanisms for supply chain diversification and crisis response coordination.[31] India maintained observer status on IPEF's trade pillar due to concerns about benefits and lack of clarity, but joined other pillars addressing supply chains, clean energy, and fair economy governance.[31]

The proliferation of trade agreements accelerated notably in 2025 after years of relative stagnation. More than a dozen agreements were signed during 2025, with the total likely to rise by December, roughly double the average annual number recorded during 2020-2024 and above the average of the 2010s.[41] This renewed momentum reflects governments' recognition of the need for deeper integration while simultaneously adapting to fragmented global conditions through selective partnership formation. The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), launched in 2020 with 22 signatories, has expanded to encompass all 55 African Union nations, making it the world's largest free trade area by number of participants.[41] The AfCFTA aims to create a single continental market for goods and services, though implementation remains in early stages with considerable work required to harmonize tariff schedules, rules of origin, and dispute resolution mechanisms.[10]

However, the proliferation of overlapping trade agreements creates what scholars term a "spaghetti bowl" of trade rules, in which inconsistent rules of origin, varying tariff schedules, and divergent standards limit the effective utilization of preferential agreements.[41] Firms face complex calculations determining optimal sourcing locations based on rules of origin compliance, tariff savings, and regulatory requirements, potentially increasing transaction costs relative to a simpler multilateral system.[41] Additionally, the divergence in standards across agreements risks fragmenting global technical specifications for products and services, creating duplication and reducing economies of scale in goods production and service delivery.[41] The World Trade Organization's dispute settlement mechanism remains paralyzed, limiting multilateral institutions' capacity to coordinate across competing regional frameworks or resolve conflicts arising from overlapping commitments.[41]

## Technological Competition and Innovation Decoupling: Export Controls as a Fragmentation Driver

Technological competition between the United States and China has emerged as a primary driver of economic fragmentation during the 2022-2025 period, with export controls strategically employed to restrict adversaries' access to advanced technologies while promoting domestic innovation capabilities. The United States Department of Commerce implemented dramatic revisions to export controls on semiconductor technology beginning in October 2022, substantially tightened in October 2023, aimed at foreclosing China's ability to obtain high-end semiconductor chips, manufacturing equipment, and requisite know-how.[13] These restrictions characterize a departure from traditional trade policy, employing asymmetric information warfare through selective technology denial rather than reciprocal tariff escalation.[13]

The Entity List, administered by the Department of Commerce, has expanded dramatically as an instrument of technological decoupling. The number of Chinese firms on this list increased from three in 1997 to 345 in 2022, with a significant acceleration in 2018 corresponding to the initiation of explicit U.S.-China technology competition.[16][51] Research measuring the impact of Entity List inclusion on Chinese firm innovation reveals both direct and spillover effects across innovation networks. Chinese firms directly targeted by Entity List inclusion experience reductions in both the quantity and quality of patents filed compared to similar non-sanctioned firms, with magnified effects for high-technology patents.[16][51] The decline in patenting is driven entirely by Chinese firms with prior U.S. collaborators, suggesting that relationships with U.S. innovators provided crucial inputs for achieving technological frontiers.[16][51]

Critically, the innovation impacts extend beyond directly sanctioned firms to affect entire technology fields through spillover mechanisms. Other Chinese firms operating in the same technological domains as sanctioned firms experience negative innovation impacts even when not directly subject to export controls, indicating that information networks and supply chains for specialized inputs carry technological restrictions beyond explicit Entity List designations.[16][51] Downstream fields relying on semiconductor innovations experience slowed innovation, whereas upstream fields supplying to the semiconductor sector patent more actively, reflecting endogenous firm responses to increased demand for substitutes.[16][51] The spillover effects extend internationally to firms outside China, including U.S. firms that previously collaborated with Chinese partners and experienced their own innovation declines following restrictions.[16][51]

China's government response to technological export controls demonstrates strategic determination to achieve technological self-sufficiency through coordinated industrial policy. The Communist Party's 20th National Congress identified the U.S.-China trade conflict as the "economic main battlefield" and promised "high-level technology self-strength and self-independence."[13] Industrial policies such as the "Made in China 2025" initiative launched in 2015 and the 2020 Dual Circulation strategy emphasize reducing reliance on foreign technology and building integrated domestic innovation ecosystems.[4] City governments have committed substantial resources to enabling chipmakers to eliminate foreign devices; Guangzhou alone committed over $21 billion in 2023 for this purpose.[13]

The effectiveness of technological decoupling strategies remains contested in the empirical literature. While new chip restrictions have significantly affected China's semiconductor ecosystem by limiting access to equipment essential for next-generation production, China simultaneously intensifies domestic investments in more advanced chips while reducing market shares of U.S. firms.[13] China's largest chipmaker, Yangtze Memory Technologies Co, reportedly faced workforce reductions of 10 percent following Western restrictions, yet Chinese semiconductor foundry Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation warned that mass production timelines might be postponed by only one to two quarters despite equipment difficulties, suggesting adaptation capacity exceeding initial expectations.[13] The possibility remains that Western controls may drive indigenous Chinese innovation in microelectronics, potentially generating technological breakthroughs transforming the global semiconductor industry rather than merely retarding Chinese advancement.[13]

The broader implications of technological decoupling extend beyond semiconductor industries to encompass the entire research ecosystem. Joint research collaboration between U.S. and Chinese researchers has declined notably, with Chinese researchers decreasing citations of U.S. papers since U.S.-China tensions escalated in 2016.[16][51] This bifurcation of research communities risks creating parallel innovation ecosystems with reduced knowledge diffusion, potentially imposing substantial economic costs through duplicated research efforts and forgone opportunities for mutually beneficial scientific advancement.[16][51] Yet paradoxically, reduced international collaboration may accelerate development of indigenous Chinese technologies as domestic firms internalize knowledge production rather than relying on external inputs.

## The Tariff Question: Quantifying Economic Impacts and Distributional Consequences

Trade economists have devoted substantial analytical effort to quantifying the macroeconomic impacts of elevated tariff regimes implemented during 2025, producing findings with significant implications for understanding fragmentation costs. The Trump administration's tariffs as of September 11, 2025, represent the highest tariff levels since the 1930s, with the effective tariff rate reaching approximately 27 percent, a dramatic elevation from historical norms.[49][52] These tariffs reduce the U.S. growth rate by 0.23 percentage points in 2025 and 0.62 percentage points in 2026, with long-run GDP levels persistently 0.6 percent smaller, equivalent to approximately $170 billion annually in 2024 dollars.[25][49] Unemployment rises 0.55 percentage point by year-end 2025 with payroll employment 740,000 lower, concentrated in sectors most exposed to trade such as durable goods manufacturing, mining, and agriculture.[25][49][52]

The inflationary consequences of tariffs impose substantial costs on household purchasing power through mechanisms of incomplete pass-through and general equilibrium adjustments. Consumer price levels rise 2.9 percent in the short run (before accounting for substitution responses), equivalent to a loss of purchasing power of $4,700 per household in 2024 dollars.[25][49] Apparel prices increase 64 percent in the short run due to concentration of tariffs on textiles and clothing, remaining 27 percent higher even in the long run after substitution occurs.[49] Food prices rise 2.6 percent short-run and remain 3 percent higher long-run, with fresh produce initially 5.4 percent more expensive, stabilizing at 3.9 percent higher.[49] These consumer welfare losses are regressive, disproportionately burdening lower-income households that spend larger shares of income on tariff-affected goods such as clothing and food.[25][49]

Tariff revenues raised through 2025 tariffs amount to $2.4 trillion over the decade 2026-2035 on a conventional basis, reduced to $4.5 trillion dynamically accounting for behavioral responses reducing import demand.[25] However, the growth impacts of tariffs offset much of this revenue, with negative dynamic revenue effects totaling approximately $587 billion over the decade due to reduced GDP growth and associated tax base erosion.[25][49] From a fiscal perspective, tariffs function as an economically inefficient revenue instrument, generating distortions and reducing productive capital stock while raising revenue. The capital stock declines by 0.6 percent in 2030 and nearly 10 percent by 2054 under tariff scenarios, reducing worker productivity and translating into persistently lower wages.[25]

The heterogeneity of tariff impacts across countries reveals strategic dimensions of tariff policy. Canada experiences the most severe impacts from U.S. tariffs coupled with Canadian retaliation, with long-run economy 2.2 percent smaller in real terms.[52] China's economy shrinks 0.6 percent long-run, identical to the U.S. impact.[52] The European Union economy expands 0.1 percentage point in the long run while the U.K. economy expands 0.2 percent, reflecting their ability to substitute for restricted Chinese imports and export opportunities generated by U.S. diversion of purchases toward allied suppliers.[52] These asymmetric impacts suggest that tariff policies function partially as devices for shifting adjustment burdens toward economically dependent trading partners rather than achieving universal gains.

The relationship between tariffs and trade flows appears elasticity-dependent, with substantial variation across product categories and country pairs. At product-level detail, tariffs display elasticities substantially exceeding aggregate estimates, reflecting that when individual products face higher tariffs, firms readily substitute to alternative suppliers or products.[28] However, aggregate tariffs on broadly-based categories such as all imports from particular countries display lower elasticities as substitution opportunities narrow. The Purchasing Managers' Index surveys for global manufacturing indicate that new export orders slipped into contractionary territory in April 2025, following temporary front-loading of imports ahead of tariff implementation in prior months, suggesting that tariff escalation pressures are translating into reduced real economic activity beyond simple price adjustments.[41]

## Financial Dimension of Fragmentation: De-dollarization Efforts and Alternative Payment Systems

Beyond goods trade, financial fragmentation represents a distinct but related dimension of the broader geopolitical reorganization of international economic relationships. The BRICS bloc (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, expanded to include Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, and the UAE in January 2024, and Indonesia in January 2025) has pursued explicit strategies to reduce dependence on U.S. dollar-denominated settlement systems and develop alternative financial infrastructure.[32][35] These efforts reflect multifaceted motivations including desire for greater financial sovereignty, reduced exposure to U.S. sanctions, and promotion of a multipolar financial order alternative to U.S. dollar hegemony.[32]

The development of the BRICS payment system represents a technologically ambitious undertaking aimed at rivaling SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication), the dominant global financial messaging system traditionally denominated in dollars.[32] The proposed BRICS Pay system operates as a decentralized cross-border financial messaging infrastructure enabling transactions between BRICS nations using national currencies rather than dollar intermediation.[32] The system incorporates encryption, distributed consensus nodes, multi-factor authentication, and technical capacity to process up to 20,000 messages per second, suggesting technological sophistication approaching existing systems.[32] However, no fully unified BRICS payment system currently exists, with Russia's SPFS and China's CIPS operating as the primary existing alternatives to SWIFT.[32]

Progress toward de-dollarization has occurred in concrete bilateral arrangements rather than through unified systems. Russia reported that approximately 90 percent of its trade within the BRICS bloc was settled in national currencies by end of 2024, reflecting deliberate government policy and institutional restructuring.[35] China and India have engaged in rupee-yuan trade agreements while Brazil and China eliminated the dollar from bilateral trade through yuan-real settlement agreements.[35] India completed its first crude oil purchase from the UAE in rupees in 2023, establishing precedent for regional commodity transactions in non-dollar currencies.[35] These transactions represent meaningful reductions in dollar dependency despite remaining far from complete de-dollarization, as dollar settlement continues for many commodity prices and international credit markets.[35]

However, aspirations for a unified BRICS currency have substantially receded following recognition of formidable political and technical obstacles. Brazil's President Lula, previously one of the most vocal advocates for a common BRICS currency, quietly dropped the idea from Brazil's 2025 BRICS presidency agenda after initial enthusiasm and prototype display of BRICS banknotes at the 2024 Kazan summit by Russian President Putin.[35] The July 2025 BRICS summit in Rio de Janeiro produced no concrete progress toward a shared currency, with the final declaration containing no mention of a common currency or coordinated de-dollarization strategy.[35] These retreats reflect recognition of fundamental obstacles including the absence of unified monetary policy among non-Western countries, lack of shared capital markets, absence of a single central bank capable of underwriting a collective financial system comparable to Federal Reserve backing of dollar-denominated assets, and limited convertibility of key BRICS currencies.[32][35]

The structural advantages of dollar dominance remain formidable despite de-dollarization efforts. The dollar functions not only as the world's most traded currency for commerce but is embedded in institutional stability and deep, liquid capital markets that BRICS countries currently lack.[32] The Chinese yuan captures approximately 7 percent of global foreign exchange transactions according to the Bank for International Settlements 2022 triennial survey, but its share of actual international payments through SWIFT has fluctuated between 2 and 4 percent over the past year, with sharp declines from peaks above 4 percent in late 2024 to around 3 percent by mid-2025.[35] This disconnect between foreign exchange transaction volumes and actual payment usage suggests that while currencies may be exchanged frequently, settlement infrastructure remains concentrated in dollar networks.[35]

The realistic trajectory for financial fragmentation appears to involve incremental development of parallel infrastructure within regional blocs rather than wholesale replacement of dollar-denominated systems. BRICS Pay pilots are projected through 2027, with potential deployment of a settlement unit for intra-BRICS trade by 2028-2030.[35] The New Development Bank has expanded lending in local currencies, establishing institutional infrastructure for non-dollar settlement.[32] These practical, regional alternatives reduce friction costs and dollar exposure within the bloc without aspiring to global reserve currency status.[35] For international trade more broadly, especially the $7.5 trillion daily foreign exchange market dwarfing annual goods trade, the dollar's position remains essentially unchallenged.[35] Global banks, corporations, and investors demonstrate limited appetite for shifting to less liquid and stable alternatives, suggesting that financial fragmentation will remain regionally bounded rather than achieving complete bifurcation.[35]

## Supply Chain Resilience and Vulnerability: Network Perspectives and Measurement Challenges

The conceptualization and measurement of supply chain resilience has evolved substantially during 2022-2025, reflecting recognition that contemporary supply networks constitute complex systems requiring sophisticated analytical approaches beyond traditional supply chain management frameworks. Supply chain disruptions, including those related to COVID-19, natural disasters, and geopolitical tensions, have prompted policymakers to identify potential vulnerabilities and develop resilience enhancement strategies.[6][15] Trade dependencies are increasingly understood as commercial links combining three characteristics: high risk of disruption, high economic importance, and constrained substitution possibilities.[6] The distinction between advantageous trade linkages and concerning critical dependencies remains contested, as policy responses attempting to minimize trade risks through supply chain restructuring may unnecessarily undermine efficiency gains from international trade.[6]

Network-theoretic approaches to supply chain resilience emphasize that supply networks constitute large-scale complex systems with heterogeneous nodes, tangled interconnections, uncertain structures, and dynamic interactions that exceed the analytical capacity of traditional dyadic supplier-buyer models.[15] Individual firms cannot autonomously manage many supply chain risks, necessitating planning, coordination, and investment at broader system levels including industrial sectors and national economies.[15] The identification of vulnerable network nodes and links represents a crucial but underdeveloped analytical capability, as network topology substantially influences how disruptions propagate through supply systems.[15] Networks with power-law characteristics (where 20 percent of hubs connect to 80 percent of other nodes) exhibit higher static resilience, yet these same hub structures render systems more susceptible to contagion when risks propagate through central nodes.[15]

The measurement of supply chain resilience itself faces fundamental challenges in balancing multiple and sometimes contradictory objectives. Network resilience measures must incorporate both network structure and inventory distribution strategies, yet existing quantitative measures often employ oversimplified assumptions about resilience. Birge and colleagues developed a measure incorporating inventory levels as firms seek to minimize out-of-stock risks through precautionary buffering, demonstrating that higher inventory reduces firm vulnerability but creates system-level costs through capital immobilization and storage expenses.[15] The aggregation of inventory costs and switching demand costs across supply networks provides one measure of resilience, yet this approach may not accurately capture dynamic resilience under extreme disruption magnitudes or account for the firm-level decision-making driving inventory holding.[15]

Climate-related risks increasingly feature in supply chain vulnerability assessments, though integration of climate and supply chain risk remains incomplete in corporate practice. Extreme weather events including floods, droughts, and disruptions to key shipping routes create cascading impacts across industries through supply chain disruptions and logistics costs.[57] The current supply chain resilience frameworks emphasize visibility, flexibility, contingency, and collaboration but often overlook climate-specific risks and decision-useful metrics connecting climate science with operational planning.[57] Barriers to integration include limited supplier transparency, poor data quality, and mismatched time horizons between short-term operational supply chain planning and longer-term climate risk assessments.[57] Companies require decision-useful metrics and tools that connect climate science with supply chain operations to anticipate and withstand disruptions from weather extremes increasingly likely under climate change scenarios.

The integration of environmental, social, and governance (ESG) considerations into supply chain management adds additional complexity to resilience frameworks. Supply chain disruptions are identified as the single biggest threat to company revenue streams according to surveys of global companies, yet many firms lack systematic approaches to identifying and prioritizing ESG risks across supply networks.[44] Human rights issues including labor rights and protection of vulnerable workers, environmental diligence covering waste management and emissions, and good governance practices preventing corruption constitute critical domains for supply chain risk management.[44] The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive establishes regulatory requirements for end-to-end supply chain diligence, compelling firms to develop sophisticated verification and monitoring systems.[44] Third-party risk management and business continuity management capabilities can be leveraged to address overlapping ESG and traditional supply chain risks, though this integration remains underdeveloped across most industries.[44]

## Innovation Networks and Global Value Chain Restructuring: Firm-Level Microeconomics

The firm-level organization of global sourcing decisions reveals that decisions to import from multiple countries operate as complementary rather than substitute choices, with profound implications for understanding supply chain fragmentation responses. Ahn and colleagues developed and empirically validated a multicountry model of heterogeneous firm global sourcing demonstrating that whether sourcing from one country raises or reduces the marginal return to sourcing from additional countries depends on demand elasticity and input productivity dispersion.[22] Under relatively elastic demand conditions with sufficiently dispersed productivity differences across input suppliers, sourcing decisions exhibit strong complementarities, such that more productive firms systematically source from more countries and grow disproportionately larger.[22]

The complementarity hypothesis receives strong empirical validation using variation in sourcing costs from China's 2001 World Trade Organization accession. U.S. firms that increased imports from China in response to this shock simultaneously increased purchases from other countries, including the United States, contrary to assumptions embedded in policy debates presuming input country substitutability.[22] This finding stands in direct contradiction to popular reasoning suggesting that tariff increases on one supplier will induce firm purchases to shift toward alternative suppliers; instead, empirical evidence indicates that reduced access to efficient suppliers actually reduces overall firm importing as fixed costs of sourcing become prohibitive for marginal suppliers.[22]

The concentration of firm size and trade participation among multinational enterprises constitutes a distinctive feature of modern global commerce with consequential implications for fragmentation responses. Multinational enterprises operating in the United States in 2007, comprising merely 1.5 percent of U.S. manufacturers, accounted for 57 percent of manufacturing employment, 76 percent of manufacturing sales, 87 percent of manufacturer imports, and 85 percent of exports.[22] This extraordinary concentration of trade participation among a small number of large firms means that disruptions to multinational investment and sourcing strategies have massive economy-wide consequences, as aggregate trade flows depend sensitively on a few thousand firms' location and organization decisions.[22]

U.S. firms' sourcing decisions exhibit systematic relationships between firm productivity and the number of supplier countries, with larger firms sourcing from substantially more countries than smaller competitors. Firms sourcing from a single country in 2007 were on average twice the size of non-importers, while firms importing from 13 countries were four log points larger, and those sourcing from 25 or more countries were over six log points larger.[22] This size-sourcing gradient reflects the existence of substantial fixed costs of importing, which limit importing profitability for smaller firms and consequently generate productivity-based sorting wherein more productive firms overcome fixed costs to access global suppliers.[22] Critically, U.S. firms typically sourced each 10-digit product from just one country in 2007, reflecting the sizable fixed costs paid at the firm-country level and indicating that observed supply chain concentration results not from firm inattention but from economic optimization under cost structures that discourage diversification.[22]

The complementarities in sourcing decisions imply that policy disruptions reducing access to current suppliers impose larger costs than simplified substitution models suggest. If tariffs or export controls make sourcing from previously accessed suppliers prohibitively expensive, firms may respond not simply by redirecting purchases toward alternative suppliers but by contracting overall import activity as the marginal return to maintaining sourcing relationships with remaining suppliers declines.[22] This adjustment mechanism represents a substantial efficiency loss distinct from simple trade diversion, as firms experience reduced access to specialized inputs that enhance productivity precisely because such inputs were economically optimal before the policy shock. The adjustment costs of fragmentation therefore exceed the direct tariff or trade costs imposed by policy changes, representing a form of economic waste arising from policy-induced suboptimality in firm production decisions.

## Emerging Trade Corridors and Regional Reorientation: Evolving Pattern of Bilateral Flows

The structure of global trade flows has undergone systematic reorientation during 2022-2025, with emerging patterns suggesting that trade fragmentation manifests not uniformly but rather through selective bifurcation of previously integrated trade networks. Analysis of trade corridor dynamics reveals substantial heterogeneity in which bilateral relationships are growing rapidly versus contracting under fragmentation scenarios.[37] Of the fifty largest trade corridors globally, analysis of future trajectories under baseline and fragmentation scenarios identifies distinct categories exhibiting differential resilience to policy shocks and geopolitical tensions.[37]

The safest bets for continued growth across all scenarios include corridors connecting China to emerging economies such as ASEAN and India, intraregional trade within emerging markets, and trade between advanced economies including Europe and the United States.[37] Corridors linking China and ASEAN demonstrate accelerating growth in fragmentation scenarios, as China shifts exports away from advanced economies with which it maintains geopolitical distance and seeks new export markets among developing partners with complementary economies.[37] The economic integration of ASEAN with China has deepened to the point where ASEAN has become China's largest trading partner, and Chinese value chains increasingly embed ASEAN economies in sectors such as electronics and textiles.[37] The Middle East represents another growth corridor for China, as the region's large net imports of manufactured goods align with Chinese export capabilities, despite potential competitive pressures on local producers.[37]

Intraregional trade corridors within emerging economies demonstrate robust growth across all scenarios, reflecting independent regional development dynamics that insulate them from geopolitical shocks between advanced economies and China.[37] Southeast Asian integration through initiatives including the ASEAN Economic Community and RCEP have laid groundwork for sustained intraregional trade growth. Similarly, Latin American integration through MERCOSUR and Middle Eastern supply chain cooperation in energy, construction materials, and services suggest that emerging regions can increase trade among themselves independent of advanced economy participation.[37]

Conversely, the most vulnerable trade corridors are those linking China and Russia with advanced economies, which would collapse sharply in extreme fragmentation scenarios from representing 15 percent of global trade currently to just 2 percent by 2035 under explicit geopolitical separation models.[37] However, critical goods and services trade might be rerouted through intermediate economies, particularly in Southeast Asia and India, which could continue assembling and shipping final goods to advanced economies even as direct China-advanced economy trade contracts.[37] This rerouting mechanism preserves some trade value through indirect channels, though at higher transaction costs and longer value chains than direct integration would entail.[37]

The fastest-growing advanced economy corridors in fragmentation scenarios would involve Europe and the United States, as European manufacturing capacity could substitute for restricted Chinese imports across machinery, autos, and clean-energy production.[37] These corridors would strengthen intra-European production and consumption networks, capitalizing on Europe's highest intraregional trade share of any global region.[37] The assumption of 20 percent tariffs between geopolitically distant economies but no additional tariffs for trade through connecting economies creates incentives for rerouting through intermediate suppliers, a mechanism limiting the complete bifurcation of global trade despite severe geopolitical tensions.[37]

## Comparative Regional Dynamics: Heterogeneous Exposure to Fragmentation

The impact of geoeconomic fragmentation manifests heterogeneously across world regions, reflecting differences in trade structure, geopolitical positioning, and economic size. The Caucasus and Central Asia region experienced notable increases in trade activity since 2022 following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, reflecting heightened transit trade and trade diversion as traditional trade routes were disrupted.[40] The Middle Corridor (the overland east-west route through Central Asia) transported 2.7 million tons in 2023, up from 600,000 tons in 2021, representing increased reliance on alternatives to sea transportation disrupted by Red Sea tensions and political barriers to Eurasian Land Bridge through Russia.[40]

Middle Eastern and North African (MENA) countries face complex trade dynamics under fragmentation scenarios. If fragmentation remains limited to targeted restrictions between specific partners (such as EU-Russia trade restrictions), MENA countries could experience expanded trade opportunities through trade diversion as displaced commerce seeks alternative routes and suppliers.[40] Trade diversion and price effects in commodity markets would generate positive but modest economic impacts, particularly benefiting commodity exporters in the Gulf Cooperation Council.[40] By contrast, if fragmentation evolves toward complete bifurcation of the world into geopolitically aligned blocs with trade halted between blocs, MENA countries whose UN voting patterns suggest misalignment with bloc divisions could experience substantial export losses, with estimated declines of 7.4 percent for non-GCC MENA countries and Pakistan depending on bloc alignment assumptions.[40]

South Asia, particularly India, occupies an ambiguous geopolitical position offering both opportunities and vulnerabilities in fragmentation scenarios. India has attempted to maintain strategic autonomy while simultaneously deepening integration into regional frameworks such as RCEP and bilateral partnerships with China and other Asian economies.[35][41] India's economic growth trajectory and underdeveloped trade relationships with Europe create potentially large expansion opportunities as European firms seek diversification from China.[37] The India-Europe corridor could achieve some of the fastest trade growth of any major corridor, reflecting both rapid Indian economic expansion and currently limited trade penetration.[37] However, India's hesitation regarding full participation in IPEF's trade pillar and its concerns about data localization requirements reflect anxieties about policy alignment and technology transfer that fragment India's policy stance relative to clear bloc membership.

Latin America faces particular vulnerabilities under fragmentation scenarios given its dependence on commodity exports to China and trade relationships with the United States. Brazil's position as a major exporter of agricultural products and minerals to China creates exposure to any China-related demand shocks or trade restrictions.[37] Mexico has benefited from nearshoring dynamics and USMCA membership, which exempts it from tariffs applicable to other major U.S. trading partners, partially insulating Mexican-U.S. trade from broader tariff escalation.[52] Argentina and other South American nations face choices regarding membership in competing trade blocs, with Uruguay's application for CPTPP membership creating friction with other MERCOSUR members who maintain common external tariff requirements.[12]

European Union exposure to fragmentation reflects simultaneously its deep internal integration and dependence on imports from China and other sources. European strategic autonomy, conceptualized as the ability to defend its own interests without relying on other forces, requires reducing dependencies on China for critical raw materials, managing technological competition with both the United States and China, and addressing financial vulnerabilities from U.S.-centric financial systems.[36] China provides 100 percent of the EU's supply of heavy rare earth elements essential for permanent magnets used in wind turbines and electric vehicles, creating critical supply chain vulnerability.[36] The EU's manufacturing capacity for semiconductors represented only 10 percent of global sales, with ambitions to increase to 20 percent by 2030 through coordinated industrial policy, yet auditors and analysts question whether current investment levels suffice for meeting this target.[36]

## Trends and Challenges: Emerging Research Directions and Unresolved Questions

The empirical research landscape emerging from 2022-2025 reveals several trajectories and unresolved challenges likely to dominate research agendas through the remainder of the 2020s. First, the dynamic interplay between corporate investment decisions, supply chain restructuring, and macroeconomic outcomes remains incompletely understood. Existing research demonstrates both that geopolitical distance increasingly influences foreign direct investment patterns and that supply chain restructuring is occurring in response to policy uncertainty, yet the mechanisms linking these firm-level decisions to economy-wide productivity growth, employment transitions, and wage distribution remain contested. The apparent complementarities in firm sourcing decisions suggest that fragmentation-induced reductions in sourcing from particular countries may contract overall trade rather than simply redirecting toward alternative suppliers, creating efficiency losses exceeding the direct trade costs imposed by tariffs or export controls. Quantifying these second-order effects requires integrating firm-level microdata with general equilibrium frameworks capable of capturing supply chain disruption propagation.

Second, the persistence of innovation impacts from technological export controls versus eventual accommodation through alternative innovation networks constitutes a critical uncertainty. Research on Entity List restrictions demonstrates that innovation declines among sanctioned Chinese firms and spillover effects across technology fields operate in the medium term (2-3 years post-sanction), yet questions remain about long-run trajectories. Will Chinese firms and government industrial policies successfully develop indigenous technological alternatives, potentially reducing foreign dependency and improving innovation autonomy? Or will the innovation declines become self-reinforcing as human capital flight and reduced foreign collaboration accumulate over time? The answer matters profoundly for the future competitiveness of Chinese firms and the global distribution of innovation capabilities, yet existing research provides limited long-run evidence given the recency of comprehensive export controls.

Third, the sustainability of financial fragmentation efforts and the potential for partial re-integration remain significant uncertainties. BRICS de-dollarization aspirations have already substantially receded from the ambitious unified currency proposals of 2024 toward more modest bilateral currency arrangements and regional payment systems. Will these incremental institutional arrangements prove sufficient for BRICS countries' economic cooperation needs, or will technical limitations and political coordination challenges prevent further development? Conversely, could some combination of technological advancement, institutional development, and geopolitical accommodation enable partial re-integration of financial systems even if full dollarization recedes? The trajectory of monetary architecture will substantially influence capital flows, exchange rate dynamics, and cross-border investment patterns for the next decade.

Fourth, the resilience of multilateral institutions and the potential for institutional reform or parallel institutional creation represents an unresolved structural question. The World Trade Organization's dispute settlement mechanism remains paralyzed, preventing resolution of conflicts arising from competing regional agreements and limiting multilateral coordination. Will WTO reform prove feasible, or will the organization become progressively marginalized as countries pursue regional and bilateral arrangements? Alternatively, could BRICS-led institutions including the New Development Bank or proposed BRICS payment systems eventually achieve sufficient scale and sophistication to provide viable alternatives to multilateral institutions for developing country participation? The institutional architecture of the global economy during the coming decade will substantially constrain or enable the sustainability of cooperation amid geopolitical tensions.

Fifth, distributional consequences of fragmentation warrant substantially increased research attention. Current macroeconomic analyses reveal aggregate costs of fragmentation, yet heterogeneous impacts across sectors, regions, and income groups suggest that fragmentation may generate substantial redistributions of income even if aggregate effects remain modest. Lower-income households in advanced economies appear disproportionately burdened by tariffs through consumption of cheaper imported goods, while workers in import-competing sectors face employment and wage pressures despite potential job creation in nearshoring sectors. Emerging market workers may benefit from nearshoring-driven employment growth while simultaneously facing vulnerability to reduced export opportunities if fragmentation contracts trade volumes. Understanding how fragmentation distributes welfare effects across populations requires more granular empirical analysis than existing research has produced.

Sixth, the measurement and prediction of supply chain disruption propagation through complex networks remains an underdeveloped analytical capability with significant policy relevance. Current frameworks often employ simplified assumptions about network structure and disruption propagation, yet real supply networks exhibit power-law characteristics, redundancy, and dynamic rerouting capabilities that sophisticated models must capture. Integration of climate risk, geopolitical risk, and operational disruptions into unified resilience frameworks remains largely aspirational in research literature. Developing decision-useful metrics and tools enabling firms and governments to quantify critical dependencies and prioritize resilience investments constitutes a substantial research frontier.

Finally, the integration of political economy perspectives examining how domestic political coalitions drive fragmentation versus integration remains insufficiently developed. Most economic research treats trade policy as exogenously imposed shocks rather than endogenous outcomes of political competition and interest group influence. Understanding why fragmentation persists despite its apparent economic costs requires engaging with political economy questions about which constituencies benefit from fragmentation, how political institutions aggregate preferences for protectionism versus openness, and whether institutional reforms might enable greater political support for integration. The disconnect between economic findings of fragmentation costs and continued political appetite for protectionist measures suggests that political economy factors substantially exceed traditional trade economic considerations in determining policy outcomes.

## Conclusion: Integration Amid Fragmentation

The period 2022-2025 witnessed paradoxical simultaneous processes of deepened economic fragmentation driven by geopolitical competition, technological decoupling, and strategic supply chain reconfiguration, coexisting with persistent global economic integration through continuing trade growth, formation of competing regional blocs, and incremental innovation in financial infrastructure. The quantitative research evidence demonstrates that geoeconomic fragmentation imposes substantial macroeconomic costs through reduced international diversification benefits, higher inflation and lower growth from trade restrictions, inefficiencies in firm-level sourcing decisions, and employment disruptions concentrated in trade-exposed sectors. Yet global trade volumes have demonstrated surprising resilience, with 2025 showing accelerated expansion at 4.7 percent average monthly growth, suggesting that economic integration remains economically compelling despite political fragmentation.

The resolution of contradictions between integration and fragmentation trajectories will likely involve complex adaptive processes rather than uniform bifurcation into isolated economic blocs. Regional trade blocs including RCEP, CPTPP, AfCFTA, and IPEF simultaneously deepen integration among member states while creating boundaries restricting trade with non-members, suggesting that the future architecture will involve hierarchical regional networks with selective inter-regional connections. China-led initiatives in RCEP and Belt and Road infrastructure provide integration pathways for emerging economies while Western-led frameworks including IPEF and bilateral arrangements reflect distinct approaches to managing China relationships. The persistence of trade through rerouting via intermediate countries even under fragmentation scenarios suggests that complete economic decoupling remains implausible, as economic gains from specialization and exchange remain substantial even under geopolitically constrained commerce.

The emerging policy priorities for addressing fragmentation's costs should emphasize supply chain resilience-building that maintains beneficial international specialization while reducing critical dependencies. Rather than attempting to eliminate imports through autarky or extremely restricted partnerships, diversifying supplier bases across friendly countries while enhancing visibility into supply chain networks offers more economically efficient approaches than broad protectionism. Supporting workforce transitions in import-competing sectors through education, retraining, and adjustment assistance addresses distributional concerns without requiring economically costly trade restrictions. Maintaining multilateral institutions' functional capacity even amid geopolitical tensions creates necessary infrastructure for resolving disputes and preserving baseline-level international cooperation.

The fundamental trajectory of the global economy during the 2026-2030 period will likely involve what might be termed "integration amid fragmentation" or "modular globalization," wherein trade and investment flows intensify within geopolitically aligned regional networks while declining sharply across geopolitical divides. This pattern would represent neither return to pre-2018 integrated globalization nor complete fragmentation into autarkic blocs, but rather a reorganization into competing networks with asymmetric integration depths. The economic costs of this reorganization will be substantial, particularly for countries occupying intermediate geopolitical positions lacking clear great power alignment, yet the persistence of efficiency gains from trade will prevent complete bifurcation. Understanding and adapting to these dynamics constitutes the central economic policy challenge of the coming decade.
